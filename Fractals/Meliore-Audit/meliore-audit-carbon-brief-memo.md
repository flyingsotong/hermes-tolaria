**Interviewee:** Leo Hickman
**URL:** https://www.carbonbrief.org/

**Date:** 21 July 2026

## Executive summary

Carbon Brief is the closest organization in this audit to the "ready" archetype: genuine hunger, a real and defensible offer, clean permission, strong culture, proven cadence. It fails the full ready designation on one dimension only — Clock — and the clock risk is structural, not temporary.

Project Cosmos, launched last month after 18 months of development, is the world's largest database of climate change research — a proprietary database of climate science metadata containing 1.8 million unique publications linked by 40 million citation relationships. It is real, it generated measurable buzz (over a dozen universities press-released on their Cosmos 500 rankings), and it is the most commercially promising asset in this audit cohort. Leo is exploring a membership model around it — options discussed included a private WhatsApp or Signal community, browsing access to the Cosmos database, and API-level access for running custom queries. Target launch is COP in Turkey this November.

Leo is a rare talent who sees the full architecture. He embraces commercial options without the legacy hang-ups that paralyze most nonprofit media leaders. He ran a pricing experiment (COP Insider Pass, $10 for two weeks), collected rich user journey data, and is now seconding a senior team member — his visuals editor, [Tom Prater](https://www.linkedin.com/in/tom-prater/) — for 2–3 months to co-develop the membership offer. He cites Climate Home News's hard paywall as a cautionary tale — it generated only $40,000 — and notes Carbon Brief itself operates on roughly £2 million a year with 25 people.

> **The commercial model is directionally right — genuine assets, sound design principles, a leader who sees the architecture — but execution capacity and clock risk make it fragile.**

Leo is the sole fundraiser, the sole commercial thinker, and the editorial director. He is pulling from the same editorial stock for commercial work — his seconded visuals editor is not a product person. The commercial thinking, however sophisticated, stays contained within the journalistic identity. The European Climate Foundation, a major funder, cut 30% last year and has declined to commit to this year's number — "it won't be more than what you currently get" is a funder telling you to prepare for another cut.

## Dimension breakdown

### Hunger (4/5)

Leo left the Guardian and WWF-UK to build Carbon Brief from a niche publication into climate's reference standard. He's been at it for 13+ years and carries none of the performative hunger for funders that shows up elsewhere in this cohort. He embraces commercial options with a refreshing willingness to experiment — FT Strategies influence is visible but the hunger appears to be his own, not borrowed.

**Evidence:**
- Ran a pricing experiment (COP Insider Pass, $10 for two weeks) and deliberately designed it to generate user journey data — genuine commercial learning, not just talking
- Seconding Tom Prater for 2–3 months to co-develop the membership offer — tangible resource commitment against a hard deadline
- Thinking about membership options (WhatsApp/Signal community, database browsing, API access)
- Self-aware about uncertainty: "I'm still struggling to think of the example of a person that would go, yeah, I'm happy to pay $1,000 a month" — not performative confidence, genuine product thinking
- Frustrated by the fundraising treadmill — "I am basically the fundraiser" — recognizes the model is unsustainable

The appetite is real. The constraint is not willingness — it's bandwidth and frame.

### Offer (4/5) — Strongest in cohort

Project Cosmos is the most commercially promising asset in this audit. It moves Carbon Brief beyond "climate journalism with a database project" into something closer to climate verification infrastructure.

**Project Cosmos** — 18 months of development, launched June 2026. Contains metadata (abstracts, titles, authors, topics, citation networks) for 1.8 million unique climate science publications linked by 40 million citation relationships, built via the open-source tool OpenAlex. Carbon Brief describes it as the world's largest database of climate change research. Proprietary and intentionally closed source to prevent AI scraping. The launch included the Cosmos 500 rankings of the world's most-cited climate scientists, institutions, and studies — a deliberate marketing tactic that generated press coverage (AFP) and press releases from over a dozen universities. Built by a contracted PhD student in France (IP owned by Carbon Brief) with informal collaboration from the University of Exeter (no contract yet).

**Key constraint:** The database contains metadata only, not full studies. Roughly 20% of studies are open access; the remaining 80% sit behind paywalls at Nature, Science, and similar journals. The abstracts alone are considered a significant and potentially trainable resource.

**Membership model** — Options discussed during the call:
- Private WhatsApp or Signal community
- Browsing access to the Cosmos database
- API access for running custom queries

The design philosophy is deliberate and unusual: **access, networking, and utility — not paywalled content.** Leo has internalized the Climate Home News lesson (its hard paywall generated only $40,000) and is deliberately designing a membership offer that sits alongside free editorial content, not cannibalizing it.

**Commercial use cases** (all hypotheses, none confirmed at a price):
- VC investors scanning for emerging university-level climate tech — identifying leading academics and institutions in niche areas like solar panel technology
- Insurers and investors needing investment-grade climate risk reports (e.g., climate change risks to coffee farming in Ghana over a 40-year investment cycle)
- Training a climate-focused LLM using the Cosmos metadata
- Bespoke literature reviews and research briefings for institutional clients

**The gap:** No confirmed buyer at any tier. Leo himself is uncertain about the commercial value: "I can't imagine someone paying enough that it would be significant to us." The COP Insider Pass trial validated willingness to pay at the low end ($10 for two weeks) but not at the institutional level. The membership model has good architecture but the offer is unproven.

The offer is strong enough to bet on — with one paying institutional client before COP as the validation threshold.

### Permission (4/5)

The structural permissions are largely in place, with one unresolved risk.

**Clean IP on Cosmos:** The PhD student who built the database worked under a contract that assigns all IP to Carbon Brief. This was deliberate and correctly executed. The University of Exeter contributed to the Cosmos visualization and network graph — but the relationship is entirely informal. No contract, no IP assignment, no commercial terms. Leo sits on Exeter's external stakeholder board (a quasi-formal relationship), and to date Exeter has expressed no commercial interest in Cosmos. But "no stated interest" and "no legal claim" are not the same thing at the point a commercial agreement is signed. This is a precondition, not a nice-to-have.

**Membership frame avoids the editorial-commercial clash:** Leo has designed the membership offer around access, networking, and utility — not paywalled content. Editorial remains free. Membership sits alongside it. This is the right architecture for a mission-driven organization transitioning to commercial revenue, and it avoids the firewall tensions that typically paralyze nonprofit media.

**Organizational thinking is evolving:** Leo has considered a separate "sister entity" for commercial work — like the Economist Intelligence Unit model which he raised himself during the call. The open-source analogy (open platform, paid services on top) was also discussed. The legal structure is not yet formalized, but the conceptual framework exists.

Legal structure unknown — UK CIC, charity, or company limited by guarantee? This needs to be confirmed before any commercial agreement is signed.

### Culture (3/5) — The binding constraint

This is the dimension that most limits Carbon Brief's commercial potential — and the one where the contrast with the Gazette is most instructive.

**The team is editorial, not commercial.** The 25-person team is experienced, award-winning, and has deep domain knowledge. Leo described Carbon Brief as operating on roughly £2 million a year. But everyone is a journalist, science communicator, or editor. There is no commercial person, no product person, and no fundraiser. Leo is the sole commercial thinker, the sole fundraiser, *and* the editorial director.

**Pulling from the same editorial stock.** [Tom Prater](https://www.linkedin.com/in/tom-prater/), Carbon Brief's visuals editor seconded for the membership build, is a trusted seven-year veteran and the right internal choice — but he is not a product manager or a revenue operator. When Leo needs a commercial mind, he reaches for his visuals editor. The secondment is 2–3 months of temporary capacity — not a permanent hire, not a structural shift.

**The deeper pattern:** Leo is designing the membership offer through an editorial lens — access, insight, community — rather than a commercial one — unit economics, customer acquisition cost, margin per tier. The commercial thinking, however sophisticated, stays contained within the journalistic identity. This limits where the business can go.

**The Gazette contrast:** Anna Saulwick hired Frank as a dedicated commercial director — someone from outside the editorial fold with product and revenue expertise. Anna is willing to go beyond traditional definitions of what a newsroom is. Leo's culture keeps the commercial work inside the editorial frame. That's the difference between a Culture 4 and a Culture 3. [See the Gazette report →](gazette.html)

**What would change it:** A commercial operator hired or seconded into the membership build. Even a 3-month external product consultant would materially reduce the execution risk on the November launch.

### Cadence (4/5)

Carbon Brief ships. The Daily Briefing publishes every morning. Articles and factchecks land on schedule. And they shipped a major product — Cosmos — from concept to launch in 18 months, with real press coverage and institutional buzz. That is not typical for a journalism nonprofit.

**Evidence:**
- Cosmos: 18 months from commission to public launch, with marketing strategy baked in (Cosmos 500 rankings, universe visualization)
- COP Insider Pass: designed, launched, executed, and user journey data collected — experimental shipping at speed
- Membership model: Tom's secondment has a hard deadline (COP in November 2026), clear deliverables, and a deliberate public launch window
- Website redesign: 11-year gap between updates, but launched today — slow infrastructure investment but they got it done

**The constraint:** Fundraising consumes Leo's time — chasing warm leads, waiting for board decisions, managing existing funder relationships. "The amount of time I spend doing that is enormous." This is the drag on everything else, and there is no one to absorb it.

### Clock (2/5) — The structural risk

This is the dimension that prevents Carbon Brief from being a full "ready" designation — and it is not a temporary problem.

**ECF uncertainty:** The European Climate Foundation, a major funder, cut 30% last year. At their annual meeting this year, they told Leo: "We don't know yet. It won't be more than what you currently get." That is a funder telling you to prepare for another cut. Leo described it as a "massive question mark" over an "important chunk" of funding. This is the single largest risk in the Carbon Brief picture.

**Oceans grant tension:** A new oceans-focused funder approached Carbon Brief cold and granted 2.5 years of funding — the first multi-year commitment CB has ever received. But the funding is designated for a specific team (editor + two reporters focused solely on oceans coverage). Leo doesn't know whether core funding is secure enough to actually build this team. The grant could end up as supplementary funding for existing staff, which creates a tension between donor intent and organizational reality.

**Fundraising is a solo operation:** Leo is the only fundraiser. He described the process as "brutal" — months of chasing warm leads, waiting for board decisions, and maintaining existing funder relationships. The Wellcome Trust was a warm lead for a human health and climate change editorial pillar — and has gone silent. This is not unusual. The pipeline is unreliable by design.

**No shutdown contingency:** Leo articulated no plan for what happens if ECF pulls out entirely. The commercial transition is not optional — it is survival. But the organization has no structural redundancy at the top. If Leo's bandwidth collapses, everything collapses.

## Recommendation signal

**Status:** Ready, with one structural constraint (🟡)

The infrastructure asset (Cosmos) is real, the membership model has sound design principles, the commercial learning culture is genuine, and Leo is the most commercially unconflicted leader you will meet in this space. He is a rare talent who understands where all of this can go.

The constraint that prevents a "ready" designation — Clock — is not fatal. The ECF uncertainty is real and pressing, but it is also the thing that makes the commercial transition urgent rather than aspirational. If the funding were stable, Leo would have less reason to ship the membership product by November. The clock is the forcing function.

**The recommendation: Meliore does not fund Carbon Brief. Meliore becomes Carbon Brief's first commercial customer.**

The vehicle: a 12-month data access and briefing contract. API access to Cosmos plus a set number of bespoke research queries per quarter — structured inquiries on landscape mapping, funder diligence, or emerging climate research that Meliore already needs for its own work. Priced institutionally, with renewal tied to delivery, not milestones.

The logic: a grant extends a runway that is structurally unstable. A commercial contract creates the feedback loop Carbon Brief actually needs — experience servicing a paying client, a reference case for every subsequent conversation, and the organizational muscle of pricing, delivering, and renewing commercial work. Meliore gets intelligence it already needs. Carbon Brief gets the capability it needs to develop. This is the highest-leverage intervention available to Meliore: it funds the capability Carbon Brief hasn't built yet, not just the operation it already runs.

**Core tension to resolve:** Carbon Brief's open-access mission and its most valuable commercial asset are pointing in opposite directions. Cosmos is worth most as a closed, proprietary, queryable database. The mission is worth most as open, free, and universally accessible. Which version of Carbon Brief is Meliore actually backing? The answer changes what the contract looks like, what the renewal logic is, and whether the Cosmos asset compounds or gets scraped the moment it opens.

## Revenue thesis and 90-day first move

**Thesis:** The Cosmos API is the first product worth pricing at institutional scale. The buyer class is clear — climate-focused insurers, investors, and foundations who need investment-grade literature reviews at speed, without Google Scholar's noise or a research firm's cost and lag. Carbon Brief's brand is the trust layer that makes the output credible. The bespoke report model (climate risk for coffee farming in Ghana, emerging solar cell research for a VC) is the right product shape: structured query → Carbon Brief-editorialized output → delivered fast.

**Meliore-as-first-customer move:** A 12-month contract for API access plus a set number of bespoke research queries per quarter. Meliore gains infrastructure it already needs for landscape mapping and funder diligence. Carbon Brief gains its first commercial contract and the organizational experience of servicing a paying client. Renewal is tied to delivery, not milestones. The contract creates a forced learning loop: pricing → delivery → renewal → reference case.

**90-day first move:**

1. **Resolve the Exeter IP question.** One conversation with a term sheet. This is a precondition — if Carbon Brief cannot clearly own the IP, Cosmos cannot be sold. Weeks, not months.

2. **Pricing test with COP Insider Pass power users.** Leo and Tom take the user journey data from the trial, identify the 20–30 users who engaged most deeply with the Cosmos browsing tier, and call them. Not a survey — a call. "Would you pay US$X/month for this? What would make it worth US$Y?" This is the pricing test Carbon Brief hasn't run yet. Days, not weeks.

3. **Design the Meliore contract.** A simple 12-month pilot agreement — API access plus quarterly briefings, priced institutionally, with clear delivery milestones and a renewal clause. Delivered as a commercial contract, not a grant letter. Before COP.
