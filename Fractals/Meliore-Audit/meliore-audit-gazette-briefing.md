## Six-dimension pre-read

### Hunger
Anna left a senior product role at Change.org to build this from scratch — real skin in the game. But the revenue language on the site ("we are now working on building a sustainable social enterprise") reads as aspiration, not desperation. Philanthropic seed funding may be cushioning urgency.

### Offer
No obvious paid product today. The revenue plan (ads, affiliate, donations, events) all require significant audience density to convert — and three people operating six titles is thin. Latent IP may exist in local audience data, community relationships, or social video production capability.

### Permission
Pty Ltd — structurally clean. But the firewall language suggests nonprofit-minded framing of commercial revenue as something to protect against, not pursue. Brad and Nick come from orgs (VICE, BuzzFeed) that struggled with editorial-commercial tension.

### Culture
Digital-native team (BuzzFeed, VICE, Junkee) — good for speed and audience. But none have built a sustainable media business. They know how to build audiences; unclear if they know how to convert them into revenue.

### Cadence
Six titles with three leaders + "more coming soon" + one job posting. They ship editorially. Unknown whether they ship commercially.

### Clock
Seed-funded, likely mid-runway. Language suggests transitioning from build phase to sustainability phase. Clock probably visible to Anna — question is whether it's visible to Brad and Nick.

## Priority questions

**1. "Why did you leave Change.org to do this?"**
Tests Hunger directly. Listen for whether she ran toward something or away from something.

**2. "What would you sell in the next 90 days, and who has already signaled they'd pay?"**
Tests whether Offer is real or aspirational.

**3. "Who on your team owns revenue thinking?"**
If the answer is "me," that's a Culture and Cadence flag. Three people can't build editorial AND commercial alone.

**4. "What's the last commercial thing you tried that didn't work?"**
Tests for shipping bias and whether failure is safe to discuss. If she can't name one, she's either not trying or not being honest.

**5. "How much runway do you have, and does Brad know?"**
Clock visibility. Vague or deflected answer = urgency may be performative.

## Coaching note

Anna is a product person building a media company — different profile than most of your other orgs. She thinks in systems and infrastructure, not content and grants. Meet her where she is: "What's the MVP?" will land better than "What's your editorial mission?"

The team's BuzzFeed/VICE background is a double signal: they understand speed and social distribution, but they're also scarred by organizations that failed commercially. Watch for how she frames those experiences — as lessons learned or as reasons to be cautious.
