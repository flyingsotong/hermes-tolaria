## Org at a glance

Carbon Brief is a UK-based climate publication that has evolved from a high-trust journalism outlet into something closer to **climate verification and expertise infrastructure**. It's been operating for ~15 years and is widely cited as an authoritative source on climate science, policy, and energy.

**Content model:** Free to view, no advertising, reader-supported donations. Content available under Creative Commons for reuse. Four flagship newsletters: Daily Briefing, DeBriefed, Cropped (agriculture/nature), China Briefing.

**Key product layers:**
- Reference-grade explainers, Q&As, factchecks, and analysis — formats that compound in value over time
- Newsletter briefing products with high retention
- Data/visuals capability (award-winning interactive features)
- **Global South Climate Database (GSCD):** searchable expert directory for journalists to source diverse voices
- **Emerging:** Large database of climate scientists with University of Exeter — citation-aware mapping going beyond "who's who" into a structured map of climate research

**Revenue model:** Free + donations + CC content reuse. From Leo's prior conversation: commercial-mindedness is present, FT Strategies influence visible. Grant funding can seed early commercial data work as bridge to productization. COP30 pilot tested an exclusive group at ~US$10.

## Your notes from prior Leo call

Carbon Brief is evolving from a high-trust climate publication into **climate verification and expertise infrastructure**.

### What seems to compound

- Reference-grade formats (explainers/Q&As, factchecks, analysis) that stay useful over time
- A product portfolio of briefing-style newsletters (Daily Briefing, DeBriefed, China Briefing, Cropped)
- A database layer moving beyond "directory" into a structured map of climate science and expertise

### The three audit questions to nail down

**1) What is the compounding product — and what is the quality spec behind it?**

Candidate assets (in order of increasing defensibility):
- Maintained explainers/factchecks library
- Newsletter briefing products
- Data/visuals capability
- The emerging research/expertise database (with University of Exeter)

From Leo: building a large database of climate scientists extending beyond "who's who" into citation-aware mapping — a "universe" of researchers and science.

Need to understand: what's the spec that makes it authoritative — ingestion rules, entity resolution, provenance, correction policy, update triggers, versioning, audit trail?

**2) Where is the infrastructure layer — and what verification cost does it remove repeatedly?**

Clearest public artifact today: Global South Climate Database (GSCD), searchable expert directory with structured fields.

From Leo: GSCD updated roughly annually by one person. This suggests a major operational gap — maintenance cadence, QA rigor, intake workflows.

Infrastructure test: name the repeatable jobs it enables for power users:
- Sourcing: find credible experts fast (especially Global South)
- Verification: triangulate claims/evidence fast
- Briefing: build decision-grade context fast

Machine-usable without losing meaning: stable URLs, structured metadata, downloadable/portable formats, clear methods and provenance.

**3) How does "free + no ads" become durable — and what is the renewal logic?**

On-site posture: free to view, no advertising, reader support, content reuse under Creative Commons.

From Leo: commercial-mindedness is present (FT Strategies influence). Grant funding can sensibly seed early commercial data work as bridge to productization.

Evidence of experimentation: COP30 pilot — exclusive group for journalists/academics at ~US$10. Low price, high learning, signals willingness to package and charge.

Renewal logic: what measurable signals justify renewal in an infrastructure frame — newsletter retention, repeat use of explainers/factchecks, citations, partner reuse of charts, GSCD adoption by newsrooms?

Working shorthand: **reference formats + briefing products + databases/knowledge graph** — then pressure-test the spec, maintenance burden, and revenue logic behind each.

## Six-dimension pre-read

### Hunger
Leo left the Guardian and WWF-UK to build Carbon Brief from a niche publication into climate's reference standard. That's a mission-driven founder who's been at it for 13+ years. Commercial-mindedness is recent (FT Strategies, COP30 pilot). The question is whether he's hungry enough to disrupt his own model — or whether the org's identity as "free, trusted, grant-funded" limits how far he'll push.

### Offer
Potentially strong. The compounding formats (explainers, factchecks) have latent value as API-accessible reference data. The Exeter database could be a defensible B2B product if the spec is rigorous enough. The GSCD demonstrates willingness to build infrastructure, but the one-person annual update cadence is a red flag — if it's not resourced properly, the data decays.

### Permission
Carbon Brief operates as a journalism outlet funded by grants and donations. The "no ads, free to view" posture is part of their brand identity. Shifting to a commercial data product would require a frame shift — not "selling out" but "charging for infrastructure services the climate field needs." The firewall on the data side is simpler than on the editorial side. Constitutional/legal structure unknown — check.

### Culture
Team of ~15-20, mostly journalists and science communicators. The editorial team is strong — experienced, award-winning, deep domain knowledge. But there's no commercial or product person visible on the team listing. Leo is the closest to a commercial thinker (FT Strategies exposure), and he's the editor. Who on the team would build and sell a product? If the answer is "Leo delegates editorial and builds product himself," that's a Cadence problem.

### Cadence
Carbon Brief ships every day — the Daily Briefing goes out, articles publish, factchecks land. They have strong editorial cadence. But the GSCD is updated annually by one person. The Exeter database is "emerging" with no publicly visible timeline. Commercial cadence is significantly slower than editorial cadence — the muscle that needs building is in product development, not content production.

### Clock
Carbon Brief is well-established (~15 years) and has broad grant support. The clock is likely longer than most orgs on your list — they're not burning runway in the same way as a seed-stage startup. But the "free + no ads" model is under pressure as the philanthropic landscape tightens. Leo may feel the clock more than he's willing to articulate.

## Priority questions

**1. "What would you sell in the next 90 days, and who has already signaled they'd pay?"**
Tests whether the commercial appetite is real or still at the "we're thinking about it" stage. Listen for specific buyers, not categories.

**2. "Who on your team would build the product?"**
If the answer is "me, in my spare time from running the editorial operation," that's a Culture and Cadence flag. If he names someone specific, probe their background.

**3. "The GSCD is updated annually by one person — what would it take to make it a quarter-cycling product that newsrooms genuinely depend on?"**
This tests whether he sees the database as a real product or a side project. A defensible answer names staffing, tech investment, and a revenue model.

**4. "What did you learn from the COP30 pilot?"**
Concrete evidence of commercial learning. Listen for specific numbers: how many signed up, what was the feedback, would they do it again at a higher price?

**5. "How much of Carbon Brief's value sits in assets that could be productized — and how much of that depends on you personally?"**
Tests the tension between institutional value and founder dependency. Carbon Brief is Leo's reputation in large part — what happens to the database authority if he steps away?

## Coaching note

Leo is a journalist-editor who has been pulled toward infrastructure thinking partly by the market and partly by his own strategic exposure (FT Strategies). He's more commercial-curious than most org leaders on your list, but the identity shift from "publisher" to "infrastructure provider" is the hardest transition in media. He'll need to be convinced that the database work doesn't dilute Carbon Brief's editorial authority — it compounds it. Your job is to help him see that clearly.
