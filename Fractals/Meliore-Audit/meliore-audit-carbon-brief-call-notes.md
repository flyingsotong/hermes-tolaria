# Carbon Brief — Post-call notes

**Call date:** 21 July 2026
**Interviewee:** Leo Hickman, Director and Editor
**Duration:** ~45 min

---

## Executive read

Carbon Brief is the most commercially interesting organization in this cohort — not because they're making money, but because Leo thinks like a product person trapped in a journalist's job. Cosmos is real, launched, and generating buzz. The membership model has good design principles (access/utility, not paywalled content). But Leo is the only commercial thinker, the only fundraiser, and the editorial director. He cannot do all three. The ECF funding uncertainty is a sword hanging over the organization. The Meliore play — first customer for the infrastructure product — makes deep strategic sense here.

## Six-dimension scores

### Hunger
**Score: 4 — Promising**
**Evidence:**

- Seconding Tom Prater for 2-3 months to co-develop membership offer — tangible resource commitment, not just aspiration

**Completeness of commercial thinking:** 4. He's moved from "we should think about this" to "we're allocating a person and a deadline." But hasn't closed anything yet.

### Offer
**Score: 4 — Promising**
**Evidence:**
- **Project Cosmos:** 18 months of development, launched ~1 month ago. Database of climate science metadata (abstracts, titles, authors, topics) via OpenAlex. Proprietary, closed source. Generated major buzz — 12+ universities press-released on Cosmos 500 rankings, AFP covered the most-cited scientist story. Universe-style visualization was a deliberate marketing tactic.
- **Building team:** Commissioned PhD student in France (IP owned by Carbon Brief — smart). University of Exeter collaboration is informal (no contract — IP risk).
- **Metadata constraint:** Contains only metadata, not full studies (~20% open access, 80% behind paywalls). Abstracts alone considered a valuable training resource.
- **Membership model:** Three tiers (Basic WhatsApp community → Mid Cosmos browsing → Premium API access ~$1,000/month). Design philosophy is access, networking, utility — not paywalled content. Climate Home News's failed paywall ($40K on £2M operation) is a live cautionary reference.
- **Commercial use cases** (all hypotheses, none confirmed): VCs scanning for emerging university-level climate tech, insurers/investors needing bespoke climate risk reports (coffee farming in Ghana example), training a climate-focused LLM, literature review generation
- **Learning asset:** COP Insider Pass generated rich Bitly/journey data — analyzed once but Tom wants to go deeper. Leo deliberately designed the trial to generate data.
- **Leo's own signal:** Not yet convinced of commercial viability himself — "I can't imagine someone paying enough that it would be significant to us." That's honest, not discouraging. He's waiting for evidence.

### Permission
**Score: 4 — Promising**
**Evidence:**
- Cosmos IP: clean on the PhD student side (contract states CB owns all work). Exeter side is informal — no contract, "friends" relationship. Leo is on Exeter's external board — quasi-formal but no commercial terms. This is a risk if Cosmos becomes a commercial product.
- Membership design philosophy is deliberately separated from content — "it's not about paywalled content. It's about networking, access, conversations, resources, and utility." This avoids the editorial-commercial firewall tension.
- Leo has thought about structural separation: a "sister entity, a more commercial-minded entity" like Economist Intelligence Unit — shows he's thinking about organizational design, not just product design
- Cosmos is intentionally closed source — controversial with academics but Leo has a "no regrets" policy. Openness may evolve.
- Legal structure unknown — this is a material gap. If they're a UK charity, commercial revenue may have constraints. Need to check.

### Culture
**Score: 3 — Mixed**
**Evidence:**
- Tom Prater — visuals editor, 7-8 years, being seconded for 2-3 months to membership work. That's one person below Leo with emerging commercial DNA. But it's a secondment, not a hire — temporary capacity, not permanent capability.
- No commercial person. No product person. No fundraiser. Leo is the sole commercial thinker, the sole fundraiser, and the editorial director.
- Editorial team is strong (25, award-winning) but optimized entirely for content production. They know journalism — not product development, not sales.
- The team meeting today was editorial prioritization. Leo is announcing Tom's secondment there — the team hasn't been in a commercial conversation yet.
- The PhD student builder was a contractor, not an employee — no institutional capacity retained from the Cosmos build.
- **The deeper pattern:** Leo is pulling from the same editorial stock for commercial work (Tom is a visuals editor, not a product person). He's designing the membership offer through an editorial lens — access, insight, community — rather than a commercial one — unit economics, customer acquisition cost, margin per tier. The commercial thinking stays contained within the journalistic identity. This limits where the business can go.
- **Comparison with Gazette:** Anna hired Frank — a dedicated commercial director from outside the editorial fold. She's willing to go beyond traditional definitions of what a newsroom is. Leo's culture keeps the commercial work inside the editorial frame.

### Cadence
**Score: 4 — Promising**
**Evidence:**
- Ships editorially every day — Daily Briefing, articles, factchecks. Strong production muscle.
- Cosmos: 18 months to build and launch — real product shipping, not just editorial shipping
- COP Insider Pass: designed, launched, executed, data collected — experimental shipping
- Website redesign: 11-year gap between updates — slow infrastructure investment, but launched today
- Tom's secondment: 2-3 months to COP (November) — clear timeline with hard deadline
- **The capacity constraint:** Fundraising consumes Leo's time — "the amount of time I spend doing that is enormous." Chasing warm leads, waiting for board decisions, managing existing funder relationships. This is the drag on everything else.

### Clock
**Score: 2 — Weak**
**Evidence:**
- **European Climate Foundation** (major funder): cut 30% last year. This year: "we don't know yet. It won't be more than what you currently get." Leo has a "massive question mark" over an "important chunk" of funding. This is the single largest risk in the Carbon Brief picture.
- **Oceans funder:** Cold approach, 2.5 years (unusually long), but designated for a specific team (editor + 2 reporters). Creates allocation tension — Leo doesn't know if core funding is secure enough to actually build this team. Could end up as supplementary funding for existing staff rather than growth.
- **Wellcome Trust:** Was a warm lead for human health/climate pillar — gone quiet, crickets. Unreliable pipeline.
- **Funding cycle:** 12-month cycles are the norm. "Pretty limiting for recruitment, retention, all the rest."
- **Fundraising capacity:** No dedicated fundraiser. Leo does it all. "Challenging from a time management point of view" — understatement.
- No visible shutdown plan. No articulated trigger for what happens if ECF pulls out entirely.
- **The Gazette comparison:** Anna has ~1 year for core (Clock 3). Leo has an unknown ECF number with a downward trajectory. This is worse.

## What surprised me

- **Cosmos is further along than the pre-read assumed.** The briefing called it "emerging" with "no publicly visible timeline." Reality: launched, live, generating press, ranking the world's most-cited climate scientists. This is a material upgrade to the Offer dimension.
- **The ECF funding uncertainty is worse than expected.** 30% cut last year + unknown this year + Leo visibly stressed about it. This is not a theoretical clock problem — it's an active, present constraint shaping every decision.
- **Leo's commercial self-awareness is striking.** He said "I can't imagine someone paying enough" and "there may not be commercial applications." That's not weakness — it's the opposite of the performative confidence you see everywhere else in this space. It makes him more credible, not less.
- **The structural comparison with Gazette is counterintuitive.** Carbon Brief has the better Offer (real infrastructure asset vs. audience-volume play). Gazette has the better Culture (dedicated commercial hire vs. temporary secondment). The organizations have strengths in opposite dimensions.

## Recommendation signal

- **Phase 2 candidate?** Yes — Carbon Brief is the most commercially interesting organization in this cohort. The infrastructure thinking is real, not borrowed. The membership design principles are sound. The commercial learning is being taken seriously.
- **Core tension to resolve:** Leo is the sole commercial thinker, fundraiser, and editorial director. None of the commercial potential is realizable without freeing him from at least one of these roles. Tom's secondment is a start, but it's 2-3 months of temporary capacity, not a structural shift.
- **The Meliore play:** Alan's instinct (Meliore as Carbon Brief's first customer, not funder) is sharper than funding outright. It gives Leo a commercial contract to learn from, proves the model, and gives Meliore access to infrastructure data it needs. But someone still needs to service the contract — Leo can't do it alone.
- **Risk: ECF uncertainty could force an editorial-only survival mode** that kills the commercial experiment before it matures. The Tom secondment is the right move at the right time, but it's fragile — if Leo gets pulled back into emergency fundraising, the secondment collapses.

## Revenue thesis

Carbon Brief's strongest commercial assets are:
- **Project Cosmos** — proprietary climate science metadata database, launched, generating buzz, closed source
- **Membership-ready audience** — COP Insider Pass validated willingness to pay for access/utility (not content)
- **Trust and brand** — 15 years of editorial authority, widely cited, institutional credibility
- **Learning culture** — Leo designs experiments to generate data, not just to execute

### 90-day first move
1. **Deep-dive the COP trial data** — extract conversion rates, user value signals, churn points
2. **Pressure-test pricing with trusted partners** — before COP launch, get real feedback on the three-tier model
3. **Formalize Exeter relationship** — at least a memorandum of understanding on IP and commercial terms
4. **Pilot Meliore as first API customer** — a paid contract, not a grant, that proves the model and gives Leo a reference customer
