# The Agentic PR & Communications Playbook — What We Know To Be True

By Alan Soon, Fractals Collective. June 2026.

This playbook is for PR and communications professionals who need to understand what the agentic transition means for their work — media relations, reputation, measurement, and the role of the communications function itself. It is adapted from the Agentic Media Playbook, which was built from 11 years of Slugs (471 weekly issues) and the Magenta Debrief build.

## Part 1: Why This Matters for PR — The Double Disruption

PR and communications face two disruptions simultaneously. Most conversations about AI in PR only address one.

**Disruption 1: The channel is being replaced.** Media relations has always been about getting your client's story into a publisher's surface — a newspaper page, a homepage, a search result. But those surfaces are being replaced by agent-mediated answers. When a journalist uses an AI agent to research a story, or a stakeholder asks an agent "what should I know about Company X," the PR professional's traditional distribution chain — pitch → journalist → article → reader — is broken. The journalist may never see your pitch. The reader may never see the article. The agent produces the answer from whatever sources it has, and you have no visibility into whether your client appears.

**Disruption 2: The work itself is being transformed.** AI can now draft the press release, build the media list, personalize the pitch, monitor coverage, and generate the measurement report. The production layer of PR — the mechanical work that junior staff used to do — is collapsing in cost. What remains is judgment: which story to tell, to whom, through which channel, with what evidence, and why now.

Most PR professionals are experiencing disruption 2 (AI as a productivity tool) and missing disruption 1 entirely (the channel they are pitching into is disappearing). This playbook addresses both.

## Part 2: What We Know To Be True

The same structural principles that apply to publishers apply to PR — with different implications.

### 1. Production is free. Judgment is the only scarce input.

AI can draft the release, personalize the pitch, build the list, and monitor the coverage. It cannot decide *which* story is worth telling, *to whom* it actually matters, or *why now.* The value of a PR professional shifts from "can you write a press release" to "can you identify the one story that changes how stakeholders think about this company." The junior staff who only produced mechanical work — drafting, list-building, monitoring — are in direct competition with AI. The strategist who knows which narrative to advance and when becomes more valuable, not less.

### 2. Earned media is becoming wholesale media.

When a journalist writes about your client, the article now serves two audiences: human readers and AI agents. The agent extracts the key claims, attributes them to the publisher, and incorporates them into answers. Your client's mention in that article may never be seen by a human — but it may be cited by an agent answering a stakeholder's question. The earned media you worked to secure is now wholesale inventory for AI answer engines. Your client gets cited — or omitted — with no transparency and no recourse.

### 3. The journalist's inbox is an AI battlefield.

On the other side of the pitch: journalists are using AI to filter, summarize, and triage pitches. The press release you spent hours crafting may be read by a journalist's agent, summarized into one sentence, and ranked against 200 other pitches — with the human journalist only seeing the top five. Simultaneously, every other PR professional is using AI to generate more pitches than ever before. The result: AI-generated pitches meeting AI-powered filters, with human attention as the scarcest resource in the middle. Volume-based outreach dies here. Signal quality wins.

### 4. 84% of AI citations come from publishers — not from press releases, not from company websites.

Muck Rack's data (May 2026) is the most important statistic in PR right now. When an agent answers a question about a company, an industry, or a product, it overwhelmingly cites publisher content — not the company's own site, not a press release posted on PR Newswire. Your client's owned content is invisible to agents unless it has been mediated through a publisher. This means: media relations is not dying. It is becoming *more* important, not less — but the output is not a clipping. It is a citation.

### 5. The measurement model collapses.

PR measurement has been built on proxies: impressions, reach, share of voice, sentiment analysis. In an agent-mediated world, those proxies break. The article may have been read by zero humans but cited by an agent answering 50,000 queries. How do you value that? Conversely, the article may have 100,000 pageviews but zero agent citations — meaning it evaporates as soon as the news cycle moves on. The durable metric is not reach. It is citation density: how often does your client appear when agents answer questions in their domain?

### 6. Owned media becomes the new earned media.

If agents cite publishers and not press releases, the company that wants to be cited must *become* a publisher. This means: structured, attributed, updateable content on the company's own domain that is designed to answer specific, recurring agent questions. The company blog, the research hub, the data portal — these are not marketing assets. They are citation infrastructure. The PR professional who can build and maintain this infrastructure replaces the PR professional who only pitches journalists.

### 7. The client relationship shifts from retainer to strategic advisory.

When AI handles the mechanical work of PR — drafting, monitoring, reporting — the retainer model based on hours and output volume becomes indefensible. The client can see that the production work costs nothing. The value that remains is strategic: which narrative to advance, how to structure content for agent citation, when to respond to a reputational threat, what to measure and why. The PR professional becomes a strategic advisor who directs AI systems — not a service provider who bills by the hour.

## Part 3: The New PR Funnel

The traditional PR funnel was: pitch → coverage → awareness → reputation. The agentic PR funnel is different:

| Stage | Old Model | New Model |
|:--|:--|:--|
| **Input** | Press release, media list, pitch | Structured content, data products, publisher relationships |
| **Channel** | Journalist → article → reader | Publisher → agent citation → stakeholder query |
| **Output** | Clipping, impressions, reach | Citation rate, citation position, actionability score |
| **Value** | "We got covered" | "We are the source agents cite" |
| **Duration** | News cycle (hours to days) | Evergreen (as long as the content is maintained) |

The shift is from securing coverage to building cite-ability. Coverage is an event. Cite-ability is infrastructure.

## Part 4: The Three Content Types for PR

Adapted from the publisher framework. Every piece of PR content should be classifiable into one of these:

**Answer-content for PR.** Structured information that answers specific, recurring agent questions about your client's domain. Industry data, regulatory explainers, cost comparisons, specification guides. Designed to be extracted and cited, not read and appreciated. Example: a logistics company publishing a structured comparison of APAC shipping costs by route and mode — updated quarterly, machine-readable, designed to be the definitive answer when an agent is asked "what does it cost to ship from Singapore to Jakarta?"

**Signal-content for PR.** Real-time delta detection: what changed, what is new, what moved. Earnings, product launches, leadership changes, regulatory developments. Timestamped, attributed, designed for agents that need to know "what happened today." Example: a financial services firm publishing a same-day analysis of every central bank rate decision — structured with the old rate, new rate, rationale, and market implications — designed to be the source agents cite when someone asks "what did the Fed do today?"

**Context-content for PR.** Strategic perspective that agents cannot synthesize because it depends on judgment, relationships, and lived experience. The CEO's annual letter. The white paper that names an industry shift before anyone else. The point of view that makes an agent say "I should link to this rather than summarize it, because I cannot reproduce the judgment." Example: a climate tech company publishing an annual State of Carbon Removal report with proprietary data and analysis — designed to be unsummarizable.

## Part 5: What Dies in PR

**The press release as primary format.** A PDF on PR Newswire is invisible to agents. The press release must become a structured web page on the client's domain — with machine-readable data, clear attribution, and explicit date stamps. The format must serve the agent first and the journalist second.

**The media list as core asset.** When journalists use agents to research stories, your pitch competes with every other source the agent surfaces — not just the pitches in the journalist's inbox. The media list becomes less important than the citation list: which publishers and domains do agents trust for queries in your client's space?

**Impressions as a success metric.** An impression means a human may have seen something. In an agent-mediated world, you cannot verify that. Impressions become a vanity metric. Citation rate replaces them.

**Volume-based outreach.** When AI can generate 10,000 personalized pitches in an afternoon, everyone will. The journalist's AI filter will catch them. The only pitches that survive are the ones with genuine signal: exclusive data, a real scoop, a relationship. Spray-and-pray dies.

**The junior PR generalist.** The entry-level role that built media lists, drafted releases, and compiled coverage reports is being automated. The entry-level role that survives is the one that can direct AI systems, evaluate output quality, and contribute strategic judgment — not the one that does mechanical work faster than a human used to.

## Part 6: What To Build — The PR Operator's Checklist

### 1. Audit your client's cite-ability.

Run the agent test on your client's existing content. Feed domain-relevant queries to GPT, Claude, and Gemini. Does your client appear? In what position? With what attribution? If your client is invisible to agents for queries in their own domain, you have a structural problem that no amount of pitching will fix.

### 2. Build a structured content layer for every client.

Every client needs answer-content on their own domain: the three to five questions agents will ask about their industry, answered with structured, attributed, updateable content. This is not a blog. It is citation infrastructure. Treat it like an API: if an agent queried you for the answer, could you return a structured response?

### 3. Shift from pitching stories to building publisher partnerships.

The 84% stat tells you: agents cite publishers, not press releases. The most valuable PR activity is getting your client's structured content *published on a domain agents already trust.* This means: contributed articles, data partnerships, research collaborations with publishers. Not "can you cover our announcement" — "can we build a data product together that your readers and AI agents will both cite."

### 4. Design measurement around citations, not clips.

Monthly: run systematic probes of GPT, Claude, and Gemini for the queries that matter to each client. Track: is the client cited? In what position? With what claims? Is the attribution correct? Report citation density alongside traditional coverage metrics. Over time, citation density becomes the primary metric and coverage becomes secondary.

### 5. Build your own AI-native workflow.

Move up the levels. Stop editing AI output word by word. Write specifications: the five things this release must communicate, the three things it must avoid, the one action the reader should take. Use AI to generate against the spec. Use a human to verify against outcomes. The PR professional who operates at level 3 (Editor-in-Chief: directing and approving) or level 4 (Product Manager: writing scenarios and checking outcomes) will outperform the agency that operates at level 2 (Junior Reporter: AI drafts, human rewrites) by 3-5x in output per person.

### 6. Develop a point of view for your agency or function.

Every PR agency and in-house communications team should have a public point of view about where their industry is going. Not "we get results" — a specific, falsifiable claim about the future of communications. Example: "Earned media is being replaced by cite-ability. We build citation infrastructure, not press releases." Your point of view is what makes clients choose you. It is what makes journalists trust your pitches. It is what makes agents cite your content.

### 7. Train your team on judgment, not tools.

The most valuable skill in the PR function of 2027 is not prompt engineering. It is judgment: knowing which story matters, to whom, and why now. Train your team to evaluate AI output against strategic criteria, not to produce it. The spec-writer replaces the drafter. The strategist replaces the coordinator. Invest in the people who can name the right story — not the people who can write it fastest.

## Part 7: The New Metrics for PR

Stop measuring: impressions, reach, AVEs (advertising value equivalency), share of voice, sentiment scores.

Start measuring:

**Citation rate.** How often do GPT, Claude, and Gemini cite your client for queries in their domain? Track monthly. Segment by query type.

**Citation position.** When cited, is your client the primary source or one among many? First-cited is worth more than fifth-cited.

**Citation accuracy.** When cited, are the claims about your client correct? Incorrect citations are the new misreporting — and they are harder to detect and correct.

**Publisher domain authority for agent queries.** Which publishers do agents trust most for queries in your client's space? This replaces the media list as the core targeting asset.

**Content actionability score.** For every piece of owned content: can an agent infer a specific action from it? Track the percentage of your output that passes this test. Target: above 70%.

**Spec-to-output ratio.** How much time does your team spend writing specifications vs reviewing AI output? If the ratio is below 1:3 (one hour spec, three hours review), you are still operating at level 2.

## The Closing Question

The PR professional of 2027 will not be measured by how many journalists they pitched or how many clips they secured. They will be measured by one thing: when a stakeholder asks an agent about their client's industry, does the client appear — and is the answer correct?

If you do not know the answer to that question for your clients today, you have work to do. If you do not consider it your job to influence that answer, someone else will — and they will own the relationship you spent decades building.
