**If you're a student pilot feeling overwhelmed or questioning if flying is for you, stop right now. This is for you.**

I've been there. I have felt the crushing weight of self-doubt you're experiencing right now. I remember sitting in the plane, wondering if I was good enough. In fact, after nearly two years as an instructor and over a thousand hours flown with students, I still have those moments. But trust me when I say this: the way you're feeling is not a sign that you're failing. In fact, it's the opposite. It's proof that you're growing.

In this article, I'm going to share my journey, some powerful stories, and practical tips that will help you regain your confidence and reignite your passion for flying.

**Every Pilot Struggles**

I really want to drive this point home:**every single pilot faces setbacks and frustration.** You might look around and think you're the only one struggling, but I promise you, most of us don't broadcast our tough days. We might think our friends at the flight school are just breezing through, but I guarantee they have hit bumps in the road too.

In fact, as flight instructors, we are specifically taught how to handle learning plateaus. A learning plateau is a phase where you feel like your progress has stopped and you're not improving. This isn't a sign that you are a bad pilot; it's a normal part of the learning process. Virtually everyone that sticks with it goes on to become a competent pilot. I'm going to say that again:**everyone that doesn't give up goes on to become a competent pilot.**

You may have heard the scary statistic that over 80% of student pilots never get their license. It's a terrifying number, and it's true. While some people stop due to life circumstances or financial issues, many quit because they get discouraged. This often happens when flying stops being as fun or your progress slows down. I don't want you to become part of that statistic. The fact that you are taking the time to read this tells me that you have what it takes to be in the 20% that make it through.

Often, the difference between those who quit and those who get their license is simply perseverance. It's not about being a "natural" or having some incredible talent. It's about pushing through the tough moments.**The successful pilot is the one that struggles and keeps going.** Please remind yourself that you are in good company. Even the pilot you look up to went through these same exact struggles.

**The Emotional Rollercoaster of Flight Training**

Flight training is an emotional roller coaster. One day you're on cloud nine after nailing a maneuver or a landing, and the next day you can't replicate it, feeling utterly defeated. What happened?

Learning any complex skill—and flying is a very complex skill—is often not a linear process. You don't improve a little bit each time in a nice, upward, straight line. Instead, you improve in spurts and plateaus. Think of it like climbing a staircase rather than walking up a ramp. Those flat steps are your plateaus, where it feels like nothing's improving. It's frustrating, I know. You're putting in time, money, and so much effort, so when you don't see progress, you start questioning everything.

But here's something encouraging:**progress is still happening.** There's a saying I love: "Progress often feels invisible until it's not." You might be improving beneath the surface without even realizing it. Then one day, it just clicks. Psychologists call this the "plateau of latent potential," where you're quietly building skills until you hit a breakthrough.

So if you feel like you're smashing your head against a wall, remember that with each lesson, you are laying the groundwork. The improvements might be a subtle, smooth control input or a better radio call, but they're all accumulating. Think about how far you've come since your first lesson. You've likely learned tons, even if it doesn't feel like it.

A quick personal story: when I was a student pilot, I hit a huge wall with landings. I felt like I was progressing so fast, learning maneuvers and feeling great. Then suddenly, we moved into landings, and I just couldn't land the plane. And I don't mean some were good and some were bad. No, every single landing was horrible. I'm talking about ballooning, bouncing, missing the centerline—you name it, I did it wrong. I remember driving home from the airport after almost every flight lesson thinking, "I don't think this is for me. I don't know if I'm ever going to get it."

I was completely stuck in my own head. But I kept coming back. I was frustrated, but I loved flying way too much to just walk away. After what felt like endless repetition, one day it all just clicked. I'll never forget the flight where my landings started to come together; I soloed shortly thereafter. It felt easy, almost like my hands knew what to do on their own. The progress had been building invisibly over all of those hard landings, and finally, it showed itself. I'm pretty sure I turned to my instructor and shouted, "Did you see that?" after I buttered my first landing, and we both had a good laugh.**My point is, your breakthrough is coming too. You just need to keep showing up.**

**Remember Your "Why"**

Whenever you're in a slump, it's easy to lose sight of why you began this journey. Let's take a step back and remember your "why." Was it the dream of soaring above the clouds? The challenge of learning a new skill? Maybe you've always imagined taking your friends or family up in a plane, or you have the ambition of becoming an airline pilot. Whatever your personal "why" is, you need to hold on to it.

Flying is supposed to be fun and inspiring, not just a grind. One of the best remedies for burnout is to recapture the joy of flying. If every flight lesson has been nothing but maneuvers and critiques, do something to remind yourself of the fun side. Ask your instructor if you can go for a nice, relaxing flight or invite them to go for a "$100 hamburger"—a common expression for spending an unnecessary amount of money to go get food at another airport.

This isn't wasted time. Taking a break from the routine and doing something fun might be exactly what you need to reignite your motivation. As you're cruising to a new breakfast spot, you'll likely look out the window and think, "Hey, I'm flying an airplane. This is pretty freaking cool." In that moment, you might even realize how far you've come—tuning radios, navigating, using the GPS—all things that used to be overwhelming. This low-pressure flight can remind you of why you're putting in all this hard work.

**The Power of Community and Mindset**

Connecting with others in the aviation community can provide perspective and moral support. Talking to other pilots, especially those who remember their student days, will show you that everyone has their war stories about training. You'll find comfort in hearing, "Oh yeah, I did that too. Here's how I got past it." It reminds you that struggling is part of the process.

The mental game is huge in flight training. You may be flooded with self-doubt. Thoughts like, "I'll never get this," or "My instructor must think I'm helpless," might be swirling in your head. We are often our worst critics. Here’s how to shift your mindset:

- **Be kind to yourself.** Struggling doesn't mean you're incapable; it means you're learning. Reframe mistakes as lessons.

- **Stop the unhealthy comparison.** This is your flight training, not a race. The national average for a private pilot's license is between 65 and 70 hours. Whether it takes you 45 hours or 100, you all earn the same certificate. A slower, steadier pace often results in more solid skills.

- **Focus on small wins.** Actively look for what you did better this time. Maybe your radio calls were crisper or you nailed the crosswind correction on takeoff. Celebrate these small victories.

- **Visualize your success.** Picture yourself as a certified pilot, taking friends up on a sunny day. Holding that vision close can pull you through the hard days.

**Practical Tips to Get You Through the Slump**

1.**Talk to Your Instructor Openly:** Have an honest conversation about how you're feeling. A good instructor will work with you to adjust the game plan, perhaps by revisiting fundamentals or explaining things in a new way.

2.**Break It Down:** If a big skill like landing is overwhelming, focus on one small part at a time. One lap, focus on approach speed. The next, on leveling off. These small successes build on each other.

3.**Change the Scenery:** Fly to a different airport. A simple change can make flying fun again and present new learning opportunities.

4.**Fly with a Different Instructor:** This can be intimidating, but a fresh perspective can work wonders. A different instructor might explain something in a way that just clicks.

5.**Take a Short, Strategic Break:** If you're feeling burnt out, a week or two off can help reset your mindset. Use the time to rest and study. Just be sure to set a date to return so a short break doesn't turn into quitting.

6.**Reevaluate Your Schedule and Habits:** Are you flying often enough to retain information? Or are you flying so much that you're burning out? Balance is key. Also, acknowledge and manage stress from outside of flying.

**Your Breakthrough is Coming**

This tough phase you're in will pass. One day, sooner than you think, you'll look back and see how far you've come. The obstacles you face now are shaping you into a safer, more resilient pilot.

Keep showing up. Keep chipping away. Lean on your love for flying. One day soon, you'll be the one telling the next generation of student pilots that you remember being in their shoes. Stick with it. I promise you, it is all worth it.
