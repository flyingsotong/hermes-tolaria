Flight training can easily run into the tens of thousands of dollars. If you're treating it as a hobby, understand that it's a luxury-level expense. Be smart with your money. Unless you're fully committed to going the commercial route, avoid student loans—you'll be paying off interest long after the thrill wears off.

### Fly often, or you’ll pay to relearn.

Inconsistency kills momentum. Gaps in your flight schedule—whether due to weather, life, or work—can set you back. Skills fade fast, and you’ll end up spending money re-learning things you already covered. Try to stay in a rhythm, even if it’s just a few short flights a week.

### Be your own final authority on safety.

Just because your instructor is comfortable flying doesn’t mean you should be. If a plane doesn’t feel safe—don’t fly it. I’ve been in aircraft with broken door latches, faulty mixture controls, and questionable maintenance. Instructors said, “Don’t worry about it,” but the truth is: if the plane’s not airworthy, it’s not worth the risk.

### Learn to read maintenance logs early.

Don’t wait until hour 40 to start asking about the plane’s maintenance history. Look through the logs yourself and ask questions. Is the AD compliance current? When was the last oil change? Did the last squawk get addressed? Knowing how to spot red flags will serve you far beyond your checkride.

Don’t train in a junker just because it’s available. Many flight schools use aircraft that are barely held together by duct tape and inspection stickers. Don’t be afraid to request a better trainer. You deserve a clean, reliable aircraft for your checkride—and your life. You’re paying for safety and quality—demand it.

### Use your heading bug in the pattern. 

A small tip that makes a big difference: set your heading bug before takeoff and adjust it throughout the pattern. It’ll help you stay oriented without needing to glance back at the runway (don't try to find the runway) or mentally guess your direction. Your patterns will become cleaner and more precise with less mental load.

### Look for creative ways to fund your goals.

Can’t keep up with training costs? That’s okay. Find other ways to generate income—rental properties, side businesses, and online work. The point isn’t to quit—it’s okay to pause strategically and return when you can fly with purpose and financial freedom. Don't break the bank.

### Remember: flying teaches more than aviation. 

Beyond stick-and-rudder skills, flight training challenges your patience, discipline, and decision-making. It forces you to take full responsibility—not just for learning, but for your life and the lives of others. This isn’t a hobby you can casually approach. The laws of physics don’t care how much you love flying—a lack of discipline, poor habits, or skipping steps can cost you everything. This is not Microsoft flight simulator. There is no respawn.

Don’t solo until you’re truly ready—not just signed off on paper, but confident in your skills, solid in your checklist flow, and calm under pressure. Flying is one of the most rewarding things you’ll ever do—but only if you respect it enough to earn it the right way.
