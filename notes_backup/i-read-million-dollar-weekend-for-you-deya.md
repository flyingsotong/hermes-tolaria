Alright, so how do you start your first business in a weekend? That is what Noah Kagan purports to teach us in his book _Million Dollar Weekend_, which we’re discussing in this episode of Book Club, the ongoing series where I distill and discuss highlights and summaries from some of my favorite books. This is a fantastic book for aspiring entrepreneurs. If you’ve ever thought about starting your own business and haven't yet done it, you need to read this book because it provides a step-by-step roadmap on how to do that.

The book is broken down into three parts: Start It, Build It, and Grow It. In this video, I’m focusing on the first two parts: Start It and Build It. If you’re watching this and haven’t yet started your business, you might have been thinking about it for several years without taking action.

**Myths**

**There's a pervasive myth among aspiring entrepreneurs that you need the right idea before you can start. However, Noah argues that you don’t start a business by first having the right idea and then starting the business. Instead, you start by deciding to start a business and then find a process to come up with the right idea.**

The core message in the first chapter, which is aptly named "Just F**\*ing Start," is to begin before you are ready.**No one feels ready to start a business, just like no one feels ready to have kids.** The timing is never quite right, so you have to just start. Noah emphasizes that most people overthink first and act later, whereas successful entrepreneurs act first and figure it out later. Rather than trying to plan your way into the confidence to act, just start acting.

Noah's motto,**"Now, not how,"** resonates deeply. Whenever we think of doing something, we often get bogged down by the "how."**Instead, think about the smallest action you can take right now.** Even if you don’t know what to do about everything, there's probably a small action you can figure out and take. Once you start, you can then get going with the broader task. The first chapter offers a lot of emotional support, stressing that the emotional side of things often holds people back more than the technical aspects.

**Customers**

Now, let's talk about the customer-first approach to building a business. I've interviewed hundreds of entrepreneurs, and all of them advocate for this approach.**You're not trying to find an idea for a business first. Instead, start with the people you want to serve. A business fundamentally solves a problem for someone willing to pay for that solution.** Many entrepreneurs fail by trying to build something without verifying that there's a market willing to pay for it. This is especially common among students who come up with ideas but don't first verify their market.

When generating business ideas, customers come first. You need someone to sell to before the product or service, even before the idea of building a business. For example, consider a dog walking app. The founder-first approach involves spending hours thinking about the app, coming up with clever names, hiring a designer for a logo, setting up an LLC, watching videos about app development, buying a domain name, and eventually giving up.**The customer-first approach involves calling three people who have dogs, asking if they’d pay for a dog-walking service, discovering their actual problem is finding dog sitters when traveling, and securing their commitment.**

In the customer-first approach, you start with an idea, talk to potential customers, see if the idea has merit, adjust based on feedback, and secure payment before developing the service.

For instance, if you're thinking about starting a business, ask yourself who you're going to sell to. This should ideally be within your own zone of influence. If you're a student, your zone of influence could be your peers, their parents, university contacts, or local businesses. Ideally, you want to target customers who have money. It's much easier to create a business where you're targeting people who can afford your solution. For example, instead of targeting your student friends who might be broke, think about the problems their parents might face or consider tapping into funds from your college or university.

**Problems to solve**

Once you've identified potential customers, the next step is to brainstorm their problems. The easiest way to do this is by asking them directly.**What are the things in their lives that are annoying or that they already pay for but aren't satisfied with? If you do this enough, you'll come up with a long list of problems that people have. The world progresses through people discovering problems and then finding solutions. For example, Uber started because someone found it annoying to hail a cab or call a taxi company.**

When validating your business idea, there are two approaches: the founder-first approach and the customer-first approach. The founder-first approach involves spending a lot of time and resources on developing an idea without verifying if there's a demand for it. This often leads to failure because you might end up building something that no one wants. In contrast, the customer-first approach involves directly engaging with potential customers to understand their needs and securing their commitment before developing the product or service.

Here’s an example of a customer-first approach: suppose you want to create a dog-walking app. Instead of spending months developing the app, start by contacting dog owners to see if they need such a service. You might discover that their real problem is finding dog sitters when they travel. If they’re willing to pay for this service, you have validated your idea. This approach saves time and resources by ensuring there is a demand before you invest heavily in development.

A practical example from the book involves an email sent by one of Noah's friends, Boris, to validate a business idea. Boris sent an email to friends offering home-cooked meals delivered by a personal chef for $20. By asking for payment upfront, he validated the demand for his service. This method can be applied to various business ideas—get commitment and payment from customers before you start building.

**Irritations**

You can also find business ideas by looking at your own problems. If something irritates you or has been on your to-do list for a while, it might be a problem others face too. For example, if there's something you wanted to buy but couldn't find, it might be a business opportunity. Noah provides prompts to identify these problems: what irritated you this morning, what’s been on your to-do list for over a week, what do you regularly fail to do well, and what did you want to buy recently but couldn't find?

In summary, starting a business involves finding a problem, ensuring people are willing to pay for a solution, and securing commitment before developing the product. This customer-first approach is crucial and can save aspiring entrepreneurs from wasting time and resources. By focusing on the needs of potential customers and validating ideas through direct engagement and pre-sales, you increase the likelihood of your business succeeding.
