**Session 3 — Origin of fear** (trigger warning: mentions of violence)

Bhanupriya's written reflection:

1. Unsafe childhood: I grew up in an otherwise loving and nurturing family. As the first grandchild in my paternal family and first child of my parents, I was the absolute focus of attention. But my mother often used to beat me up and it was always in relation to academic performance or work at home. There was constant pressure to be excellent at everything — school, academics, being a perfect human. Any single mistake, however small, attracted punishment, often a good thrashing. One example: I must have been 7 or 8 and my mom was sitting with some older cousins and a freedom fighter died. It was the time of no internet and newspapers were the only source of information. They asked me who this freedom fighter was and I said I didn't know. It was followed by ridicule from the other women and my mother just joined them. Not knowing was not safe. This is where the fear of intellectual failure comes from. It was never OK to not be/appear to be intellectual. My body and mind just internalized the fear. I loved learning but it did not come with joy but with the fear of failing = ridicule, punishment.
2. Unsafe with peers: When you are a child knowing and doing things much beyond your age (I was a bit precocious much like most kids are these days), you do not fit in among your peers. They look at you with disdain and then bully you. It happened with me at school and also among my cousins (mostly male). In a patriarchal society like India, girls are supposed to know less and be less. And when they are not, they are excluded. I face this in my extended family even today. The contradiction of expectation of being more at home and being less outside with peers created a very strange idea of being for me. I wasn't a person. I was a person only if I achieved at home and if I was 'normal' outside. Its very conflicting. My innate need was to just learn more but also have healthy friendships like everyone else. This duality has carried on for most of my adult life where I have felt the need to achieve because I loved the idea of doing something good but the fear of failure as well. As time went by, I did things (innate need and motivation) but didn't enjoy the process. (Like Behanbox.)
3. What swimming did to me:
   - Showed me what love means: I feared water and got into swimming because I had no choice. But as I kept smashing targets ahead of time, I kept wondering how I was able to do this. Did reaching goals give me a high? I thought very deeply about this, and I realised that it isn't reaching goals. If given a goal, I will perhaps do it but would I get the same satisfaction as swimming. I started running/speed walking but it didn't give me the same feeling as swimming does. It taught me what it feels to love something deeply where smashing targets isn't a matter of getting validation from others but an inner satisfaction. In the beginning I would smash targets but never tell anyone (except my husband) and felt very happy. It was the joy of learning.
   - Find people who can help you: I would not have achieved what I did in swimming if I did not have a coach. My coach did not judge me or push me. He knew when to step in with his words without being preachy. I remember early on when my body couldn't take the stress, I messaged him I was unwell and stayed away for 4 days. When I went back, he smiled and asked me what happened. I told him body felt tired and I slept most of the time. Then he spoke for 15 mins (for the first and last time ever) and told me that body is nothing, mind matters. The days I feel like this are the days I must turn up in the pool. Those words stay with me everyday mainly because how he believed that I knew the weight of what he was saying. I see him coaching others too, but the way he treats me is completely different. He really believes in me and treats me like an athlete and not like a swimming student. I love that. For the first time in my life, I feel someone actually stood by me and was patient with me and also treated me as a 'person'. This matters.

*This is why I also love my sessions with you. Opening up about all this is not easy but why I do this is because you treat me as a person and not as a 'hero' or as a 'case to be worked upon'. You are helping me find what I love and the feelings that come with it. Love is joy but also confidence and energy. I wish that people are treated for who they are, not what is envisioned of them. I wish kids are taught early that they are persons and not the experiments/lab rats of their parents. And today I really wish we all discover what we love. Because beyond fear lies love.*
