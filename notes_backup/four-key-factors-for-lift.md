**1. Angle of Attack (AOA or CL)**
- The angle between the relative wind and the chord line of the wing
- Increasing AOA increases lift up to the critical angle (stall point)
- Too high AOA leads to airflow separation and stall
**2. Airspeed (V²)**
- Faster airspeed generally creates more lift
- Lift increases with the square of airspeed (double speed = quadruple lift)
- This is why slower aircraft need larger wings or higher AOA
**3. Air Density (ρ)**
- Denser air produces more lift
- Affected by:
 - Altitude (higher altitude = less dense air)
 - Temperature (warmer air = less dense)
 - Humidity (more humid = less dense)
**4. Wing Surface Area (S)**
- Wing Area: Larger surface area creates more lift
- Wing Shape (Airfoil): Different designs optimize for different conditions
- Aspect Ratio: Long, narrow wings are more efficient
- Wing Configuration: Flaps, slats, slots affect lift capability
**Lift Equation**
L = CL × ρ × V² × S × ½
- L = Lift
- CL = Coefficient of lift
- ρ = Air density
- V = Velocity
- S = Wing area
- ½ = Constant
These factors work together, allowing pilots to adjust for different flight conditions. For example, when air density decreases at higher altitudes, pilots can compensate by increasing airspeed or angle of attack to maintain the same lift.​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​​
