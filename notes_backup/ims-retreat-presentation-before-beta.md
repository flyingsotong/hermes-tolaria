**Key Takeaway**

"Good institutions are not defined by rigid adherence to tradition but by their ability to adapt while maintaining core values."

The digital revolution, however, upended this information hierarchy. The rise of the internet and social media broke down barriers to publishing and distributing information. Gurri argues that this explosion of information has led to widespread skepticism toward traditional institutions. The public, now equipped with alternative viewpoints and easier access to information, has increasingly questioned elite narratives. This democratization of information has eroded the authority once held by traditional gatekeepers, leading to what Gurri calls a “crisis of authority.”

This technological shift did more than introduce new tools—it reshaped the entire information ecosystem, creating an environment ripe for a new type of trusted figure: the creator or influencer. As trust in mainstream institutions declines, many people now turn to individuals who are seen as more transparent, authentic, and relatable. Influencers and creators offer a direct connection to their audiences, contrasting with the sometimes opaque and impersonal nature of large institutions. This personal link fosters trust, even when these creators lack formal credentials or journalistic training.

In essence, influencers and creators are filling the vacuum left by the decline in institutional authority. They’re not seizing power maliciously but are capitalizing on the breakdown of information monopolies. By offering content that feels tailored and responsive to their audience’s concerns, they’ve redefined how trust and legitimacy are constructed in the digital age. Trust, once tied to institutional prestige, is now often rooted in the perceived authenticity and relatability of an individual.

This ecosystem is evolving though. The technical and financial barriers that once made independence impractical are falling away, and many journalists are finding they can strike out on their own. The creator economy offers both new monetization options and the promise of something perhaps even more valuable: true independence.

The challenge isn't just about retention either – it's about transformation. Publishers need to ask themselves some hard questions:

How can we create environments where talented journalists can build their brands without feeling constrained?

What value can we offer that platforms can't?

How do we balance institutional authority with individual authenticity?

What new business models might support this transformation?

Q: What are influencers doing that mainstream media can't?

The first problem is practical: how do you invest in individuals? Traditional grant-making frameworks aren’t really designed for someone building their journalism career on platforms like Substack or YouTube. There’s more risk involved, less institutional accountability, and an informality that can make traditional funders uneasy. (Of course, it’s that very informality that often makes these creators effective and trustworthy in the eyes of their audiences.) Even if you navigate these technical hurdles, a deeper challenge remains: how do you promote journalistic standards and values in a space that tend to take pride in breaking away from traditional journalism?
