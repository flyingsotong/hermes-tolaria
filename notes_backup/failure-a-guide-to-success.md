**Failure: A Guide to Success What I've learned from coaching media entrepreneursWhat are the most common beliefs among my clients?**

At the start of the year, I began my journey as a professional coach. As part of the certification with the International Coaching Federation, I had to put in 100 hours of coaching. This is what I've learned from having had all this time talking to xxx folks from xxx countries.

**Have a clear idea of what success looks like.** That way you don't focus on your failures or your fears. Instead of writing success statements, entrepreneurs are writing failure statements in their heads, eg. What if I can't find customers? What if I can't manage a team? What if I can't work with partners? Instead, flip those thoughts: How can I find customers? How can I manage a team? How can I work with partners? These questions will lead to a clear success statement. I know I'm successful when I can find 100 customers.

**Define your failure.**Know when it's time to let go. I know this isn't for me if I can't find product market fit in 6 months, or I can't fund raise in a year, or I still don't understand what my audience wants. Draw that line, and give yourself permission to walk away. Neither success nor failure is final -- but you need them both to start the next chapter.

**Nothing is meant to be forever**-- including the startup that you want to create and run. Take the stress out of the equation by asking yourself how long do you really want to do this for? What milestones do you need to hit to know? What would make you want to do this for 3 years?

**Take responsibility for your past, present and future.**Things happen because you allowed it. Things didn't happen because you didn't do it. The strongest entrepreneuers are those who take responsibility and move forward instead of blaming others.

Normalizing the wrong things -- that this is meant to be hard... and therefore losing positive energy

**Perfection is your biggest competitor** We all procrastinate. We drag things out unnecessarily because we don't want to get to it. The important thing is to understand why you're doing that. Most often, it's a drive for perfection and the assumption that not getting there is failure. It's not.

**There is no "right" career path** This industry is changing quickly. Old pathways are done. Don't live someone else's career. You have yours.

There are no straight lines in careers today for you to follow. If you're trying to connect the dots that way, you'll get frustrated because everyone's circumstances are different. But more importantly, if you're defining your path based on someone else's, you'll miss a ton of opportunities.

Skills and mindsets are increasingly more important than roles and titles.

You're living longer. There needs to be a better way to live out the rest of your life instead of following a path.


Everyone likes to talk about success, but few are keen to talk about potential failure. This is something that's come up in several coaching calls that I've done recently.

It's a recurring theme that I see from a lot of media entrepreneurs: a lack of clear boundaries around how far they could take their startups.

But neither success nor failure is final. They're just chapters in your story. But you need to write these chapters yourself — no one else will give you permission to succeed OR fail.

The real freedom comes when you own these definitions. When things don't go as planned (and they won't), you'll have clarity instead of chaos.

Draw that line in the sand early. How are you defining failure in your journey? How will you walk away from what you started?


I've noticed something interesting in my recent coaching calls with media entrepreneurs. While we spend hours discussing growth and success metrics, there's one crucial conversation that often gets overlooked: defining failure on your own terms.

It's often easy to say to a coach, "What does success look like for you?" But the harder question is, "What does failure look like for you?"

Media founders I work with have ambitious visions. But what's missing is clarity around boundaries — specifically, how far they're willing to take their startups before deciding it's done. Knowing where to draw that line is critical.

Neither success nor failure is permanent. They're learning opportunities. But to make the most of them, you have to define them by your own terms, and not what your friends and family see or want of you. If you are hoping that one of them gives you permission to call success or failure, you're setting yourself up for great disappointment. No one else can do it for you.
