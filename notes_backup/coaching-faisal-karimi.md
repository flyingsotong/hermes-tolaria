**January 17, 2024**

---

**Background**

Faisal Karimi is an Afghan media entrepreneur and journalist who fled Afghanistan ~3 years ago after the Taliban takeover. He is currently based in San Jose, CA with family (5 people total, including 3 kids), and is a recent Stanford JSK Journalism Fellow graduate.

He runs two media initiatives:

1. An exile newsroom covering women's issues in Afghanistan (with 15 female journalists working anonymously under Taliban rule)
2. A new media platform for Afghan immigrants in California

He was seeking funding and connections to support his new media platform, and navigating the challenges of being new to the United States — building trust with potential donors and establishing a strong online presence.

---

**Main challenges**

- Financial pressure of supporting family in an expensive Bay Area while trying to launch media ventures
- Dilemma between taking a full-time job for stability vs. pursuing entrepreneurship
- Lack of strong donor/funder connections in the US context
- Cultural adjustment and rebuilding professional network from scratch
- Dealing with trauma of exile and separation from family/home
- Legal status uncertainties affecting opportunities

---

**Key advice given**

1. Focus on telling his powerful personal story more openly on platforms like LinkedIn
2. Launch the website even if not fully ready (especially the English version for donors)
3. Be more direct in asking for help and connections to donors
4. Leverage existing networks and conferences more strategically
5. Two specific connections offered: Ross Settles and Patrick Boehler for exile media support

---

**How I could have better supported him**

1. Earlier structure — the conversation meandered before getting to concrete action items; establishing clear objectives earlier would have helped
2. Deeper emotional support — while his trauma and family pressure were acknowledged, more space to explore the emotional toll would have been valuable
3. More specific action planning — a clearer timeline or action plan for next steps would have been useful
4. Additional resource connections — more specific grant opportunities or emergency funding options for exiled journalists could have been explored
5. Follow-up framework — a clear follow-up mechanism to check on his progress with the suggested connections and LinkedIn outreach
6. Mental health resources — given the trauma he mentioned, information about support services for refugee journalists could have been provided
7. Immediate network activation — rather than just promising to connect him later, real-time introductions during the call could have been made

Valuable points: emotional validation was provided, and the practical networking connections seemed to be appreciated. The suggestion to be more open about his needs and story on LinkedIn was particularly actionable.
