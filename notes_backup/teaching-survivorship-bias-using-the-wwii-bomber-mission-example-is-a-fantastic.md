Lecture Plan: The WWII Bomber & Survivorship Bias

Objective: Students will understand what survivorship bias is and how it can lead to incorrect conclusions by analyzing the famous WWII bomber example.

Materials:

 * Slide 1: Title Slide - "Survivorship Bias: The WWII Bomber Story"

 * Slide 2: Image of a WWII bomber.

 * Slide 3: Diagram showing bullet holes on a returned bomber (like the one Abraham Wald would have seen, with more holes in the wings, fuselage, and tail, and fewer in the engines/cockpit). You can draw this simply or find an image online. Use red dots to represent bullet holes.

 * Slide 4: A blank diagram of a bomber.

 * Optional: A whiteboard or flipchart for brainstorming.

Lecture Outline:

1\. Introduction (5-7 minutes)

 * Hook: Start with a compelling question: "Imagine it's World War II. Allied bombers are being shot down at an alarming rate. The military needs to make a critical decision: where should they add armor to the planes to best protect them and their crews?"

 * Set the Scene: Briefly describe the stakes – lives are being lost, resources are scarce, and they can't armor the entire plane due to weight.

 * Introduce the "Problem": Explain that the military decided to collect data. They examined the planes that returned from missions and mapped out where they had been hit by enemy fire.

 * Show Slide 3 (Bullet Hole Diagram): "This is the kind of data they saw. Notice where the bullet holes are concentrated." (Point to wings, fuselage, tail).

2\. The Intuitive (and Wrong) Solution (5-7 minutes)

 * Ask the Audience: "Based on this data, if you were advising the military, where would you tell them to add the extra armor?"

   * Pause for responses. Most people will say: "Where the most bullet holes are – the wings, fuselage, and tail!"

 * Acknowledge and Validate: "That's a very logical and intuitive conclusion! It seems to make perfect sense. If those areas are getting hit the most, strengthening them should make the planes more resilient."

 * Introduce the Counter-Intuition: "However, a brilliant statistician named Abraham Wald was brought in to look at this problem, and he came to a completely different, counter-intuitive conclusion."

3\. The "Aha!" Moment: Introducing Survivorship Bias (10-15 minutes)

 * The Missing Data: "Wald's genius wasn't just in analyzing the data they had, but in realizing what data they didn't have. What kind of planes were they not seeing?"

   * Guide them to the answer: "The planes that didn't make it back! The ones that were shot down!"

 * Explain the Implication: "Think about it. The planes that returned had bullet holes in certain places. What does that tell you about those places?"

   * Answer: "It tells you that planes can get hit in those places and still survive and return home."

 * The Crucial Insight: "Therefore, the areas with fewer or no bullet holes on the returned planes are actually the most vulnerable areas. Why? Because if a plane got hit there, it didn't survive to be inspected!"

   * Show Slide 4 (Blank Bomber Diagram): "So, if the planes were randomly hit across their surface, and we only see the survivors, where are the deadly hits occurring?" (Draw imagined "critical" areas like engines, cockpit, fuel tanks, etc., and explain that these were the areas likely hit on the planes that didn't return).

 * Define Survivorship Bias: "This is the core concept of Survivorship Bias. It's the logical error of concentrating on the things that made it past some selection process (the 'survivors') while overlooking those that did not (the 'failures' or 'non-survivors'). We only see the 'winners' and mistakenly draw conclusions based solely on their characteristics, ignoring the 'losers' who are absent from our data set."

 * Wald's Recommendation: "Abraham Wald recommended adding armor to the areas where there were no bullet holes on the returning planes – because those were the critical areas where a hit meant the plane went down."

4\. Broader Applications & Discussion (10-15 minutes)

 * Beyond Bombers: Emphasize that survivorship bias isn't just about WWII planes. It's everywhere!

 * Brainstorm Examples (or provide your own):

   * Business: Looking only at successful companies (e.g., Apple, Google) to determine what makes a business successful, ignoring the thousands of startups that failed doing similar things.

   * Self-Help/Success Stories: Reading books by "successful" people and thinking their specific habits or strategies guarantee success, without considering how many people tried the same things and didn't succeed.

   * Investment: Analyzing the performance of mutual funds only by looking at currently existing funds, ignoring those that closed down due to poor performance.

   * Evolution: Focusing on successful adaptations without considering organisms that went extinct.

   * History: Drawing conclusions only from surviving documents, buildings, or artifacts, leading to an incomplete picture of the past.

 * How to Mitigate it:

   * Ask about the "missing data": "What am I not seeing here?" "Who isn't in my sample?"

   * Look for failures: Actively seek out information on what didn't succeed or what didn't survive the process.

   * Consider the complete picture: Understand the selection process and how it might be skewing your observations.

5\. Conclusion & Q\&A (5 minutes)

 * Recap: Briefly summarize survivorship bias and the bomber example.

 * Key Takeaway: "The most important lesson from the WWII bomber story is to always question what data you don't have. Sometimes, the most valuable insights come from the silent majority of failures."

 * Open for Questions: Encourage students to ask questions and discuss further examples.

Tips for Delivery:

 * Storytelling: Present the bomber story as a narrative. Start with the problem, build the tension, then reveal Wald's insight as a clever solution.

 * Visuals are Key: The bullet hole diagram is crucial. Make it clear and easy to understand.

 * Interactive: Ask questions, encourage participation, and let students try to figure out the "correct" answer before revealing Wald's solution.

 * Relatability: Connect the bias to everyday examples so students see its relevance beyond the historical anecdote.

 * Enthusiasm: Your excitement about the concept will be contagious!

By following this structure, you can deliver a memorable and impactful lecture on survivorship bias using the compelling WWII bomber example.
