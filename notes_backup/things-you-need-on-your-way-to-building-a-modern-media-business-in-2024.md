[Entrepreneurship 2](Things%20you%20need%20on%20your%20way%20to%20building%20a%20modern%20media%20business%20in%202024/Entrepreneurship%202)

Who is this session for

- Early stage media entrepreneurs

- Product managers

- Journalists who are looking to jump to a startup

The best thing that could happen to you in business is that you find viability and utility. The next best thing is that you fail fast.

Product and business failure happens when you run out of time or money to figure out how to be valuable.

To succeed, you need to get out of the media business.

Journalism -- or the lack of -- isn't the problem.

Because the whole point of journalism isn't about content. It's about helping people make better decisions.

Polling booth, supermarket stores, financial markets. We all got into journalism because we wanted to do important work for society. That work begins with your ability to service a user's need.

“Text on the internet is arguably the most competitive medium in all of human history. If you’re going to ask people to pay for a newsletter, it has to be something they’re not getting anywhere else.” -- Ben Thompson, Stratechery

1.**Don't just build a . Serve one.**

2. Relevance is in the utility of others

3. Value is in the the utility of others

4. Find out how to make 10 people happy. Then 100

5. If you don't know the first names of your audience, you don't know them

6. It's not the CMS. It's the CRM

7.**Go niche**

8. Value is in understanding what a community needs, and delivering that

9. Don't get sucked into scaling for the sake of scaling. Understanding what your user wants and delivering on that is more important than scale for the sake of scaling.

10.**Understand who you're actually competing against**

11. Obsess about being able to solve a problem for your user -- it's not about being the first to break news

12. We live in an information economy. Economies are defined by scarcity, not abundance (scarcity = value), and in an age of information abundance, what’s scarce?

13. A: Attention. The scale of the world’s largest companies, the wealth of its richest people, and the power of governments are all rooted in the extraction, monetization, and custody of attention.

14.**Don't get married to a format**

15. Be married to the problem. Not the tech or the format. The latter changes. And is a pain to build if you’re not from tech. Deeply understand the problem because you’ll spend the next few years wrestling with it.

16. Figure out the problem before you work on a format

17.**Stand out**

18. Getting the right attention matters

19. Good marketing sells once. A good product sells itself over and over again. For an early stage startup, go narrow and deep with sales — talk to a small set of users interested in what you're trying to do, not a broad audience who are indifferent

20.**Get comfortable with not knowing -- but learn how to test and corroborate**

21. Don't wait for motivation, or worse, inspiration. Create habits around testing instead.

22. There is no such thing as “launch day”. Don’t think of it as getting the product out to your user – think about this as the day you get feedback coming in.

23. Don’t wait for perfect. Perfect costs time, money, energy. Good enough is good enough to get feedback.

24. Product and business failure happens when you run out of time or money to figure out how to be valuable

25. The best predictor of success: how often you talk to users. Figure out how to operationalize that early so it’s in your DNA. Do things daily. Do things weekly. Do things monthly

26. If you’re the founder, 80% of your head needs to be on “listening” mode. The rest is acting on what you’ve heard.

27.**Bonus: Define success on your own terms -- what does success mean to you?**

28. Ask: What are the risks to failure? What do I need to do to de-risk it?

29. Momentum is oxygen. If you lose it, fear sets in.
