Fulfilling this lifelong dream at 51 wasn't just about learning to fly; it was a profound, and often humbling, lesson in how learning itself changes as we get older.

It took me nearly 40 hours of flight time – almost double what a 20-something might need. There's a slight sting in admitting that, but it speaks volumes about the journey.

I went into it thinking I'd be somewhat immune to the stress of an instructor's critique or the sheer frustration of not memorizing sequences quickly, especially while multitasking in a cockpit. I was wrong.

There were many moments I felt defeated, wondering why on earth I was tackling something this demanding – I was never math inclined, nor do I have natural hand-eye coordination.

One instructor offered a fascinating insight:

"Older guys like you," he said, "often learn by trying to understand all the connections – how the plane works, the 'why' behind everything. Younger folks? They often just do as they're told and absorb procedures directly." It made them faster. This really highlighted how our learning approaches can shift.

This experience also threw a spotlight on something crucial, often overlooked in how we teach and learn: we don't all handle stress the same way.

The pressure of mastering a risky skill isn't just about "resilience" in the traditional sense of powering through. How we react is deeply intertwined with our upbringing, our beliefs, and how we perceive judgment.

It’s not about good or bad; it’s about understanding these individual differences. This can't be ignored in any educational setting. The goal isn't just to absorb knowledge, but to find the joy and motivation in the doing, even when it's tough.

Neuroplasticity is real – our brains can keep learning, but that changes. For me, it meant more repetition, a deeper dive into understanding the 'why', and a whole lot more patience than my younger self might have had.

So take the risk. Do the hard thing on your bucket list. Especially while you still can.

How has your learning process changed as you've gotten older? What "hard things" are you planning to work on?
