**The "S" in MSL is "Sea."**

**MSL (Mean Sea Level):** This is your altitude (height) measured from the average sea level. Imagine a constant, flat baseline of the ocean. When Air Traffic Control (ATC) gives you an altitude to fly, it's almost always in MSL. Think of it as a universal reference point. Your altimeter in the aircraft, when set to the local QNH or standard pressure (1013 hPa / 29.92 inHg), will show your altitude in MSL.

**Think of the "G" in AGL as standing for "Ground."**

**AGL (Above Ground Level):** This is your height measured from the ground directly beneath you. This measurement is constantly changing as the terrain below the aircraft changes.

**Imagine you're flying over rolling hills**

**MSL:** Your height above that constant sea level remains relatively stable if you're flying at a consistent altitude.

**AGL:** Your height above the ground will change as you fly over hills (AGL decreases) and valleys (AGL increases), even if your MSL altitude stays the same.


**How this applies to sea level and circuit height**

Sea Level: When referring to the height of an airport or terrain, it's almost always given in MSL (Mean Sea Level). For example, an airport might have an elevation of 500 feet MSL. This means the ground at the airport is 500 feet above the average sea level.

Circuit Height: Circuit height is usually expressed as a height above the aerodrome (airport) elevation. This means it's a height AGL (Above Ground Level) at that specific airport. For example, a standard circuit height might be 1000 feet AGL. So, if the airport elevation is 500 feet MSL, when you are flying the circuit at 1000 feet AGL, your altimeter (which reads MSL) will show approximately 1500 feet MSL (500 feet airport elevation + 1000 feet AGL).

**Simple Reminders**

 * ATC uses MSL for altitudes.

 * Circuit heights are usually given in AGL (above the airport).

 * Sectional charts primarily use MSL for elevations and airspace boundaries (AGL values are often in parentheses).
