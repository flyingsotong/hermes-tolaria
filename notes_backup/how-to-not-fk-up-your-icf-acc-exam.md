Here is a list of what not to do in coaching, according to the ICF, summarized by Bard:

- Do not tell the client what to do or how to do it.

- Do not focus on the past, particularly the emotional past.

- Do not choose the topic for the client.

- Do not be unsupportive or disrespectful to the client.

- Do not focus on your own performance or demonstration of knowledge about the topic.

- Do not direct the conversation.

- Do not listen for the place where you can demonstrate your knowledge about the topic or tell the client what to do about the topic.

- Do not focus consistently on instructing the client or sharing your own knowledge, ideas or beliefs.

- Do not ask leading questions or contain pre-determined answers.

- Do not attend to an agenda or issues not set by the client, but set by you.

- Do not insist the client carry out specific actions prescribed by you.

- Do not suggest actions or steps to the client that do not have a clear relationship to the client’s stated agenda.

- Do not invite the client to identify or explore how the client’s learning can be applied to future actions or activities that support the client’s agenda.

In short, do not take over the coaching session and do not tell the client what to do. Instead, focus on listening to the client, helping them to explore their own thoughts and feelings, and supporting them to find their own solutions.
