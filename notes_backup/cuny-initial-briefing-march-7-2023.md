**What's the hardest part of building a media startup?**
Figuring out the problem to solve on behalf of your user. If you can't figure out a real verifiable problem to solve, you can't create engagement, you can't create value, and so you can't make money. **The Splice What's-The-Problem Canvas**
(Focus on the problem -- not the business for now)
**PERCEPTION**
What's the problem that I perceive?
How did I form that perception?
How does the problem show up? What are the symptoms? When does it happen? Where does it happen?
Who is this a problem for? Who benefits from this getting solved? Who benefits from this problem not getting solved?
What's the desired outcome?
**ASSUMPTION**
What are the 3 main assumptions am I making?
How can I find out if these 3 assumptions are indeed true?
What do I not know yet?
What questions should I be asking the user?
**ACTION**
Write the first draft of the problem statement. For [person] to achieve [need/want], she needs a way to [solution]. A potential solution could be a [product] that addresses her [need/want]. I know if I'm right if [x] happens as a result.
How will I continue to test this?
How will I gather feedback on this?
How will I implement the feedback I've gathered?

**Q\&A -- and what have you learned**
1.**Don't just build a comunity. Serve one.**
2. Relevance is in the utility of others
3. Value is in the the utility of others
4. Find out how to make 10 people happy. Then 100
5. If you don't know the first names of your audience, you don't know them
6. It's not the CMS. It's the CRM
7.**Go niche**
8. Value is in understanding what a community needs, and delivering that
9. Don't get sucked into scaling for the sake of scaling. Understanding what your user wants and delivering on that is more important than scale for the sake of scaling.
10.**Understand who you're actually competing against**
11. Obsess about being able to solve a problem for your user -- it's not about being the first to break news
12. We live in an information economy. Economies are defined by scarcity, not abundance (scarcity = value), and in an age of information abundance, what’s scarce?
13. A: Attention. The scale of the world’s largest companies, the wealth of its richest people, and the power of governments are all rooted in the extraction, monetization, and custody of attention.
14.**Don't get married to a format**
15. Be married to the problem. Not the tech or the format. The latter changes. And is a pain to build if you’re not from tech. Deeply understand the problem because you’ll spend the next few years wrestling with it.
16. Figure out the problem before you work on a format
17.**Stand out**
18. Were you at Rishad's session before this? Also see JP's work in branding
19. Getting the right attention matters
20. Good marketing sells once. A good product sells itself over and over again.
21.**Get comfortable with not knowing -- but learn how to test and corroborate**
22. Don't wait for motivation, or worse, inspiration. Create habits around testing instead.
23. There is no such thing as “launch day”. Don’t think of it as getting the product out to your user – think about this as the day you get feedback coming in.
24. Don’t wait for perfect. Perfect costs time, money, energy. Good enough is good enough to get feedback.
25. Product and business failure happens when you run out of time or money to figure out how to be valuable

28.**Bonus: Define success on your own terms -- what does success mean to you?**
