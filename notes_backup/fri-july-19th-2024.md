- From Slugs We often talk about Splice as a service — to a community, to our readers/users/subscribers, to people finding themselves in a rapidly changing media industry. But if there’s one thing that we are most obsessed with, it’s trying to truly understand what people want — and then go deeper. As coaches, we’ve learned that what people say they want isn’t always what they need. We’ve learned that serving someone means to clearly see a need and to deliver overwhelmingly against it.

We’ve been teaching this concept of hospitality for years (even though we’ve never called it that). We’ve asked media entrepreneurs over and over: What does your user want? What is her intention? How do you know if that’s true? How do you test your assumption? How do you get feedback?

It’s not about content. Hospitality in journalism means deeply serving the reader's needs and aspirations by focusing on a demand, not in your ability to create a supply of content. It's about creating a dialogue, not a monologue, where we listen intently to our audience and respond thoughtfully.

I like this definition by restaurateur Danny Meyer: “Hospitality exists when you believe the other person is on your side. The converse is just as true. Hospitality is present when something happens for you. It is absent when something happens to you.” What can you do for your audience today?

This will be a theme at Splice Beta this year. We’re in the business of making our community seen and heard. That’s a job that AI won’t take from us.
