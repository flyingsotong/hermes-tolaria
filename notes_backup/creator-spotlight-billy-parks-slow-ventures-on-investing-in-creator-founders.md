This is a conversation from the Creator Spotlight Podcast, where host Francis Zer interviews Billy Parks, a partner at Slow Ventures. They discuss the firm's $60 million creator fund, which focuses on investing in "creative CEOs"—entrepreneurs who use media to build scalable product or service businesses rather than traditional media companies.
Parks outlines their four-part investment thesis (**Creator, Community, Category, and Scale**), using their first $2 million investment in woodworking creator Jonathan Catz-Moses as a case study. The ideal creator for their fund has deep expertise in a specific niche, a passionate, cult-like following, and is building a business that can scale significantly. They are betting on the founder, not just a single business idea, by taking a small equity stake (\~10%) without seeking operational control.
*Francis Zer:** Welcome back to the Creator Spotlight podcast. My name is Francis Zer, and today we're speaking with Billy Parks, a partner at Slow Ventures. He is specifically on their creator fund, a $60 million fund launched at the start of this year to invest in creators. The fund's first investment was announced in early August, which was $2 million for Jonathan Catz-Moses, who is a woodworking creator with over 500,000 YouTube subscribers and a 7-figure woodworking product business. We spoke about the fund's four-part investment thesis, how that applies to Jonathan Katz-Moses' business, and all sorts of other things about how he thinks about investing in creators, how creators should be focusing on building non-media businesses. It's a great conversation. Enjoy.
The first thing I want to talk about to contextualize this whole conversation is the Slow Creator Fund's core thesis, which there are four parts. So there's the**creator**, the**community**, the**category**, and the**scale**. For "creator," you're looking at creators operating as community-first entrepreneurs building businesses supported by content and distribution. There's a spectrum from influencer/celebrity to entrepreneur, and you guys are working more towards the entrepreneur end.
Then there's "community," which there is a cult-like community around the creator with a specific why or reason for attachment. And again, there's a spectrum here from bonded by demographics towards bonded by psychographics. You want bonded by psychographics.
Number three, "category": niche creators who are the person in their growing high LTV (lifetime value) vertical with white space for businesses. Spectrum again: general lifestyle to niche and high value. You want niche and high value.
Finally, "scale," which is creators earning revenue from advertising, which could be platform brand deals or product sales, demonstrating the ability to convert. So the final spectrum here is from zero not monetizing to $10 million or more, and you guys are looking broadly from like the one to five million sort of range. So that's the summary of the thesis and I'll hand the mic to you.
**Billy Parks:** Yeah, well, thanks for that. I'll put it into the way I speak best, which is in the layman's. From the creator standpoint, the fund is really focused on not so much like talent or entertainment creators, although many of them have a very strong entrepreneurial streak. But when they have kind of general audiences that love them 'cause they're funny or they do great pranks, but it's not very clear that they have a niche or a voice of authority in a very specific space, those are not the ones we look for.
The ones that we look for have this kind of voice of authority in a specific space. I think of it as like they have the permission to sell a product, build a service for their audience because they are deep in the space. They know the industry, they know the white space. When they build something, you're like, "Yeah, that's for me." You know, like they have an inherent understanding of product-market fit.
There's a lot of folks with scaled audiences that are really interested in building media companies and want to launch studios and that kind of stuff. And while that's a really interesting business, it's not necessarily a business that we're focused on. We're focused on products and services and building ecosystems that can kind of exit one day.
**Francis Zer:** I think this is more and more the more I research the creator economy. The people who are truly thriving, they don't have media businesses. They have a business that started with media, maybe as a way of proving content-market fit and building an audience that they're converting into customers.
**Billy Parks:** Look, I think there's two types of businesses creators can build, and I kind of put them in a bucket of like a lifestyle business or like a scalable venture scale business. I think you can have a really good life telling stories online, doing brand deals and making hundreds of thousands, if not multiple millions of dollars a year building a media-type business. The ones that we're really interested in are the ones that are hyper-focused on taking over the world. They have like a cult-like following and they want to build really scalable big products or services for them.
And I think media businesses are hard. There's some fantastic exits and then there's a lot of exits that are a lot harder. To each their own, it's just not what we're looking to do.
**Francis Zer:** To make this concrete for people, let's talk about what you actually have invested in so far. At the beginning of August, you guys announced that you'd closed your first investment with Jonathan Katz-Moses. He had a construction company, a bartending job, and he was like, "I'm going to start making videos about my woodworking." All the way to now. I think last year he said his business made about $6 million in revenue. He has like 15 full-time employees and you guys just invested $2 million in him. Tell me how he illustrates this thesis.
**Billy Parks:** Yeah, Jonathan's a really good example of a super entrepreneurial creator. He knows the tools he needs for the job, and he started innovating on tool production and selling those, making them in a very small shop. Now he's in a 30,000 sq ft area. He's been building a relationship with an audience around his story as an entrepreneur and mostly as a woodworker. He only publishes like once a month, but there's tons of content on the site that's evergreen that anybody who wants to consider the right tool for the right job can go and watch.
He's a really good example of a community that loves him. He is in a passion niche area that's high spend. It's a passion. If you're a woodworker, you would wear a cap that says "I'm a woodworker." Categories that say, "I am this" and "this is part of my community," and you have somebody who stands out in that community—those are the great kind of people to get behind.
**Francis Zer:** When we talk about someone like Jonathan, right, where it's like, is he a startup founder or is he a creator in this media sense? It's kind of like a perfectly overlapping Venn diagram that's just a circle. My question for you is, is the term "creator" increasingly meaningless because you're looking to invest in essentially startup founders who found product-market fit through media first?
**Billy Parks:** 100%. I think your definition sits well with me. I don't think a creator necessarily needs to be monetizing. The Venn diagram that we're talking about is the one that has your creator on one side and your entrepreneur on the other side. And where they meet is where we really like them. They don't have to be monetizing outside of brand deals just yet. It's always great for them to show signal that people are willing to spend, but it's not necessary. There are creators that we're in negotiation with that have not made a dollar other than working with brands, but they have a really great idea about what they want to do, and that's fine with us too, as long as we feel like they can pull it off.
**Francis Zer:** This is the "Creative CEO," which you describe as creators leveraging media and brand to build category-specific businesses. They have 200,000 to 1 million+ followers, $500,000 to $2 million in brand partnerships and advertising, and zero to $5 million in off-platform revenue. Again, that word "CEO"—it's like you're looking for startup founders.
**Billy Parks:** Yeah. They should have a really strong relationship with a really passionate audience in a pretty specific vertical. And they should be at a place in their journey where capital helps them scale. The other important thing is we don't just want to invest in a creator with one idea, because as an entrepreneur, you got to take a lot of shots on goal. We really want them to try a bunch of stuff, and that's why the capital can be really nice. We didn't just invest in Jonathan's tool business; we invested in Jonathan. We invest in them directly with the idea that they can try multiple businesses.
**Francis Zer:** I understand that he had had similar conversations before that always fell apart because whoever he was talking to wanted some control of the business, and he did not want to give that up. I understand that Slow has zero control of the business, which seems pretty rare. Tell me why he wanted to accept this capital from you and what it means for you guys to have zero control.
**Billy Parks:** Yeah, our job is to match capital with really great founders that are also creators. We're betting on them. We don't sit on their boards and approve budgets. Listen, Jonathan can call me and say, "Hey, man, let's go get a burger and talk about content and creative strategy," and I'll be there. But our job is to really find the best people that can run it themselves. That's why we don't take control. Our work is done, so to speak, when we pick the right person.
**Francis Zer:** I understand that it's intended to be for about 5 to 10% ownership of the creator's company. How does this compare to more traditional startup investing?
**Billy Parks:** Around 10%, plus or minus. It's pretty similar. The mapping of the deals and the equity stakes are very much in line with a $65 million seed-stage or early-stage venture fund.
**Francis Zer:** Are there people that you've been talking to that have said no to investment? Tell me the sort of reasons that people would not want to take this investment.
**Billy Parks:** When the deals fall apart, they're generally on terms. Like, valuations. It's really hard to justify some valuations, especially in creator businesses because a lot of their revenue so far has been brand-supported. And while making $2 million a year from brands is a huge, high-margin accomplishment, it's a hard thing to think of as an exitable business. If you stop doing it, it's over. That's "keyman risk."
Sometimes they say, "I just want you to invest in my new business, not in me holistically as a creator." For us, we're actually betting on them and their audience and their ability to convert and try new things.
**Francis Zer:** This gets at a conflict in the creator economy where people grow based on their personality but then want to step back and have the business not be so much about them.
**Billy Parks:** I totally hear where their anxiety comes from, and it's a good place. At some point, they don't want to be in front of the camera. Building a business is the best way to do that. Look at Doug DeMuro, an auto influencer who built an auto auction platform called Cars & Bids. He knew he was an expert, he saw the white space, and he also knew he didn't want to make three videos a week for the next 20 years. That's a great instinct. We bet on Doug. People come to Cars & Bids, and many of them know who Doug is, and many of them don't. Your content gets the business from zero to ten, and then it can grow beyond you. It doesn't have to all be about you. You just get to build the business because you know the audience and the space.
**Francis Zer:** So what are some of the big red flags you're looking for that might be different than more traditional startup investments?
**Billy Parks:** I don't know that they would be different. Red flags we look for are: you've got a big audience and great ideas, but are you really hungry and 100% focused on building this? If it takes a long time for them to get the deal together, you can tell. If they aren't pushing through this deal, are they really going to push through on this business? Another flag is if you can't just pick up the phone and call the creator. If they have layers of lawyers and agents that gatekeep, that's unhealthy for an entrepreneur. We need to make sure they're running the show.
**Francis Zer:** I want to talk about VidCon. You mentioned a lot of the people you're investing in are not necessarily going to VidCon, implying that there's less of a need to go there anymore because skills like optimizing thumbnails and titles are more democratized. The creator economy has widened.
**Billy Parks:** First of all, I think VidCon is rad and serves a great purpose. The industry track has done a great job of teaching brands how to work with creators. The place where I see an opportunity for VidCon to catch up is having more focus on creators that are building non-media businesses. The creators need education about that as well. Jonathan Katz-Moses, you ask him about VidCon, he kind of doesn't really know what it is. He's not there to learn about thumbnails.
There's a real opportunity to have more information for creators like they would get in business school—how to hire a CEO, how to build a supply chain, how to build a technical product. A lot of creators need to be doing the same kind of education that entrepreneurs are doing to be entrepreneurs. This new class of creator is thinking like that, and those are the ones we're looking for.
**Francis Zer:** This reminds me of a quote about the type of creator Medium wanted to attract. It wasn't the person who is a "creator qua creator," just making media for the sake of it. It was the expert—like the expert woodworker writing about things he's learned in his spare time. The content business isn't the full business; it's a steam valve for things he's working through.
**Billy Parks:** Yeah, it comes back to this voice of authority and area of expertise. If all you're doing is communicating an idea, where are you getting the idea if you haven't spent time working in that field? From your work life, from your experience set, then you start to create content that puts your voice of authority out in the world. And then you can figure out how to turn it into a business. Jocko Willink was a Navy SEAL. He talks about leadership, jiu-jitsu, fitness, discipline. Then he turned that into leadership courses, then boots and tactical apparel, and then an energy drink. That goes back to that "shots on goal" idea.
**Francis Zer:** It's the classic advice for being a writer: to be a great writer, live a great life first. It's the same with this.
**Billy Parks:** Yeah. There is entertainment on the internet, and there's also a ton of learning. When it comes to these learning platforms, you learn and then you buy. If you are learning how to quilt from the Missouri Star Quilt Company, then you go to her store and you buy all the materials because you want to support your creator. The learn-to-buy thing is really important.
**Francis Zer:** And this goes back to the thesis at the beginning. You're looking for somebody who's creating that connection. I think we'll end it right there. Billy, thank you for coming on.
