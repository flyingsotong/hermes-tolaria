# Deskilling is real, but it is conditional — Shangyuan Wu's study on what journalists are losing and gaining

I was reading the NiemanLab writeup of Shangyuan Wu's new study on journalistic deskilling when I caught myself doing the thing I always tell people not to do — reading the secondary source first.

The NiemanLab piece was fine. It hit the headline findings: journalists are worried AI is eroding research skills, writing skills, interviewing skills, the ability to think for themselves. One interviewee said AI tools were "dumbing down the profession." That's the hook, and NiemanLab took it.

But the actual paper — "Is Artificial Intelligence Causing Journalists to 'Deskill'?," published in Journalism Practice in June — is doing something more interesting than the headline suggests. Wu interviewed 14 journalists and editors in Singapore, a market where AI adoption is actively encouraged by government policy. The findings map almost perfectly onto the argument I've been making in these pages for the last year.

**DESKILLING IS REAL, BUT IT IS CONDITIONAL**

The participants named six areas of skill erosion: research (journalists stop tracking down primary sources), writing ("if the app crashes, they might say they can't produce a script on their own"), note-taking (AI transcribes everything, but writing by hand is how you remember), analytical skills (asking ChatGPT for the top five trends means you never develop your own instinct for what matters), interviewing (questions the machine wrote for you aren't questions you own), and — underneath all of it — independent thinking.

That last one is the quote that stopped me. Participant I put it this way: "If you get the machine to do all that for you, then you don't think anymore."

But here's the part the NiemanLab piece mostly glides over. Not all the participants were equally concerned. Several pushed back, pointing out that AI transcription frees you to actually listen during interviews, that research assistance is not fundamentally different from a search engine. The paper's real insight is conditional: skills erode when you outsource judgment, not when you outsource labor. Transcribing an interview is fine. Asking the model to decide what the story is, is not.

This distinction matters because it determines the response. If deskilling is inevitable, the only move is to resist the technology. If it is conditional — if it depends on how deeply you let AI into your cognitive loop — then the move is to build better boundaries around your own practice.

**The upskilling catalog**

Wu's participants named 11 areas where journalists need to grow. Some are familiar: fact-checking and discernment, editing, interviewing, storytelling. Others are genuinely new to the job description: stylistic writing with actual voice (not just clean prose but signature), personal branding, prompt-writing as a craft, mastery of the technology — knowing which tools are out there and how one is better than another for different functions — ethical use of AI, which one participant described as not passing off what AI has written as your own work, and, above all, the ability to come up with original ideas. "AI can give you what is out there," one participant said, "but it cannot provide that innovation or creativity."

The paper argues that "journalism is more than an accumulation of technical skills; it should be accompanied by a moral compass which journalists must not lose." This is not the kind of thing that fits neatly into a newsroom training budget. You can teach someone to write a better prompt, but you cannot teach them to care about the consequences — which is what Participant E named as the actual skill: "a stronger sense of what is the right thing to do."

A disposition like that is the one thing a language model cannot generate.

**The framework gap**

What makes this study useful is the line it draws between two modes of AI use: instrumental (where AI is a tool you direct and check) and substitutional (where AI replaces the cognitive work you used to do). The participants' anxiety clustered entirely in the substitutional mode.

This is the same line I've been drawing in Slugs — between production that is cheap and judgment that is scarce. The study gives it an empirical basis that my weekly rants don't have.

Here's the uncomfortable question Wu's paper leaves for any journalist or editor reading this: **If the app goes down tomorrow, can you still do the work yourself?**

If the answer is no, you already know what to do next.
