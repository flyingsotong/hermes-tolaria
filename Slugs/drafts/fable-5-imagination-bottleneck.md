Hi FNAME,

I've been watching my timeline dissect Fable 5's pricing all week. The spreadsheet crowd has run the benchmarks: $9 here, 40 minutes there, quality parity with the $1 model on standard tasks. The conclusion is already hardening into conventional wisdom — route your known work to cheap models, save the frontier for… well, nobody's quite sure what.

The pricing debate is real. It's also the wrong conversation.

**YOUR IMAGINATION IS THE BOTTLENECK**

Software engineering already ran this experiment. On ordinary dev tasks — implement this feature, build that thing — the $1 model ties the $9 model. Same quality, same output, nine times cheaper. So yes, route your routine work to cheap models. That part is settled.

But the second test is the one that matters. Hand the frontier model a problem nobody thought to assign — something that wasn't on any backlog, something an engineer had to suspect was newly possible and spend money just to find out. That's where the cheap models fall away entirely. Two hours and $40 later, you get a result the engineer couldn't have produced on their own. The model didn't discover the task. Someone imagined it into existence.

AI can execute anything you ask of it. It cannot decide what's worth asking.

This is where the news industry — our industry — should be paying attention, and mostly isn't. We've spent two years bolting AI onto the old layout. Summarize this press release. Transcribe that hearing. Generate five headline variants. Draft the social copy. All of it is faster and cheaper than it was, and all of it is work everyone already knows how to ask for. When you and every other newsroom run the same tasks through the same models, the output converges. The tools are doing exactly what they were asked to do. The sameness is coming from the questions, not the answers.

The real unlock comes when you, as an operator who actually understands where the bodies are buried in your beat, break free and use these models in ways they've never been trained on — because the thing you're asking for didn't exist as a documented use case. You imagined it.

At a time of falling AI prices, this is where the value chain shifts. Upward. The cheaper execution gets, the more commoditized it becomes, and the more valuable every frontier-prompted question becomes. Your competitive advantage was never going to be running the same summarization workflow as everyone else at a tenth of a cent less per token.

So here's the test. If you had $40 to blow on a single frontier model task — one shot at a question nobody in your newsroom has asked yet — what would you spend it on?

Don't say summarizing parliamentary proceedings.

— Alan Soon
