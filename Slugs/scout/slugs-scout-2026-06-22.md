## Candidate 1: Meta internally exposed employee tracking data for AI training

- **Moment:** June 22 — Meta's internal employee-tracking program, designed to collect keystroke, mouse, and screen data for training AI agents, was accidentally exposed internally. Full prompts, private conversations between employees, and compensation data were leaked across the company.

- **Primary source:** Wired — https://www.wired.com/story/meta-accidentally-let-employees-access-each-others-keystroke-data/ (verified 200)
- **Secondary source:** Business Insider — https://www.businessinsider.com/meta-ai-training-data-leak-exposed-employee-activity-across-company-2026-6 (verified 200)

- **Rupture:** The company pushing most aggressively to "train on everything" just demonstrated what everything really means — your own employer's surveillance apparatus, turned inward. The privacy boundary isn't outside the company walls; it doesn't exist inside them either. And the leak proves the system broke internally before it ever reached production.

- **Theme:** Platform power / AI and media paradigm shift

- **Angle for Alan:** This is the logical endpoint of the "data is the new oil" framing. If Meta (which has more data than almost any publisher) can't contain its own training pipeline, what chance do publishers have of protecting theirs? The rupture for media: the same AI models publishers are licensing or building on are being trained on employee surveillance data. The boundary between "work product" and "training data" doesn't exist.

- **Quotes from source (Wired):**
  - "Employees had previously raised concerns about the initiative, which involves collecting workers' keystroke data to train AI models."
  - "The exposure included full prompts, private conversations, and in some cases made employees' compensation data visible to colleagues."

- **Alan signal needed:** Alan reading the Wired piece at breakfast, or a conversation with a founder about what data their AI vendor is actually training on. A coaching client who just signed a deal with an AI platform and is now asking about data provenance.

- **Closing provocation:** "If Meta can't keep its training data contained, why would you trust any platform with yours?"

## Candidate 4: Sakana AI launches Fugu — multi-agent orchestration as a product

- **Moment:** June 22 — Sakana AI launches Fugu, a multi-agent orchestration system accessible through a single model API. Fugu Ultra matches Fable and Mythos on benchmarks by coordinating multiple smaller models rather than running one large one.

- **Primary source:** VentureBeat — https://venturebeat.com/orchestration/no-claude-fable-5-no-problem-sakana-achieves-frontier-performance-with-new-fugu-multi-model-auto-synthesis-system (verified 429/rate limit, confirmed via HN)
- **Secondary source:** Sakana AI — https://sakana.ai/fugu-beta/ (verified 200)
- **HN item:** Nikkei Asia — https://asia.nikkei.com/business/technology/artificial-intelligence/japan-s-sakana-fugu-multiagent-a (verified 200)

- **Rupture:** The "best model" race is ending. Fugu achieves frontier performance not through a better single model but through orchestration — multiple smaller agents working together. For media, this means the AI product question shifts from "which model" to "how many agents and how do they talk to each other." The moat isn't the model; it's the architecture.

- **Theme:** AI and media paradigm shift

- **Angle for Alan:** This maps directly to Alan's "production is free" thesis. When multi-agent orchestration is a single API call, the scarce work shifts again — from prompt engineering to agent topology design. For newsrooms, the question isn't "can AI write articles?" but "how do you design a system where five specialized agents (researcher, writer, editor, fact-checker, publisher) collaborate better than one monolithic model?"

- **Alan signal needed:** Alan building FlyCheck or Magenta and noticing that his own workflow is already multi-agent (separate tools for different tasks, not one LLM). Or a coaching conversation where a founder describes their "AI stack" and Alan realizes they're describing agent orchestration without knowing it.

- **Closing provocation:** "When frontier performance comes from orchestration, not scale, what's your architecture?"
