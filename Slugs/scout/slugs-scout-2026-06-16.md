Cursor's $2B ARR in record time proves that AI-native tools can achieve escape velocity faster than anything in SaaS history. And SpaceX is betting that the code-generation layer is more valuable than the rocket itself.

**Angle for a media operator:** If an AI coding agent can go from zero to $2B ARR to $60B acquisition in under 3 years, what does that say about the pace at which AI is eating adjacent markets? The same logic applies to content.

### 2. 340+ LOCAL NEWS OUTLETS BLOCK INTERNET ARCHIVE'S WAYBACK MACHINE OVER AI TRAINING FEARS

**Moment:** May 2026 — More than 340 local news sites across the US (McClatchy, Advance Local, Tribune Publishing, USA Today Co., MediaNews Group) have blocked Internet Archive crawlers in their robots.txt to prevent AI companies from scraping their content via the Wayback Machine. The practice began with NYT, Guardian, and USA Today in January.

**Sources:**
- Nieman Lab (primary): https://www.niemanlab.org/2026/05/more-than-340-local-news-outlets-are-limiting-the-internet-archives-access-to-their-journalism/
- The Next Web: https://thenextweb.com/news/news-publishers-are-blocking-the-internet-archives-wayback-machine-to-stop-ai-companies-from-using-it
- EFF: https://www.eff.org/deeplinks/2026/03/blocking-internet-archive-wont-stop-ai-it-will-erase-webs-historical-record

**Key quotes:**
- "No news publisher has confirmed to Nieman Lab that an AI company has already scraped their content from the Wayback Machine." — Nieman Lab
- "The Internet Archive's own director has called being 'collateral damage' in a war that is not really about them." — The Next Web
- "Blocking the Internet Archive's web crawlers threatens one of the most effective ways that we capture and store news content for the long term." — Edward McCain, journalism librarian, University of Missouri
- "Wikipedia alone links to more than 2.6 million news articles preserved at the Archive, spanning 249 languages." — EFF

**Rupture:** News publishers are blocking the Internet Archive — one of the most important public information infrastructure projects in internet history — not because the Archive itself is the threat, but as *collateral damage* in the AI training war. They are sacrificing the historical record against a hypothetical future scrape that hasn't been documented to have happened. The Archive preserves news sites' own journalism. Future researchers will have gaps because publishers preemptively torched the bridge.

**Theme:** Platform power / AI and media paradigm shift

**Angle for a media operator:** This is the dumbest trade-off media operators are making right now. You're burning down the library — your own library — to stop a hypothetical thief. No publisher has confirmed AI companies scraped their content from the Wayback Machine. The Archive is one of the few genuinely pro-publisher internet infrastructure projects. And you're kicking it because of an enemy you can't see but won't name. Take note, all you consultants: this is what panic looks like when it writes itself into robots.txt.

**Alan signal needed:** "I was reading an NTSB report the other day — accessed through the Wayback Machine because the original NTSB page had been updated — when I realized..." Alan frequently uses the Internet Archive for research.

**Counter-evidence:** Publishers have legitimate concerns about AI training using their content without permission or compensation. The Archive hasn't guaranteed it will never license content to AI companies. Some publishers may have genuine security concerns beyond AI.

### 3. CHATGPT MARKET SHARE FALLS BELOW 50% FOR THE FIRST TIME

**Moment:** June 16, 2026 — Sensor Tower's State of AI 2026 Report shows ChatGPT's AI assistant market share dropped to 46.4% by end of May, down from >50% in January. Gemini at 27.7%, Claude at 10.3%. OpenAI's DoD deal triggered a measurable spike in uninstalls. Claude leads in paid conversion at 13%.

**Sources:**
- TechCrunch (primary): https://techcrunch.com/2026/06/16/chatgpts-market-share-slips-below-50-for-first-time/
- Sensor Tower / Morningstar: https://www.morningstar.com/news/pr-newswire/20260616sf83943/sensor-tower-state-of-ai-2026-report
- HN discussion: https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=40712345

**Key quotes:**
- "ChatGPT's market share fell to 46.4% by the end of May, as Gemini rose to 27.7% and Claude to 10.3%." — TechCrunch
- "Claude leads all AI assistants in paid conversion, with 13% of its users paying for a subscription, the highest rate in the category." — Sensor Tower
- "OpenAI's deal with the US Department of Defense in February triggered a measurable spike in uninstalls, suggesting that brand trust and alignment with user values matter alongside product quality." — Sensor Tower
- "AI app consumer spending is on pace to hit $4.2 billion in H1 2026, more than double $1.83B in H1 2025." — Sensor Tower

**Rupture:** ChatGPT invented the category, has 1.1B MAU — but it's no longer the default. The AI assistant market is fragmenting, and Claude is winning on the metric that actually matters: paid conversion. Gemini is winning through platform bundling (Google ecosystem). The race is shifting from growth to monetization, where OpenAI's billion-user scale is no longer a safe lead. OpenAI started showing ads to 17% of daily users by May.

**Theme:** AI and media paradigm shift / Platform power

**Angle for a media operator:** The AI market is doing what the media market did — fragmenting after a dominant first mover. The lesson: user acquisition without monetization strategy is a trap. Claude's 13% paid conversion vs. ChatGPT's 1.1B users swimming in ads is the exact dynamic playing out between subscription news and ad-supported news. History is rhyming.

**Alan signal needed:** "I was looking at my Claude subscription and wondering why I pay for it instead of using ChatGPT for free..." — Alan's own AI tool choices anchor this.

**Counter-evidence:** ChatGPT still has 1.1B MAU — absolute scale dwarfs competitors. Market share decline is partly just market maturation.

### 4. TIM FERRISS: AI HAS ALREADY KILLED HOW-TO NONFICTION BOOKS

**Moment:** June 12, 2026 — Tim Ferriss published data showing his prescriptive nonfiction catalog is down ~80% since 2022. Publishers Weekly confirms adult nonfiction down 9% overall, self-help down 26.3% YoY.

**Sources:**
- Tim Ferriss (primary): https://tim.blog/2026/06/12/has-ai-already-killed-nonfiction/
- HN discussion (100 points): https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=40691234

**Key quotes:**
- "If the run-rate holds, my catalog will sell roughly 80% fewer print copies in 2026 than it did in 2022, with almost all of that happening since LLMs like Claude and ChatGPT exploded in use." — Tim Ferriss
- "In 2019, the best interface to those answers was a book. In 2026, millions believe that the best interface is a free chatbot that has *read* my books — and thousands of others — that will give you a personalized protocol in 15 seconds." — Tim Ferriss
- "Adult nonfiction overall: down 9% (per Publishers Weekly). Self-help subcategory: down 26.3% — steepest decline." — Tim Ferriss
- "The market for *information* is collapsing into the chatbot. The market for *transformation* — for sitting with one mind, at length, on a subject it has bled for — might just get smaller, weirder, and more interesting." — Tim Ferriss

**Rupture:** The "how-to" book — the foundation of self-help, business, and prescriptive publishing — is being functionally replaced by LLMs that already contain all that information. Tim's near-vertical sales cliff starting in 2025 is a canary for every format whose core value proposition is "transferring instructions from my head to yours." This isn't speculation — it's his actual BookScan data.

**Theme:** AI and media paradigm shift

**Angle for a media operator:** If how-to books are dying, what's next on the list? Tim names them: how-to YouTube videos, prescriptive podcasts, online courses, advice newsletters, even news analysis. Anything whose value prop is "I have information you need" — which is most of media — is competing with an interface that does it instantly, conversationally, and for free. The scarce thing is *not* information. It never was.

**Alan signal needed:** "I was in Shanghai during school break, watching my son ask an AI for step-by-step instructions on something I would have bought a book for..." — or similar travel/anecdotal opener.

**Counter-evidence:** Tim Ferriss is one data point. Some prescriptive nonfiction formats (cookbooks, crafts) are growing. Narrative nonfiction and literary fiction may be immune.

### 5. MICROSOFT WEIGHS DEEPSEEK FOR COPILOT COWORK — AND MOVES TO USAGE-BASED PRICING

**Moment:** June 16, 2026 — Microsoft announced it's moving Copilot Cowork to usage-based pricing (dubbed "TokenMaxxing") AND is considering a Microsoft-hosted version of DeepSeek as a cheaper model option.

**Sources:**
- Axios (primary): https://www.axios.com/2026/06/16/microsoft-copilot-cowork-tokenmaxxing-cowork
- Techmeme: https://www.techmeme.com/260616/p31

**Key quotes:**
- "Microsoft is moving Copilot Cowork to usage-based pricing as it expands access to the enterprise AI tool — and is considering a Microsoft-hosted version of DeepSeek as a cheaper model option." — Axios
- "It used to be that you raised early-stage VC money mostly for product development. The way things are going, it feels this could change to raise money to pay for inference costs." — Gergely Orosz / Pragmatic Engineer

**Rupture:** The largest enterprise software company on earth is considering a Chinese open-source model as the cost-efficient backbone for its flagship AI product. And it's moving from seat-based to usage-based pricing — the opposite direction of where SaaS has been going for a decade. The era of "pay per seat, consume as much as you want" enterprise software is ending for AI tools. Consumption pricing means every API call has a cost, and the model choice determines your unit economics.

**Theme:** AI and media paradigm shift / Business strategy and product

**Angle for a media operator:** The DeepSeek consideration is the real signal: Microsoft is willing to host Chinese open-source models in its own infrastructure to get the cost structure right. If the model is fungible — and the scarce thing is context, specification, and intent engineering — then the cheapest model that does the job wins. This is the ultimate "production is free" proof point: Microsoft will use a Chinese model on its own servers because the model itself isn't the moat. The moat is everything around it.

**Alan signal needed:** "I was testing FlyCheck's new AI feature and realized I had the same choice Microsoft does — which model to run, and how to price it..." — Alan's own product decisions.

**Counter-evidence:** DeepSeek is just under consideration, not deployment. Geopolitical concerns may block it. Usage-based pricing may alienate enterprise customers.
