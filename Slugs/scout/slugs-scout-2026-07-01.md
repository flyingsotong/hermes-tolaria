## 1. Valnet Moves Journalists to Pay-Per-Click — Journalism Reduced to Traffic Arbitrage

**Date:** June 29, 2026
**Source:** [Press Gazette](https://pressgazette.co.uk/news/confusion-and-anger-as-publisher-moves-journalists-to-pay-per-click-contracts/)

**The moment:** Valnet — a "digital media investment company" that owns Polygon, GameRant, Screenrant, Movieweb, and 24 other brands reaching 300M monthly visitors — issued new "Pay Per Session" contracts to 30 freelance writers at The Gamer. Writers are now paid $8 per 1,000 clicks, with no base pay. Payout is capped at 15 days — so evergreen guide content that generates clicks over months stops paying after two weeks. Editors get $3 per 1,000 clicks. The contracts were sent from Valnet's Canada HQ at 11pm UK time without warning to staff, including the editor-in-chief.

**Key quotes:**

> "The swiftness and brutality of this one felt different. It felt like a considered hostile move, so unworkable for most people that it could have only been designed to reduce headcount." — Lex Luddy, former The Gamer editor

> "Capping the payout at 15 days is clearly a kneecapping of the rewards that Valnet claims this new system will motivate." — Lex Luddy

> "Even in a space that has seen a near-endless parade of layoffs and site closures in the last two years, this particular news brought out a level of open vitriol and bad mouthing towards Valnet that you rarely see in a business where finding work is so hard." — Lex Luddy

> "If the plan is to charge by the view, what's to stop a writer bombarding their articles with bot traffic or spamming views via Reddit or X? Maybe they're just banking on being the only game in town hiring junior writers." — Art Anthony, former The Gamer freelance

> "They expect freelances to work like salaried staff." — anonymous freelance

> Pay is so low that Valnet "now attracts writers from countries where the dollar goes further — with writers in India and Africa working for Valnet sites." — Art Anthony

**The rupture:** This is not a one-off bad employer. This is the logical endpoint of ad-supported digital media taken to its terminal conclusion. When the only metric that matters is traffic, and traffic can be manufactured, there is no floor. Valnet's founders made their initial fortune through online pornography (Brazzers, linked to Pornhub). They now run 27 "media brands" — and the calculus is pure: content is a cost center, writers are fulfillment, and if $8/CPM freelancers from low-wage economies can generate enough clicks, the model works. The question for Slugs readers: is this the inevitable destination for all ad-supported journalism, or is Valnet an outlier? And if it's the destination — what's the alternative?

**Theme:** Business strategy and product

**Angle:** The funnel is dying because intent forms inside the agent, not on the publisher's site. But Valnet is running the old funnel at maximum extraction — and the workers inside it are now being paid per drop. What happens when publishers realize the only way to survive the old model is to eat their own?

**Alan signal needed:** A conversation with a media CEO wrestling with writer compensation in the current environment. Or Alan's own experience managing contributors at Splice — the tension between paying fairly and making the numbers work.

**Counter-evidence:** The Gamer's traffic (9M monthly visits, 1.8 pages/visit) suggests some demand exists. The Times' Discover traffic is up a third year-on-year. Not all publishers are collapsing — but the ones running pure ad arbitrage are racing to the bottom fastest.

**Provocation (closing line candidate):** "The question isn't whether $8 per thousand clicks is fair. The question is whether the model that produces that number can produce journalism at all."

## 2. Time Rebuilds Its Website for AI Agents, Not Humans

**Date:** July 1, 2026
**Source:** [Digiday](https://digiday.com/media/how-time-and-others-are-rebuilding-parts-of-the-web-for-ai-agents/)

**The moment:** Time is converting ALL its webpages from HTML into markdown — a simplified format designed for AI agents, not human readers. The bots get stripped-down content and metadata; humans get the full page experience. "We're separating out that traffic," says COO Mark Howard. Time uses TollBit to do the conversion, which claims a 90% reduction in tokens. The Economist is doing a narrower version — agent-readable marketing copy outside its paywall. A third major publisher is testing WebMCP, a Google/Microsoft standard for websites to communicate directly with AI agents.

**Key quotes:**

> "[The bots are] just getting the content itself and the metadata, but they're not getting the full page experience, and we're routing all the humans to the full page experience. So we're separating out that traffic." — Mark Howard, COO, Time

> "Now we're starting to think about, as the volume of bot traffic continues to increase significantly — and we see through a number of our vendor partners that we have very high domain authority with AI bot traffic — there's value in that." — Mark Howard

> "If you believe there's a value to being discovered by these bots and agents, then you should build them. If you don't believe [that], I would ask, why would you build them?" — Scott Messer, principal, Messer Media

> "There's a philosophical debate around: is that a visit? Is that valid traffic? Did a human see it? Can a bot be influenced by ads? I don't know, probably not." — Scott Messer

> "AI can comprehend more of your article because they're not spending money parsing out other HTML that's on the page. We see, on average, a 90% reduction in tokens." — Toshit Panigrahi, CEO, TollBit

**The rupture:** The most prestigious magazine brand in America is now building a parallel web — one URL, two versions, one for humans and one for machines. This is not a sci-fi prediction. It's happening today at Time. The strategic bet: being cited by AI agents is worth the cost of building and maintaining a separate infrastructure. But as Scott Messer points out, "if there is no click, no ad impression and no check, the build is pure cost." The web is forking — and nobody knows whether the agent fork will ever pay. The publishers building for it are betting on visibility and citation as the new currency. But visibility in an AI answer doesn't pay the newsroom.

**Theme:** AI and the media paradigm shift

**Angle:** We're past the "should we let AI scrape us" debate. Time has answered: yes, but on our terms, in our format. The next question is whether those terms produce revenue — or just relevance.

**Alan signal needed:** Alan building FlyCheck and Magenta with AI tools — his own lived experience of building products in an AI-mediated world. He knows what it means to architect for machines vs. humans. Also: checking his own sites' bot traffic. How much of Magenta's traffic is human?

**Counter-evidence:** The Economist is being far more cautious — starting only with marketing content outside the paywall. Hard-paywalled publishers face a fundamentally different calculus. And the consultant Messer's pushback is significant — if there's no revenue, this is pure cost dressed as strategy.

**Provocation (closing line candidate):** "The web is forking into two internets. One pays. One doesn't. Time is betting on both. The question is whether the one that doesn't pay eventually starves the one that does."

## 3. Google Says "New Value Exchange" — While Replacing Publisher Links With AI Summaries

**Date:** June 29, 2026
**Sources:** [Press Gazette — Google value exchange](https://pressgazette.co.uk/platforms/google-accepts-need-for-new-value-exchange-with-publishers/) | [Press Gazette — Google Discover](https://pressgazette.co.uk/platforms/google-ai-changes-could-deal-further-blow-to-publisher-discover-traffic/)

**The moment:** In a single week, Google did two things. (1) Published a policy paper — "A Pragmatic Approach to AI Governance in America" — saying it's "piloting novel ways to partner with websites whose content meaningfully contributes to the freshness and factuality of generative AI responses" and proposes FARO (Frontier AI Regulatory Organization). (2) Started replacing publisher links in its Discover feed with AI-written summaries that bunch 14 publishers under a single summary where you can only click through to the main one.

**Key quotes from the policy paper:**

> "Google is exploring new types of partnership and value-exchange models. For instance, we are piloting novel ways to partner with websites whose content meaningfully contributes to the freshness and factuality of generative AI responses through the process of grounding."

> "While this use is legally protected, responsible model developers should give website owners choice and control over whether their content is used in model development via simple, machine-readable robots.txt tags."

> "Collaborations with publishers of 'specialised, non-public' content are the ones most likely to be sustainable for developers and meaningful for the ecosystem." — i.e., public journalism is not where the money flows.

**Key quotes from Discover coverage:**

> "A load of publishers all got bunched together, and what I noticed was that loads of publishers that were grouped together actually covered different angles... the AI summary that Google used when it was grouping all these publishers together wasn't even relevant to all of the stories that were being listed." — Nicola Agius, Reach SEO and Discover director

> "Google's making it really difficult, especially for publishers." — Lily Ray, VP SEO strategy, Amsive

> YouTube has jumped "off the chart" in search visibility. "One thing they're doing in Discover is they not only group all the brand icons together as citations, but then when you click on the brand citation, it often takes you to the brand's Youtube account." — Lily Ray

> Social media now makes up "almost a quarter" of the Discover feed. "More publishers than ever getting into Discover" — about 64% more than last year — competing for a shrinking pie.

**The rupture:** The contradiction is the story. Google's policy paper performs concern — "new value exchange," "partnership," "choice and control" — while its product teams are actively removing the click-through that makes publisher content valuable. The partnerships Google talks about are explicitly for "specialised, non-public content" — licensed databases, educational materials. Public journalism? Still free for the taking. Robots.txt is the "control" Google offers — an opt-out from a system that will take your content by default. The UK regulator already told Google to give publishers control over how their content appears in AI answers. Google's response: a policy paper that says "trust us, we're piloting things."

**Theme:** Platform power

**Angle:** Google is running two plays at once — the policy play (we believe in partnerships) and the product play (we're replacing your links with summaries). The gap between them is where publishers live. Alan's aviation analogy: it's like ATC telling pilots "we value your flight plans" while removing the runways.

**Alan signal needed:** Alan checking Magenta's Discover traffic, noticing the drop. Or reading the Google policy paper on the same day the Discover changes hit his feed. Or a conversation with an SEO lead at a publisher who's seen the data.

**Counter-evidence:** The Times reports Discover traffic "up by more than a third year on year" — but their focus is on hard news, not lifestyle content, and they treat Discover as "bonus traffic" that doesn't convert to subscribers. Different publishers are having different experiences. The Preferred Sources feature (users pick favorite sources) could be genuinely helpful — Google says people are "twice as likely to click through to a Preferred Source."

**Provocation (closing line candidate):** "Google says it wants a 'new value exchange.' The old one was: we write, you link, we both make money. The new one is: we write, you summarize, we hope you remember to credit us."

## 4. OpenAI Builds the Full Ad Stack — Image, Video, Conversational Ads

**Date:** July 1, 2026
**Source:** [Digiday](https://digiday.com/marketing/openai-looks-beyond-a-single-ad-format-with-image-video-and-conversational-ads-in-the-works/)

**The moment:** Three OpenAI job listings reveal text, image, video, native, conversational, and interactive ad formats in development. Engineering roles paying $230K-$385K. Privacy and safety "baked into" the engineering — "policy-aware UX patterns," "format validation" at build time. The current ad format (launched 4 months ago) is simple — headline, image, link. Now OpenAI is building the full stack.

**Key quotes:**

> "The roles they are recruiting for need to tackle the thorny questions of the marketing ecosystem: attribution, brand safety and device modelling. Setting this up is not going to be easy. This is a different type of situation because no one knows what the right way to run ads in OpenAI is right now." — Rob Webster, CEO, TAU Marketing Solutions

> "The real challenge is what advertising looks like in an advice system. How do you reconcile what we call the dual-alignment problem? Are you optimizing for user trust or advertiser value? Those objectives are often incompatible. Solving that problem — if it's solvable at all — will take more than innovation around ad formats." — Andrew Frank, Gartner analyst

> "So far [OpenAI] has just been plowing ahead with a global launch of the one single format and placement they've ever tested, without any idea if that actually works best for advertisers or for users. The reality is, different types of ads will perform better for different audiences, different advertiser goals and different types of conversations. It's vital that they test and learn." — Nate Elliott, eMarketer

**The rupture:** OpenAI — the company that positioned itself as the anti-ad AI lab — is now building the most sophisticated ad stack in the industry, inside the very interface where user intent forms. The "dual-alignment problem" Gartner identifies is the core rupture: a chatbot that gives you advice while showing you ads for products related to that advice is a fundamentally different trust proposition than search ads. The user doesn't know where the advice ends and the ad begins. And OpenAI is hiring engineers to solve this at the code level — "policy-aware UX patterns" is corporate-speak for "make the ads not feel like ads."

**Theme:** Platform power

**Angle:** The AI platform is becoming the ad platform. The interface where intent forms — the chat window — is now the ad surface. For publishers, this is a competitor they cannot match: OpenAI has the user's attention, the context, and now the ad inventory. The question isn't whether ChatGPT will run ads. It's whether anyone else's ads will matter once ChatGPT's are native to the conversation.

**Alan signal needed:** Alan testing ChatGPT's existing ads — noticing when the conversational trust breaks. Has he felt the shift from "assistant" to "ad platform"? Or: a conversation with a brand or publisher who's already buying OpenAI ads and can't tell if they're working.

**Counter-evidence:** OpenAI has been explicit that trust is the constraint — "the product only works if people don't feel like they're being sold to mid-conversation." The fact that privacy/safety engineers are being hired alongside ad engineers suggests they know the risk. But "baking in guardrails at the engineering stage" is an aspiration, not a guarantee. Facebook said the same thing.

**Provocation (closing line candidate):** "The AI that gives you advice is about to start selling you things. The question isn't whether that breaks trust. It's whether anyone will notice the difference."

## 5. Spurious DMCA Claims Weaponized to Silence Journalism About AI Content Farms

**Date:** Late June 2026
**Source:** [Press Gazette](https://pressgazette.co.uk/news/spurious-copyright-claim-sees-second-press-gazette-story-removed-from-google-search/)

**The moment:** Anonymous entity "DRF Corp" filed a DMCA takedown against Press Gazette's reporting on Clickout Media — a UK company that bought three reputable sports news websites and introduced AI-generated reporters whose stories are "littered with errors and fabrications." The DMCA claim alleged Press Gazette copied content from a month-old Reddit post about casinos — entirely unrelated to the journalism. This is the second time this year a fake copyright claim has been used to remove Press Gazette's Clickout reporting from Google search.

**Key quotes:**

> "The DMCA (and its variants around the world) were a really terrible legislative mis-step which, as well as spurious and consequence-free takedowns like this, is responsible in large part for the transfer of value from the media to the tech giants over the last few decades. By effectively rendering copyright infringement consequence-free, and reserving the right for tech platforms to profit from it, this law created anarchy online and made copyright infringement into a business model — now being exploited by AI companies and a swarm of proxies helping them get whatever they want, regardless of what the owners say." — Dominic Young, CEO of Axate and SPUR founder

> "Yet again a tech giant proves that it can moderate and censor content its friends don't like but won't deal with the wide range of highly offensive, vile, outrageously false and manifestly dangerous content they host and promote." — David Worsfold, media consultant

**The rupture:** Copyright law was designed to protect creators. It's now being weaponized against journalists reporting on AI content farms — with Google as the unwilling (but compliant) enforcer. The DMCA has no penalty for false claims. File a takedown, the content disappears, the journalist's work is erased from search — and there's zero consequence for the filer. This is the platform-power story from the ground: not a policy paper, not a keynote, but a specific mechanism being used to silence specific journalism about specific bad actors.

**Theme:** Platform power

**Angle:** We talk about AI scraping as the copyright problem. But the more pernicious problem might be copyright law itself being used as a censorship tool against journalism that exposes AI content farms. The platforms comply with takedowns automatically — they have to, under the law. And the law has no built-in penalty for lying.

**Alan signal needed:** Alan has seen content removed or flagged. Or: the broader pattern of "copyright as censorship" — this isn't the first time and won't be the last. The connection to his "funnel" framework: if bad actors can remove good journalism from search with a fake DMCA claim, the funnel is broken at both ends.

**Counter-evidence:** The first Press Gazette article was reinstated after Press Gazette contacted Google. The system has some recourse — but it requires the publisher to have the resources and relationships to escalate. Smaller publishers without a direct line to Google are out of luck.

**Provocation (closing line candidate):** "Copyright was supposed to protect the writer. Now it's being used to erase them. The DMCA was written before AI content farms existed. The law hasn't caught up. And the platforms are happy to comply — it's cheaper than checking."
