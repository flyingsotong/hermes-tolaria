Snippet: The publisher becomes a wholesaler

Hello FNAME,

I’m writing this on the last day of our family vacay to Korea — it’s been a long time on the road, but all that has been marked by some wonderful memories together. I’ll keep this email short.

OpenAI declared recently that chat is dead. An OpenAI exec told the Financial Times that the conversation window — the defining interaction of the ChatGPT era — is being retired. Thibault Sottiaux described the replacement as “your own personal agent, supporting you across everything in your life.”
https://arstechnica.com/ai/2026/06/chat-is-dead-openai-preps-overhaul-of-chatgpt/

The immediate take is: chat was friction, now the system infers intent. The scarce thing shifts from what you articulate to what you want done. From dialogue to delegation. 

But I want to stay with the uncomfortable consequences for information flows — because a year from now, this changes everything about how journalism works.

The intent layer is the only layer

Today's media distribution game is about owning a surface — an inbox, a feed, search results. That world assumes the reader navigates. A year from now, the reader doesn't navigate. They specify an outcome and their agent executes. 

The publisher's content is somewhere in that execution chain, but the publisher has no control over sequence, framing, or even awareness that their content was used.
Whoever maps user intent to information sources owns the relationship. OpenAI is building this with ChatGPT as a superapp. Google is building this with Gemini inside Workspace. Apple is building this with on-device context, which it announced at WWDC earlier this month. The publisher is showing up with chopsticks to a gunfight. The only move is to be the best source for a specific class of agent questions — but only if you’re willing to change what you produce.

The feedback loop breaks

Today you measure opens, clicks, time on page. In agent-execution mode, the agent extracts what it needs and the human never directly interacts with your surface. You get no pageview, no click, no open. The only feedback is whether the agent keeps querying your source — and you only know that if the agent provider tells you. They have no incentive to.

This is the death of the engagement-based business model. If you can't measure attention, you can't sell attention. Subscriptions survive only if the reader has a direct relationship with you — but in an agent-execution world, that relationship is optional. The reader pays for the agent. The agent pays for content at wholesale.

The wholesale shift

Today you're a retailer — you find readers, sell subscriptions, build direct relationships. A year from now you're a wholesaler. You sell content in bulk to agent providers who package it into their retail product. Your brand becomes a data source tag — “source: Magenta Debrief” — not a destination. The economics of wholesale are worse. Lower margins, no upsell, no relationship. But it's not optional. If your content is invisible to agents, it's also invisible to the reader who uses agents — and that's the growth segment.

Three things that survive

Answer-content — information structured to answer specific, recurring agent questions. Regulations, specifications, comparisons, how-tos. Structured, attributed, updateable.
Signal-content — real-time delta detection. What changed, what broke, what's new. Timestamped, versioned, causally linked.
Context-content — analysis agents can't synthesize because it depends on lived experience, relationships, and judgment. The agent links to it rather than summarizing it, because the agent knows it can't reproduce the judgment.

Almost everything else — narrative features, hot takes, aggregated roundups — becomes inventory for agents to summarize and discard. The writer who produces those is in competition with a summary model. The writer who produces what the model can't reproduce has pricing power.

The uncomfortable move

A year from now, the question isn't “how do I get my content into agents” but “how do I know when my agent used my content, and how do I get paid for it?”

Run the test this week. Take your last three pieces. Paste each into an LLM and ask: “Based only on this content, what single action would you take on a reader's behalf?” If the LLM can't name a specific action — not a feeling, not an insight, a concrete execution — you wrote something that no agent will ever surface for anyone.

The action the LLM can infer is the shape of your survival. Everything else is inventory.

— Alan Soon


ONE (RELATED) THING

Le Monde's agent paradox is the most concrete version of the funnel-is-dead thesis I've seen. The French publisher with 665,000 digital subscribers blocks nearly all non-human traffic by default — no licensing deal, no crawl. The publisher now sees that the harder problem is already here: their paying subscribers are the ones using AI agents to read articles, and you can't block the agent without blocking the subscriber. The question isn't whether agents will show up at the publisher's door — it's whether publishers can figure out, before their subscribers do, that the agent is the subscriber.
