# Slugs: The Co-Pilot Isn't There to Fly the Plane

Researchers in the US and Europe have spent 20 years investigating how to replace one of the two pilots in a commercial cockpit with an AI system. The finding coming back from human factors experts is not that AI can't fly — it's that flying is not what a co-pilot is primarily for.

The real function of a human co-pilot, the research shows, is managing the cabin crew response to a disruptive passenger, handling gate logistics on arrival, and talking a startled colleague through the cognitive shock of an exploding engine. These are the tasks that don't show up in the job description but hold the entire system together. Remove the human, and the functions disappear — not because the AI can't fly the plane, but because no one wrote a spec for the invisible work.

Newsrooms are making the same mistake at scale.

Every newsroom automating editorial functions is writing a job description for what they think an editor does: review copy, assign stories, manage workflow. They're automating the visible layer. What they're missing is the non-editing operational layer underneath — the editor who absorbs uncertainty during a breaking news event, regulates the team's emotional temperature, recognizes when a sourced claim doesn't pass the smell test even though every checkbox is ticked, and makes the judgment call that no prompt could have captured because the context was never written down.

The aviation research identifies a specific design principle worth media product leaders internalizing: two humans in a cockpit is not redundancy in the engineering sense. It is a distinct second cognitive function acting as a separate, parallel regulatory system. The co-pilot exists to catch what the pilot misses not because the pilot is incompetent, but because human cognition under operational pressure follows a predictable pattern of narrowing attention, filtering out peripheral signals, and anchoring on early assessments. The second pilot is there to widen that aperture.

The equivalent function in a newsroom is the editor who sits outside the production flow — not the line editor polishing prose, but the person whose job is to stay calm while everyone else is reacting, to ask the question no one has time for, to hold the framing until the facts settle.

That person is disappearing first in the automation wave. Not because anyone decided they weren't valuable, but because their value was never codified, measured, or priced. And when you automate by job title rather than by function, you don't make the system more efficient — you make it more brittle at exactly the point where brittleness is most dangerous.

The aviation industry spent 20 years and millions of dollars to learn this. Media organizations don't have 20 years. The question is whether they can learn the lesson without crashing first.
