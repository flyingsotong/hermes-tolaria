10 Tips for Better Due Diligence
<https://twitter.com/steviemctweets/status/1592249833204903936>
**Smart summary:** The thread author lists some due diligence tips they learned from working at Skadden, KKR, and Goldman Sachs. These tips include things like checking the company's social media, reading everything in the data room, verifying the accuracy of the company's capitalization table, and asking the company how they make money.
As an LP in a few funds and a GP myself, I am becoming disgusted by the _due diligence_ being done by folks. I continue to meet investors who lack the basic knowledge of what diligence should be. Here are a few things my time at Skadden, @KKR_Co, and GS taught me re: diligence...
1. FFS Google the company, founder, and key employees - This might seem too basic but it seems like it's rarely done these days. Socials are also great to find out who these people really are and the kind of people you will be dealing with.
2. Read EVERYTHING in the data room - If a company doesn't have a data room you shouldn't be investing in them. If you are cutting checks off of minimal materials on a co and just what is being said in the pitch or by other investors you're not doing your job correctly.
3. Verify the correctness of their cap table - Spot check with a few people to make sure what you're being told is correct. Less than 15% of the cap tables I see are actually correct. If you have done incorrect math it impacts you and your bottom line and therefore your LPs.
4. Keep asking the company "what do you actually do?" and "How do you make money?" Ask multiple people on the founding team separately. Ask to speak with team members about their jobs. If you can't answer what a company is actually doing and how they make money you pass.
5. Check for legal filings against company, founders (and any former companies the founders may have had), and key team members. This is self explanatory y'all.
6. Ask for personal and professional references and actually check them. If the Founders are serial founders make sure to get references from folks at their former companies.
7. Always ask to meet the whole founding team together at least once. You need to see the dynamics and how they get along. Also, this will let you know if there are any key strategies they have internal disagreements about.
8. If there is a technical aspect of the company, ask an unbiased third party to evaluate. Someone smart who you trust, but not in the "i know a guy" way.
9. Anytime you get pushed to skip diligence or speed it up because of a "hot" round...pass. Every single time this happens something will come out later that good diligence would have uncovered. If you do invest in these situations and your investment goes to 0 IT'S YOUR FAULT.
10. If legal or regulatory compliance is needed ask to speak with their legal team on the strategies they're using. I'm always shocked, but many times there is nobody actually driving legal strategies and it will be the death of the company.
