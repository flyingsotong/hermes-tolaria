**1. A Risk-Based Approach**
This is the central pillar of the entire framework. Instead of a single, restrictive set of rules for all drones, the regulations are calibrated to the level of risk a specific drone operation poses. The greater the potential risk to people, property, or other aircraft, the more stringent the requirements for pilot competency, equipment airworthiness, and operational permits.
**How it's applied**
1.**Weight Tiers:** A drone weighing less than 250g is considered low-risk and is exempt from registration. As the weight increases (e.g., >1.5kg, >7kg, >25kg), the requirements for pilot certification and permits become progressively stricter because a heavier drone can cause more damage in an accident.
2.**Purpose of Flight:** "Business" use is considered inherently higher risk than "Recreational" use. Commercial operations often involve flying for longer durations, in more complex environments, or for clients who expect a high standard of safety. Therefore, business users face the strictest requirements, needing a UA Pilot Licence and an Operator Permit from the outset for outdoor flights.
**2. Protecting Manned Aviation**
Given Singapore's status as a major international aviation hub with a busy and complex airspace (Changi Airport and military airbases), a primary goal is to prevent any conflict between drones and manned aircraft.
**How it's applied**
1.**No-Fly Zones:** Strict no-fly zones are established within 5km of any airport or airbase.
1)**Altitude Limits:** A general altitude cap of 200 feet (approx. 60 meters) above mean sea level is enforced for recreational and educational users to keep them well clear of commercial flight paths. Flying higher requires a specific Class 2 Activity Permit.
2)**Permitting Process:** The Civil Aviation Authority of Singapore (CAAS) and the Republic of Singapore Air Force (RSAF) are involved in the permit approval process, especially for flights that require airspace clearance, ensuring that drone activities do not interfere with national aviation operations.
**3. Accountability and Traceability**
In the event of an incident, misuse, or breach of regulations, authorities must be able to identify the person responsible.
**How it's applied**
1.**Mandatory Registration:** All drones weighing over 250g must be registered. This creates a direct link between a physical drone and its owner.
2.**Remote Identification:**The upcoming requirement for Broadcast Remote Identification (B-RID), essentially a digital license plate, will allow authorities to identify drones in real-time during flight, enhancing situational awareness and enforcement capabilities.
3.**Operator Permits:**The Operator Permit system for businesses ensures that not just the individual pilot, but the entire organisation, has a robust safety management system in place and is accountable for its operations.
**4. Ensuring Public Safety and Security**
The regulations differentiate between the risks posed to the public in different environments. This includes both physical safety from falling drones and security concerns related to surveillance.
**How it's applied**
1.**Indoor vs. Outdoor Distinction:**Flying indoors is generally less regulated because the risks are contained. However, once an indoor flight involves a large crowd (>50 people) or is a competitive event, the risk to the public increases, triggering permit and pilot certification requirements.
2.**Publicly Accessible Areas:**The rules for indoor flying become stricter if the location is "publicly accessible," acknowledging the higher risk to people who are not involved in the drone activity.
3.**Protected and Prohibited Areas:**Flying over designated protected areas (e.g., sensitive government installations) requires an additional permit from the Singapore Police Force (SPF), while flying in prohibited areas is banned entirely to prevent security breaches.
4.**Crowd Restrictions:**The threshold of "more than 50 individuals" is a clear marker used to identify higher-risk activities that require formal permits, as events with crowds significantly increase the potential for harm in case of a mishap.
