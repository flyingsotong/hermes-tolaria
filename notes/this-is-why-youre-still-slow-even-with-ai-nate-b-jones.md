# THIS is Why You're Still Slow Even With AI (Nate B. Jones)

**Summary**

AI hasn't eliminated the bottleneck in knowledge work — it's moved it. Execution used to be the scarce resource, so we built elaborate rituals to protect it: PRDs, approval gates, consensus meetings, meticulous planning. Those rituals are now the bottleneck. When a prototype is faster to build than a PRD is to write, optimizing for execution protection actively slows you down.

The new scarce resources are**clarity** (knowing what's worth building),**ambition** (swinging big when you have 50 shots a year instead of 3),**distribution** (getting things into people's hands), and**relationships** (the one thing you can't vibe-code).

Nate B. Jones identifies eight habits that made sense in the old model and are now costing you: the permission loop, polish as procrastination, meetings as default, structured waiting, planning over doing, the deck instead of the demo, consensus before action, and hoarding until ready. All are risk-management rituals designed for expensive execution — and all have had their logic inverted.

The fix isn't better tools. It's stopping the things that are no longer worth doing: ship while others plan, iterate while others align, learn while others polish.

---

**The Bottleneck Moved — Here's What to Do About It**

The one constant right now is chaos. I hear it over and over again from folks. The rate of change, the sheer unpredictable chaos of AI is very difficult to navigate — hard to tell what's up and what's down.

So I want to simplify it. I want to zero in on some of the underlying drivers that are shifting what truly AI-native working looks like, why most of our work habits are now optimizing for the incorrect thing, and I want to give you eight specific habits you can break to start working in a more AI-native way.

---

But first, two scenes from the last month.

**Scene one:** Anthropic shipping co-work — a full product feature, built in 10 days with just four people, written entirely in Claude Code. And Claude Code, mind you, is an entire product that is less than a year old. These folks haven't had years of AI-native working to pull this off. The Anthropic team is evolving as they go.

**Scene two:** At your company — and many companies I've worked at in the past — there's a conference room where I guarantee you a leader is asking for a 30-day implementation roadmap, or a three-month implementation roadmap for their AI strategy, with phases and milestones and resource allocation and a plan to protect capacity.

Here's the problem: execution capacity isn't scarce anymore. 10 days, four people, shipping 60 to 100 releases daily. Execution capacity is not the problem.

When we build our AI strategies, we're frequently asking for guidance on the thing that is no longer scarce and no longer requires efficiency. We have spent so much of our business lives assuming that execution capacity is scarce. Anthropic and many other organizations are now showing that it's not.

And yet every organization, all of our individual work habits, anyone who's had a career more than three or four years — it's all built around the implicit answer to one question: *What's expensive here?* And the answer has always been: execution.

For most of our careers, building things required scarce hours from scarce people with scarce skills. Finding really good engineers was hard. Training them took years. Every hour of their time was precious. So we evolved elaborate rituals to protect that capacity — planning processes, approval gates, specs, PRDs, meetings to align before anybody built. All of it was designed to protect precious engineering execution time so it wouldn't be wasted on the wrong problems.

That made sense. When the meeting to discuss a feature takes much less time than the time to build it, you definitely want to hold the meeting. When gathering requirements costs much less than writing the code, you gather requirements first. When rework is expensive, you plan meticulously to avoid it.

AI has inverted the entire cost ratio. We're no longer debating waterfall versus agile. We're talking about a different kind of work entirely.

Agile never imagined a world where everybody commits code. Cursor went from $1M to $500M in annual revenue faster than any SaaS company in history — and they're not done. They're launching Cursor for designers, not as a separate product, just relentlessly shipping features that make it easier for other job families to use the tool. What used to be an impossible product expansion is now another day at Cursor. What used to take months to plan and ship is now 10 days for four people. Coinbase engineers report that single people are now refactoring or building entire codebases in days.

In that world: the meeting to discuss a feature now takes longer than building the feature. The PRD can take longer than the prototype. The planning process can take longer than shipping three versions and seeing which one works.

**The bottleneck has moved — but our work habits are still stuck in the way we've worked most of our careers.**

---

There's a manufacturing principle that explains what's happening here. When you eliminate a bottleneck in a manufacturing system, the bottleneck doesn't disappear — it moves somewhere else downstream. Resistance is never destroyed, it's relocated.

For decades, the constraint in knowledge work was execution. Now that AI has largely removed that constraint, the bottleneck has shifted. It hasn't disappeared. It's shifted to:

**Clarity.** Do you actually know what's worth building? That is now a billion-dollar question. You can build faster than you can think. The ones that make money in 2026 will be the ones who have clarity — who know what product the customer actually wants. PRDs were always a substitute for clarity, a big hedge against expensive rework. But writing a PRD can now cost more than shipping the whole thing. I have seen PRD cycles at big companies take longer than Claude took to ship all of co-work.

**Ambition.** Are you swinging hard enough? When you had three or four shots a year, small bets made sense. But when you can ship every 10 days — 50 swings a year — suddenly your risk is timidity. The danger isn't building the wrong thing, because you've got 50 shots to get it right. The danger is not building enough things toward a larger, truly transformative vision. We're going to see a lot of people using AI to build horseless carriages — that's what we called cars before we had a mental model for them. We need the ambition and the eyes to see what the 10x better product looks like in our particular domain and shoot for it.

**Distribution.** When everybody can build, product is not the moat it once was. Getting it into people's hands is the moat. Cognition — makers of the AI coding agent Devon — chose not to go after distribution themselves. Instead, they partnered with Infosys and are deploying Devon across a third of a million people and their entire global client base. Because Infosys has distribution. Infosys has decades of enterprise relationships. The technology is the easy part. Reaching customers is hard.

**Relationships.** When capabilities are compounding quickly and platforms keep shifting, the thing that is durable is relationships. You can't vibe-code a relationship. Companies with strong business relationships will have durable advantages. And you individually will have a durable advantage by being known as a trusted person to work with — because when technical skills are rapidly becoming a commodity, people will turn to someone they can trust.

**The Eight Habits to Break**

Most of the work habits we've embodied are risk-management rituals designed for a world where execution was expensive. They've calcified into defaults that persist even though all of the unit economics have flipped.

You've probably felt this — spending more time prepping for work than you should, sensing there should be an easier way, protecting something that doesn't need as much protection anymore.

Here are the habits that made sense in the old model and are now actively costing you.

---

**1. The Permission Loop**

*Old logic:* Doing something was expensive, so check before you do. Get buy-in. Make sure you're building the right thing before spending precious resources.

That logic is now broken. The email thread to get approval can take more time than building the prototype. The Slack conversation to confirm direction can take longer than trying both directions and seeing what works.

Approval processes were designed to reduce risk. They don't anymore. They just add slowness.

Organizations need to shift. People need to be free to default to doing — building the rough version, showing it, asking forgiveness when needed. Leaders need to cast a wider vision so teams can ship relentlessly and autonomously against that direction.

---

**2. Polish as Procrastination**

*Old logic:* You get one shot, so make it count. Don't waste expensive execution on something half-baked.

People are now spending 80% of their time on the last 20% of quality, when the marginal value of that polish is dropping fast. I'm not saying good thinking is going out of style. I'm saying polish is becoming a way to avoid getting your ideas into contact with reality quickly.

Even a directionally correct, ambitious idea is enough to get going. The rough version that exists will beat the polished version that doesn't. And you can always improve it. The value of shipping ugly is up.

(That said — there's a huge amount of money to be made in polished UI for AI products. We're leaving value on the table by shipping so fast. Take that as a goal to iterate toward. But if you're getting started, ship first.)

---

**3. Meetings as Default**

*Old logic:* Get alignment before action. Get everybody in the room so we don't waste expensive execution time.

An hour of six people's time is six hours of work — often enough to just build the thing.

Meetings still feel responsible. They distribute accountability. But meetings about what to build often don't actually resolve what to build. They surface opinions, create action items, and create delays.

Just build it and show it. Replace the meeting with a product demo. Before you schedule your next meeting, ask: *what if I built the rough version of this and showed people instead?* That's what AI-native companies are doing.

---

**4. Structured Waiting**

*Old logic:* Coordination is important. Wait for feedback, wait for the sync, wait for someone to unblock you. Everyone's time is precious, so respect the process.

Most of what you're waiting for doesn't need to be waited for. You end up outsourcing your momentum to other people's calendars.

Stop waiting. Do the next thing while you wait for feedback on the first thing. Assume the answer is yes. If you're blocked on a decision, make a provisional decision, let people know, and keep moving.

Waiting an hour in the 2010s was waiting an hour. Waiting an hour now is waiting a prototype.

---

**5. Planning Over Doing (Measure Twice, Cut Once)**

*Old logic:* Planning is cheap. Execution is expensive.

I've watched people spend eight weeks writing a plan that ends up being almost always incorrect — plans almost never survive contact with reality.

See if you can cut your planning by 90%. Replace it with learning through prototyping. Replace it with a bold rough direction and aggressive shipping, optimizing for what works. If you haven't built something in the last couple of weeks, you're probably overplanning.

Let reality inform the plan instead of trying to predict reality with the plan. Prediction has become expensive and luxurious. Execution is cheap and more accurate.

---

**6. The Deck Instead of the Demo**

*Old logic:* Build consensus with a deck. Walk it around. Workshop the fonts. Get stakeholder alignment.

Forget all of that. Build the demo. Show it.

Build a working prototype and show that instead of the deck. All of the rituals we prepared as a hedge against execution are now in question. Why not just do the work? Why not just ship to customers?

---

**7. Consensus Before Action**

*Old logic:* Get everybody aligned before moving.

The cost of consensus has 10x'd or 100x'd. And ironically, consensus often wasn't real anyway — people would agree in meetings then undermine decisions later.

Let consensus come from results. Just try things. "I tried X and here's what happened" is much more persuasive than "let's agree to try X." Run the experiment first, then everyone will align when they see the data. And it's never been faster to run that experiment.

---

**8. Hoarding Until Ready**

*Old logic:* Don't show work until it's complete. Half-finished work wastes other people's time.

You're probably sitting on ideas, drafts, and prototypes until they feel ready — which means you're getting feedback late, after you've already invested in a direction that might be wrong.

The cost of getting to a rapid version is so cheap now. You just have to be willing to show it. That takes a little ego death. We have to be willing to show work that's raw and unfinished. What if people think it's bad? Those are the reactions you need. Finding out you're wrong a week from now is better than finding out a month from now.

Half-finished work where you put some thought in — where you have a direction and just want feedback on a quick prototype — that's different from AI slop with no thinking behind it. The through line is still clear thinking. That doesn't go away.

---

**Putting It Together**

All eight habits are risk-management rituals that made sense when doing was expensive. The unit economics have all flipped.

**Old way:** You have an idea. You write a proposal. You schedule a meeting. The meeting surfaces questions. You update the proposal. Weeks later, you get approval to pilot.

**New way:** You have an idea. You spend an afternoon building a rough version. You show three people. Two have concerns that kill the idea — good, you found out in a day instead of a month. Or they like it and you iterate from there.

The through line is simple: stop treating process as a prerequisite and start treating iteration — getting your ideas into contact with reality — as the process.

---

**The Counterargument**

In some fields you'll hear: *quality matters here, my boss expects a plan, I'll get in trouble if I just do things.*

Fair. These habits have different impacts in fields with high legal and compliance risks. But ask yourself: how much of the process you're following is actually required, and how much is just how things have always been done? Most of us, when we're honest, realize we have more latitude than we're using. The habits that feel mandatory are often just defaults nobody has questioned.

Start small. Pick the habit that feels lowest stakes. Try skipping the polish. Skip a meeting and build the thing instead. Make a provisional decision instead of waiting.

---

**The Big Picture**

The chaos you're feeling is not random. It's the gap between where the bottleneck has moved and the habits you still have today.

When you close that gap — when you align your work habits to how AI actually changes scarcity — you're going to know where to spend your time. You're going to understand why meetings can't be the default anymore. The truly precious resources — clarity, ambition, distribution, relationships — are even more valuable now that execution is cheap.

The people who figure this out first will be operating at a velocity that feels a lot more like Anthropic or Cursor and a lot less like a traditional big company. Not because they have better tools — almost everybody is getting the fancy tools now. But because they stopped doing the things that are no longer worth doing.

They'll be shipping while other people plan. Iterating while other people align. Learning while other people polish.
