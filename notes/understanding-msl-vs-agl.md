Objective: By the end of this lesson, you will be able to confidently differentiate between Mean Sea Level (MSL) and Above Ground Level (AGL) and correctly identify these values on an aeronautical chart.

1. **MSL is your "Map Altitude," AGL is your "Drone Altitude."** All permanent objects on a chart (airports, mountains, towers) use MSL, a fixed height from the sea. Your drone's legal flight ceiling (e.g., 400 feet) is based on AGL, your height from the ground directly beneath you.
2. **On a chart, parentheses mean AGL.** The primary, larger number for an obstacle is its height in MSL. The number inside the parentheses () is its height in AGL.

**1. Key Concepts: What Are MSL and AGL?**

Think of altitude in two different ways: one is a fixed, universal reference, and the other is your height relative to what's directly below you.

- **MSL (Mean Sea Level):** This is your altitude measured from the average level of the ocean's surface. It's a **fixed, standardized benchmark**. All permanent features on a map — like airports, mountains, and obstacles — use MSL for their official elevation.
  - **Think of it as:** Your "official" or "true" altitude.
- **AGL (Above Ground Level):** This is your altitude measured from the ground **directly beneath your drone**. This value is constantly changing as you fly over hills, valleys, and buildings.
  - **Think of it as:** Your height relative to your immediate surroundings.

**2. A Simple Analogy: The Skyscraper on a Hill**

Imagine a tall skyscraper built on top of a 300-foot hill.
- The skyscraper itself is 1,000 feet tall.
- If you are standing on the roof, your **AGL** is **1,000 feet** (your height from the ground at the base of the building).
- However, your **MSL** altitude is **1,300 feet** (1,000 ft building + 300 ft hill). Your height from sea level is fixed, no matter where you are on the roof.

Now, if you fly your drone from the rooftop out over a valley next to the hill, your MSL altitude might stay at 1,300 feet, but your AGL could suddenly be much higher, because the ground is now farther below you.

**3. How to Read MSL vs. AGL on a Chart**

Aeronautical charts use a simple system to show both values.

- **MSL is the Main Number:** Almost all primary elevation numbers on a chart are in MSL.
  - **Airport Elevation:** The listed elevation for an airport is **always MSL**.
  - **Maximum Elevation Figure (MEF):** The large number in a quadrant on the chart, indicating the highest feature in that area, is in **MSL**.
  - **Obstacles (Towers, etc.):** The primary number listed for an obstacle's height is its top in **MSL**.
- **AGL is the "Extra Info" in Parentheses:** For man-made obstacles like towers, a second number is often provided to tell you how high it is from the ground it sits on.
  - This AGL value is **always shown in parentheses** ().

**Example from the Chart:**

Let's look at a typical tower symbol on a chart. You might see:

> 1849
>
> (1520)

This means:
- The very top of the tower is **1,849 feet MSL**.
- The tower itself is **1,520 feet AGL** (i.e., it's a 1,520-foot tall structure).

**4. Why This Is Crucial for Drone Pilots**

Your flight regulations are almost always based on **AGL**. The most common FAA rule for recreational and Part 107 pilots is to not fly more than **400 feet AGL**.

**The Common Trap:** If you take off from a 500-foot MSL hill and fly straight out, maintaining your altitude, you will quickly be more than 400 feet AGL once you fly over the lower ground surrounding the hill. You must always be aware of the terrain beneath you to ensure you are flying legally and safely.

**5. Lesson Summary & Memory Aid**

- **MSL (Mean Sea Level):** Altitude from the **S**ea. It's a **S**tandard, fixed number.
- **AGL (Above Ground Level):** Altitude from the **G**round. It's variable and what **G**overns your drone's legal flight ceiling.
- **On a Chart:**
  - Big number = **MSL**
  - Number in () = **AGL**
