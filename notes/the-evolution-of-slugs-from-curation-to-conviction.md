# The Evolution of Slugs: From curation to conviction

**A profile of the newsletter that grew up with media's hardest decade**

## The beginning

On July 31, 2015, Alan Soon sent 40 people a newsletter called "The Splice Slugs — Week 1." The subject line was: *This week's must-reads on media trends by The Splice Newsroom.*

It was a curation play. A filter for busy media professionals drowning in noise. Here are the links you need to know about, here's why they matter. Open rate: 70%. Click rate: 15%. The audience was small enough that every subscriber was known, every open was a signal, and the format was simple enough that you could write it in an afternoon.

Slugs was born in the era of platform dependency. The questions were: How do we get Google traffic? How do we beat the Facebook algorithm? What's the right paywall strategy? The newsletter was a commentary track on an industry trying to figure out the internet.

It wasn't called a "media intelligence report" yet. That would come later.

## The format evolves (2015-2018)

The first three years were a search for the right shape. The subject line shifted six times:

- *This week's must-reads on media trends by The Splice Newsroom* (Jul 2015)
- *Your weekly wrap up of media trends, threats and tips* (Oct 2015 — the word "threats" appears)
- *Your weekly intelligence of media trends, threats and tips* (Sep 2017 — "intelligence" enters)
- *Your weekly media intelligence of trends, threats and tips* (Sep 2017 — moving words around)
- *The Splice Newsroom intelligence report* (Sep 2017 — shorter, cleaner)
- *The Splice Newsroom media intelligence report* (Oct 2017)
- *Splice Slugs: your media intelligence report* (May 2018 — settled, and never changed since)

That last one locked in at Week 147 and has been the subject line for eight years. It's a brand identifier now, not a description. Habitual openers know what it is. The question is whether it still signals what's inside.

The title naming evolved too: *The Splice Slugs — Week N* became *Splice Slugs — Week N — Date* around March 2018. "The" dropped, dates standardized, the format solidified.

## The audience grows (2015-2024)

From 40 recipients to 2,250 at peak. The growth wasn't linear — it was steady and organic:

- 2015: 114/issue
- 2017: 785/issue
- 2019: 1,826/issue
- 2022: 2,024/issue
- 2024: 2,246/issue (peak)

No viral moment, no single subscriber spike. Just consistent value delivered weekly for long enough that the audience became the industry. Subscribers from AFP, BBC, CNN, The Economist, Google, Harvard, Reuters, Wall Street Journal, the U.S. State Department. The people who shape media policy and practice started their week with Slugs.

Total lifetime: 714,000 sends, 321,000 unique opens, 66,000 clicks, 1,102 unsubscribes. A 45% lifetime open rate over 11 years is exceptional by any standard.

## The format shifts (2018-2022)

Sometime around 2018, the newsletter stopped being a roundup. It's hard to pinpoint exactly when — Mailchimp doesn't measure content structure — but the shift was real.

The early Slugs was a curator's voice: here's what happened, here's what it means, here's why you should care. Links were the product. The newsletter was a launchpad to the rest of the web.

The mid-period Slugs started connecting dots. Two stories that seemed unrelated were actually the same story told two ways. The voice shifted from informative to analytical. Links were still important, but the framing became the value.

By 2022, the links had become supporting evidence rather than the main event. The essay itself was the product. The reader was meant to finish the email informed — not because they clicked away, but because the argument was delivered inline.

The data reflects this shift. Click rate declined steadily:

- 2015-2016: 16.1%
- 2017-2019: 11.6%
- 2020-2021: 10.1%
- 2022-2023: 8.5%
- 2024-2025: 7.9%

But open rate held. Readers weren't disengaging — they were reading differently.

## The subject matter deepens

The preoccupations of Slugs track the industry's own arc, but consistently ahead of the curve:

In 2015-2017, the conversation was about platform dependency, traffic declines, paywall strategies, Google algorithm changes. Standard media survival talk.

By 2018-2019, it had shifted to Facebook's pivot, subscription models, membership economies, newsroom innovation. The questions were getting harder.

2020-2021 was the Covid era — disruption, remote newsrooms, the great reset of media economics. Slugs covered it, but kept its eye on the structural changes beneath the crisis headlines.

2022-2023 was the arrival of AI. Not as a tool to write faster — as a force that rewrites the physics of publishing. Slugs was early on this, treating GPT's arrival not as a productivity story but as a restructuring of the value chain.

2024-2025 crystallized into what Slugs is now known for: agents, headless media, the death of the funnel, licensing over impressions. The argument that information becomes software — that discovery now flows through actionability, not distribution.

By 2026, the question is no longer "how do I get read" but "can my information be acted on at all."

## The 2026 moment

The most recent chapter is also the most distinct. In October 2025, Slugs went quiet for 119 days — along with its sister newsletter Frames. A deliberate pause, a reset.

When it returned in February 2026, something had shifted. The essays were denser. The arguments were tighter. The scene opening — Shanghai, Adelaide flight training, watching Nvidia and Microsoft keynotes back to back — became a trapdoor into a larger idea.

And the click rate collapsed. From 8-12% before the pause to 2-3% after. At first glance, alarming. But the open rate held at 47%. Readers were still opening, still reading — they just weren't clicking.

The explanation is simpler than the data suggests: Slugs stopped asking people to click. The modern Slugs is a single sustained argument delivered inline. Three or four links as supporting evidence, not as the main course. The reader finishes the email informed. The essay is the product, not a gateway to a product.

This is a fundamentally different newsletter than the one that launched in 2015. The first Slugs sent you to other people's work. The current Slugs sends you into Alan's thinking. One is curation. The other is conviction.

## What stayed the same

Through every evolution, four things held:

**The scene opening.** Alan writes from where he is — Shanghai with his family, a small town in South Australia during flight training, a hotel room watching keynotes. The personal location isn't color. It's grounding. The reader trusts the argument because they trust the witness.

**The directness.** "It won't. And I think we're finally at the moment where I have to stop softening that." That voice was there in 2015. It's stronger now.

**The closing question.** Every issue leaves the reader with something to answer, not something to do. Not a call to action — a call to thought. "If your homepage stopped existing tomorrow, what would your audience still pay you for, and who exactly would the buyer be?"

**The warm sign-off.** New subscribers from Centre for Advancing Journalism, Disclose, Locales.tv, and Cinereach. Welcome. Let me know what you think.

## The arc

Slugs started as a filter and became a framework. It began by telling you what happened in media this week and ended up telling you how to think about what *could* happen next. The audience grew not because the newsletter got louder, but because it got clearer.

The industry it covers went through its hardest decade — platform dependency, pandemic, AI disruption, the dismantling of the page-view economy. Slugs covered all of it, but more importantly, it named the patterns before the industry had language for them: headless media, information as software, actionability as discovery, the funnel as a dead institutional arrangement.

The newsletter didn't just report on the transformation. It became an example of it. No homepage. No ad stack. No paid tier. Just a relationship with readers who trust it enough to open it every week. That's what "be callable" looks like when you're the one writing.

In 2026, at 471 issues and counting, Slugs is still a weekly argument for a different model of media. And the proof is in the inbox.
