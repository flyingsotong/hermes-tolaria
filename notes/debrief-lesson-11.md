# Debrief Lesson 11

1.**Drifting into the left on downwind — not identifying and correcting:**

-**Why it happens:** Usually due to engine torque and P-factor, especially at lower airspeeds and higher power settings (like after takeoff or initial downwind entry). Your plane naturally wants to yaw and roll left. Sometimes, a slight crosswind from the right can exacerbate this without you noticing.

***Tips:**

 -**Anticipate:** _Know_ that the aircraft wants to turn left. Be ready to apply slight _right_ rudder pressure as soon as you roll wings level on the downwind leg.

 ***Visual Reference:** Pick a point far off your right wingtip that is parallel to the runway. This could be a road, a line of trees, or a field boundary. Your goal is to keep that reference point in the same position relative to your wingtip. If it starts moving forward, you're drifting left (towards the runway). If it moves backward, you're drifting right (away from the runway).

 -**Look Far Ahead:** Don't just stare at the runway abeam your position. Look further down the extended centerline of the runway. This helps you maintain a parallel track.

 ***Scan Regularly:** Include your ground track in your scan. Look outside at the runway, check your reference point, check your instruments, then back outside. Don't fixate on any one thing.

 -**Small Corrections:** Use small, smooth rudder inputs to correct your track. Don't wait for a large deviation. Correct small drifts early.

1.**Not holding airspeed and actively fixing:**

-**Why it happens:** Forgetting the fundamental relationship:**Pitch controls Airspeed; Power controls Altitude (or Rate of Descent/Climb).** Also, not trimming properly forces you to constantly fight the controls.

***Tips:**

 -**Pitch for Airspeed:** Set the desired power setting for the phase of flight (e.g., downwind). Then, adjust the aircraft's pitch (nose position relative to the horizon) to achieve your target airspeed. If you're slow, lower the nose slightly. If you're fast, raise the nose slightly.

 ***Power for Altitude/Rate:** Once you have the correct pitch attitude for your target airspeed, adjust power if you need to maintain altitude, climb, or descend at a specific rate.

 -**Scan, Scan, Scan:** Your instrument scan must constantly include the airspeed indicator. Look outside (Attitude), check Airspeed, back outside (Attitude), check Altitude/VSI, back outside (Attitude)... etc.

 ***Small, Frequent Corrections:** Don't wait for the airspeed to be 10 knots off. If you see it deviating by 3-5 knots, make a tiny pitch adjustment _immediately_.

 -**Trim:** Once you have the correct power set and the correct pitch attitude established to hold your airspeed and altitude/descent rate, _trim_ the aircraft to relieve control pressure. This makes it much easier to hold the desired parameters. Re-trim after every power or pitch change.

1.**Watch the horizon picture!**

-**Why it happens:** Often due to fixating inside the cockpit (on instruments) or not understanding how the view outside relates to the aircraft's state.

***Tips:**

 -**Primary Reference:** The horizon is your primary reference for attitude control (pitch and bank). Learn what the aircraft looks like relative to the horizon in different phases of flight (climb, cruise, descent, turns).

 ***Look FAR Ahead:** Don't stare down at the ground directly in front of you. Look out towards the horizon. This gives you a much more stable reference.

 -**"Set the Picture":** For level flight, note where the horizon cuts across your windshield or sits relative to the top of your instrument panel/cowling. For a standard climb or descent, learn that specific "picture."

 ***Peripheral Vision:** Use your peripheral vision to keep the wings level with the horizon unless you intend to turn.

 -**Connect Inside and Outside:** When you glance inside at your attitude indicator, confirm that it matches the picture you see outside. This builds the connection. Spend 80-90% of your time looking outside.

1.**On low level circuits — cruise at 23 manifold pressure when straight and level no matter what:**

-**Why it happens:** Forgetting procedures, task saturation, or not prioritizing the power setting during that specific flight segment.

***Tips:**

 -**Procedural Item:** Make setting 23 manifold pressure a specific, mandatory step in your flow/checklist for entering the straight and level cruise portion of the low-level circuit.

 ***Link to PAT/APT:** When you establish straight and level flight in that segment, think:**P**ower (Set 23),**A**ttitude (Set level flight pitch picture),**T**rim (Trim off control pressures).

 -**Consistency:** Practice setting this power level consistently every time you reach that segment. It builds muscle memory and reinforces the procedure.

1.**Keep it simple — don’t overthink it. It’s all PAT APT — all procedures:**

-**Why it happens:** Trying to manage too many variables simultaneously without a framework, leading to cognitive overload.

***Tips:**

 -**PAT / APT Mantra:** This is likely referring to**P**ower,**A**ttitude,**T**rim or**A**ttitude,**P**ower,**T**rim (the order can vary slightly depending on what you're trying to achieve, but the elements are key). When you need to change your aircraft's state (e.g., level off from a climb, enter a descent):

 1. Set the**Power** for the new phase.

 2. Set the**Attitude** (pitch picture) for the desired performance (airspeed/rate of descent).

 3.**Trim** to relieve control pressures once established.

 -**Trust the Procedures:** Your instructor is teaching you standard procedures for a reason – they work! Focus on executing the learned steps for maneuvers, checks, and circuit legs.

 ***Verbalize:** Talk yourself through the procedures out loud (or silently). "Okay, turning final. Power set, attitude for descent, check airspeed, trim."

 -**Checklists:** Use your checklists diligently. They are designed to simplify complex tasks and prevent forgetting crucial items.

 ***One Thing at a Time:** While multitasking is necessary, try to focus on completing one step of a procedure before jumping to the next, especially when learning.

1.**In stalls — you only need to put the nose to the horizon or slightly under — no panic:**

-**Why it happens:** Instinctive (and incorrect) reaction to pull back when the nose drops, or an excessive, panicked forward push, leading to large altitude loss or even a secondary stall in some cases.

***Tips:**

 -**Primary Action - Reduce Angle of Attack:** The _only_ way to recover from a stall is to reduce the Angle of Attack (AoA). This means smoothly relaxing back pressure or applying gentle forward pressure on the control column.

 ***Goal Attitude:** Aim the nose _at_ the horizon or just slightly below it. This ensures you have sufficiently reduced the AoA without entering an excessive dive.

 -**Smoothness is Key:** Avoid abrupt, jerky movements. Smoothly lower the nose, level the wings using rudder primarily (ailerons can aggravate the stall when stalled), and smoothly apply full power _after_ reducing the AoA.

 ***Practice and Visualize:** Practice stall recoveries with your instructor until the correct, smooth response becomes instinctive. Visualize the recovery sequence: Recognize stall -> Reduce AoA (Nose to Horizon) -> Level Wings -> Full Power -> Recover from descent.

 -**Trust the Airplane:** The airplane _wants_ to fly. Reducing the AoA allows it to do so. Don't fight it with excessive control inputs.

**General Tip:**

-**Chair Flying:** Sit in a chair at home, close your eyes, and visualize the entire flight, especially the parts you struggled with. Go through the procedures, the visual cues, the control inputs, and the corrections in your mind. This is incredibly effective.

Don't get discouraged! This is all part of the learning process. Every pilot has gone through this. Your instructor is giving you valuable feedback to focus on. Keep practicing, focus on these points, and you'll see improvement. Good luck!
