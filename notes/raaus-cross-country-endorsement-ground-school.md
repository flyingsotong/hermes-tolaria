# RAAus Cross Country (X) Endorsement — Ground School

## Regulatory requirement

You need this endorsement to act as PIC beyond **25 NM** from your departure point. Chaining flights of 25 NM or less does NOT satisfy this rule — it's distance from original departure point. (OM Section 2.01, paras 5 & 28)

Group D variation: pilots without X can operate 25-100 NM if a CFI/Examiner endorses their logbook confirming navigation competence (OM Section 2.07, para 36-37).

## Minimum flight experience

**Group A or B (3-axis aircraft):**
- 10 hours dual cross-country navigation training
- 2 hours solo cross-country navigation
- Pass RAAus Flight Navigation written exam (80%)
- Pass navigation flight test with DCFI, CFI, or Pilot Examiner

**Group D (weight-shift/trikes):**
- 2 hours dual cross-country navigation training
- 1 hour solo cross-country navigation
- Pass RAAus Group D Flight Navigation written exam (80%)
- Pass navigation flight test with CFI or Pilot Examiner

## Exam structure

The written examination is set to **Unit 2.03 of the RAAus Syllabus of Flight Training** and administered at an approved RAAus Flight Training School. Pass mark: **80%**. You sit it prior to being recommended for the endorsement.

## Unit 2.03 — Navigation syllabus

### 1.1.1 Charts and airspace (standard: P/Cert)

On WAC, VTC, VNC, and ERC-L charts covering the local area of operation:

- Identify major features aiding map reading: roads, rivers, lakes, coastlines
- Identify obstacles, spot heights, elevation above terrain
- Identify CTA, PRDs, aerodrome data on VTC/ERC
- Decode symbols with reference to chart legend
- Assess terrain height from hypsometric tints and contours
- Estimate track and distance
- Explain the reason for chart orientation in flight
- Use ERSA to extract runway data and special procedures
- Identify airspace boundaries and symbols on AIP visual charts
- Apply magnetic variation to obtain magnetic direction
- Determine head/tail wind and crosswind components from W/V and HDG

### 1.1.2 Computation techniques

Mental rules of thumb for:
- Time interval from GS and distance (e.g. 120 KT = 2 NM/min)
- Endurance from fuel flow and fuel available (excluding reserves)

### 1.1.3 Form of the Earth

Understand:
- Shape and rotation of the earth
- Latitude and longitude
- Meridians of longitude, parallels of latitude
- True north vs magnetic north
- Terrestrial magnetism, magnetic variation, change in variation with time
- Distance on earth: relationship between 1 minute of latitude and 1 NM
- UTC, Local Mean Time, Local (Standard) Time, Local Summer Time
- Extract beginning and end of civil twilight from AIP daylight and darkness graphs (+/- 5 min)
- Factors causing daylight to diminish earlier than AIP graph times
- Earth's rotation and revolution effects on daylight and seasons
- Conversions between LMT, UTC, Local Standard Time, Local Summer Time

### 1.1.4 Time

- Beginning and end of daylight
- Period of daylight calculations

### 1.1.5 Charts and publications (advanced)

From AIP Visual Charts (VTC, VNC, WAC, ERC-L, AUS PCA) and ERSA:
- Select appropriate charts/documents for given operational information
- Extract and decode symbols from AIP visual charts
- Interpret topographic detail on WAC, VTC, VNC
- Measure distance using chart scale and latitude scale
- Plot position by latitude/longitude and by bearing/distance
- Estimate track and distance

### 1.1.6 Computations (advanced)

Review basic computations, then:
- Solve GS, distance, fuel used, fuel required, fuel remaining, fuel consumption problems
- Determine HDG, GS, and drift given TAS, W/V, TR
- Determine TR given HDG, TAS, W/V

### 1.1.7 Pilot navigation (in-flight)

- Principles of map reading: chart orientation, map-to-ground vs ground-to-map
- Select position lines to establish GS, track error, or a fix
- Select ground features for position at low level (500 ft AGL), mid-level (2000-10,000 ft), over mountains, coastal, densely/sparsely populated areas
- Chart preparation: draw tracks, track error lines, time/distance markings
- Select WAC(s) and AIP visual charts for a given route
- Determine track made good (TMG) from planned/actual track
- Calculate drift
- Determine heading alteration to parallel track or intercept track at a nominated point
- Revise/confirm ETAs using latest GS or time/distance proportion
- Establish DR position using latest TR and GS
- **Monitor flight progress** with an in-flight navigation log
- **Monitor fuel consumption** and revise reserves
- **Plan in-flight diversions** around adverse weather and to suitable aerodromes

## Unit 2.03 — Meteorology syllabus

### 1.2.1 Local weather knowledge

Demonstrate knowledge of likely local occurrence of:
- Thunderstorms
- Low cloud
- Poor visibility
- Turbulence

And how these phenomena affect safe aeroplane operation.

### 1.2.2 Knowledge of forecasts and reports

- Understand weather forecasts, reports, and broadcasts pertinent to the area of operation
- Know terms and abbreviations used in forecasts and where to obtain decodes
- Demonstrate ability to obtain relevant forecasts

### 1.2.3 Significance of observations

Recognise signs indicating:
- Turbulence, thermals, dust devils
- Wind gradient, wind shear

Describe the effect of these phenomena on flight characteristics.

### 1.2.4 Atmospheric pressure

- Units of measure
- Variation with height
- Pressure altitude
- Effects of pressure altitude
- ICAO standard atmospheric pressure

### 1.2.5 Atmospheric temperature

- Units of measure
- Variation with height
- Density altitude
- Effects of density altitude
- ICAO standard atmospheric temperature

### 1.2.6 Pressure systems and fronts

- Depressions (low pressure)
- Anticyclones (high pressure)
- Cold and warm fronts
- General characteristics of pressure systems and fronts over Australia
- Horizontal pressure
- Isobars

### 1.2.7 Cloud classifications

Identify different cloud classifications and the weather associated with them.

### 1.2.8 Visibility

Effects on visibility of:
- Haze
- Smoke
- Fog

### 1.2.9 Turbulence

Knowledge and understanding of:
- Mechanical turbulence
- Terrain-induced turbulence
- Convectional turbulence
- Local winds
- Slipstream
- Wake turbulence

### 1.2.10 Wind

- Wind velocity
- Wind shear
- Wind gradient
- Backing and veering
- Sea breezes
- Foehn winds
- Valley winds
- Anabatic and katabatic winds

### 1.2.11 Mountain waves

- Conditions and severity at which they occur
- How they can affect flight conditions

### 1.2.12 Met reports and forecasts

Understand:
- METARs
- SPECIs
- TTF (Trend Type Forecast)
- TAF (Terminal Aerodrome Forecast)
- AFOR (Area Forecast)
- SIGMET
- AIRMET

## Study approach

Ground school for this endorsement maps to the PPL navigation and meteorology syllabus. The most efficient path:

1. Learn the navigation computation fundamentals (HDG/GS/drift/ fuel) as these underpin everything
2. Practice chart reading on local-area WAC and VTC — buy the paper charts
3. Study weather theory, then practice reading actual METARs and TAFs daily
4. Practise cross-country flight planning end-to-end using AIP materials
5. Work through past exam questions at your FTS

The 80% pass mark means you need genuine understanding, not just memorisation — focus on scenarios and "what would you do" problems.
