1.**1200 AGL (Above Ground Level):**
- This means the aircraft is**1200 feet directly above the terrain (ground)** immediately beneath it.
- It's a measure of _height_ above the physical surface.
- If the terrain elevation changes (e.g., flying over hills or valleys), the aircraft must climb or descend to maintain a constant AGL height.
- AGL is crucial for low-level operations, takeoffs, landings, and ensuring obstacle clearance. Radar altimeters directly measure AGL.

2.**1200 QNH (Altimeter setting based on local sea level pressure):**
- QNH is a specific barometric pressure setting used in aviation altimeters. When an altimeter is set to the current local QNH, it indicates the aircraft's**altitude above Mean Sea Level (MSL)**, assuming standard atmospheric conditions.
- So, 1200 QNH means the aircraft's altimeter reads 1200 feet, indicating it is approximately**1200 feet above Mean Sea Level**.
- This altitude remains constant relative to sea level, regardless of the terrain elevation below (assuming the aircraft maintains level flight and the QNH setting is correct).
- QNH is used for maintaining vertical separation between aircraft, flying airways, and adhering to airspace altitude restrictions, typically below a designated "Transition Altitude".

**Here's the Key Difference Illustrated:**

Imagine you are flying over an airfield that has an elevation of 500 feet MSL.

- If you are flying at**1200 feet AGL** over the airfield, your altitude above sea level (what your altimeter would show if set to QNH) would be 1200 ft (AGL) + 500 ft (airfield elevation) =**1700 feet MSL (or 1700 feet QNH)**.
- If you are flying at**1200 feet QNH** (meaning your altimeter reads 1200 ft MSL), your height above the airfield would be 1200 ft (MSL) - 500 ft (airfield elevation) =**700 feet AGL**.

**In summary:**

- **AGL** = Height above the ground directly below. Varies with terrain.
- **QNH** = Indicated Altitude above Mean Sea Level (based on local pressure). Constant relative to sea level, independent of terrain directly below.
