# AJF workshop 2024

_This half-day session will cover the changes in the media/information landscape in the past five years, and where it'll be two years from now. Here are the key themes:_

_The traditional media business model is broken and that forcing old media concepts into new digital formats is a mistake._

_The internet has transformed media to be user-centered, demand-driven, and interest-based._

_Attention is finite, but content is becoming infinite, especially with AI, posing a significant risk to the media industry._

_Consumers are not primarily interested in "journalism" as a concept; instead, they want solutions to their problems._

_To survive, media companies need to focus on solving specific customer problems rather than just producing content._

_What one-size-fits-one looks like in media as we shift towards niche, specialized content rather than one-size-fits-all approaches._

_A look at the role of creators/influencers and why they're picking away at the work that journalists have traditionally done, and what we can do about that._

_This presentation basically calls into question the assumptions we've been making about media -- how it works and how it matters, and the definition and function of journalism in it. The key takeaway is that our mindset that media is "mass" is a fallacy and journalism needs to be redefined under new constructs: user first, demand-led, and niche._

_We can explore more relevant metrics to measure success -- factors that go beyond merely citing page views, and are aligned and specific to value that's actually being created for the user._


**Summary from transcript**

1. Modern media needs to be user-centered, demand-driven, and interest-based. This was emphasized multiple times as a core principle.

2. The media landscape has been transforming for 30 years due to the internet. It's not a recent disruption.

3. Content creation alone is not enough. Media companies need to focus on solving specific problems for their audiences.

4. Understanding audience needs and intentions is crucial. Analytics alone don't tell you why people consume content.

5. The value of journalism lies in serving people's needs for information, not just in creating more content.

6. People don't buy products (or content), they buy outcomes - things like status, power, knowledge, etc.

7. Mainstream media assumptions about what audiences want may be flawed. It's important to actually ask and listen to audience needs.

8. Personal branding for journalists is becoming increasingly important as a way to build authority and career resilience.

9. AI and generative content pose both challenges and opportunities for journalism.

10. Smaller, niche media organizations that serve very specific audience needs may be better positioned for success than large legacy media companies.

11. The future of media isn't about creating more content, but about providing valuable, targeted information that helps people solve problems or make decisions.
