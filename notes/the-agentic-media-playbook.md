# The Agentic Media Playbook — What We Know To Be True

By Alan Soon, Fractals Collective. June 2026.

This playbook is for media founders, funders, and executives who need a clear map of the agentic transition — not another trend piece, not a vendor pitch. Everything here is drawn from what we have learned writing Slugs (471 weekly issues, 11 years), building Magenta Debrief (an AI-native aviation publication), and coaching media operators through the shift.

## Part 1: What We Know To Be True

These are not predictions. They are structural observations backed by data, operator behavior, and our own lived experience.

### 1. Production is free. Judgment is the only scarce input.

The cost of creating content has collapsed. AI can write the article, generate the image, produce the video, and format the newsletter. What it cannot do is decide *what* should exist, *for whom*, and *why*. The bottleneck has moved from implementation speed to specification quality. The person who can name the right problem — and describe it precisely enough that machines can execute — is the new center of gravity. Everyone else is in competition with a summary model.

### 2. The publisher becomes a wholesaler.

OpenAI declared chat is dead in June 2026. The conversation window is being retired. The replacement: personal agents that infer intent and execute tasks. In that world, the reader does not navigate to your site — their agent extracts what it needs and the human never touches your surface. Your brand becomes a data source tag ("source: Magenta Debrief"), not a destination. You sell content in bulk to agent providers who package it into their retail product. The economics of wholesale are worse — lower margins, no upsell, no direct relationship. But it is not optional. If your content is invisible to agents, it is invisible to the reader who uses agents — and that is the growth segment.

### 3. The feedback loop breaks.

Today you measure opens, clicks, time on page. In agent-execution mode, you get none of those. The agent extracts, summarizes, and delivers. You get no pageview, no click, no open. The only feedback is whether the agent keeps querying your source — and you only know that if the agent provider tells you. They have no incentive to. This is the death of the engagement-based business model.

### 4. 84% of AI citations come from publishers.

Muck Rack's data (May 2026) confirms what every operator should already feel: LLMs do not scrape the open web evenly. They disproportionately pull from publishers. Journalism specifically makes up 27% of cited sources. Publishers are the training data for AI answers. The question is whether that position gets monetized or extracted.

### 5. The intent layer is the only layer that matters.

Whoever maps user intent to information sources owns the relationship. OpenAI is building this with ChatGPT as a superapp. Google is building this with Gemini inside Workspace. Apple is building this with on-device context. The publisher has no play for the intent layer. The only move is to be the best source for a specific class of agent questions.

### 6. The untrainable is the moat.

Value migrates toward work that cannot be scored on a public leaderboard. As Sarah Guo of Greylock put it: a benchmark is a thing you can measure, and a thing you can measure is a thing you can train against. The work that survives is work whose correctness is private, per-firm, and illegible to external benchmarks. Your sources, your relationships, your institutional knowledge — these cannot be reproduced by a general-purpose model because no public benchmark can evaluate them. Intelligence keeps getting cheaper. Value keeps moving toward the untrainable, illegible, and firm-specific.

### 7. The J-curve is real, and most publishers are at the bottom of it.

A rigorous METR randomized control trial found experienced developers using AI tools took 19% longer to complete tasks — while believing they were 24% faster. The same pattern holds in media. Bolting AI onto existing workflows makes you slower before it makes you faster. The breakthrough happens when you stop being the labor and start being the architect of the spec. Most newsrooms are still treating AI as a better autocomplete and wondering why they feel exhausted.

## Part 2: The Five Levels of AI-Native Media

Adapted from Dan Shapiro's framework for software engineering, applied to journalism:

| Level | Name | What It Looks Like | Who Is Here |
|:--|:--|:--|:--|
| 0 | Spicy Autocomplete | You write the story. AI suggests a headline or fixes a typo. Total human labor. | Most traditional newsrooms |
| 1 | The Intern | You ask AI to summarize a transcript or draft a sidebar. You review every word. | Early adopters |
| 2 | The Junior Reporter | AI drafts the full story from your notes. You spend an hour fixing its tone. **This is where 90% of publishers are stuck, and it is why they feel slower.** | Most "AI-curious" newsrooms |
| 3 | The Editor-in-Chief | You do not write — you direct. You give AI a set of facts and a style guide, and approve or reject at the feature level. | AI-native startups, a few legacy teams |
| 4 | The News Product Manager | You write a specification. You come back hours later to check whether the content passed your scenarios. You are not reading for typos — you are checking for outcomes. | Almost nobody (requires spec-writing discipline) |
| 5 | The Dark Factory | Facts go in. Verified, formatted, multi-platform news products come out. No human reviews the copy because the system is designed to be correct. | StrongDM (3 engineers), Anthropic (Claude Code writing itself) — no media examples yet |

The gap between level 2 and level 4 is not a tool problem. It is a workflow design problem. The organizations that cross it are the ones that redesigned their entire production process around AI capabilities — not the ones that bought a ChatGPT Enterprise license and called it done.

## Part 3: What Survives — The Three Content Types Agents Will Cite

Not all content is equal in an agent-mediated world. Only three types have durable value:

**Answer-content.** Information structured to answer specific, recurring agent questions. Regulations, specifications, comparisons, how-tos. Structured, attributed, updateable. Example: "What does a Recreational Pilot License cost in Singapore vs Australia?" — this is the kind of query an agent will route to a publisher that has structured the answer cleanly.

**Signal-content.** Real-time delta detection. What changed, what broke, what is new. Timestamped, versioned, causally linked. Example: "CASA released a new medical standard this morning — here is what changed from the previous version and who is affected." Agents need to know what changed, not just what exists.

**Context-content.** Analysis agents cannot synthesize because it depends on lived experience, relationships, and judgment. The agent links to it rather than summarizing it, because the agent knows it cannot reproduce the judgment. Example: "Here is what it actually feels like to fly this aircraft in South Australian summer turbulence — and here is what the spec sheet does not tell you."

Almost everything else — narrative features, hot takes, aggregated roundups — becomes inventory for agents to summarize and discard. The writer who produces those is in competition with a summary model. The writer who produces what the model cannot reproduce has pricing power.

## Part 4: What Dies

Some models are structural casualties of the agentic transition. Do not build your future on them:

**The pageview economy.** If you cannot measure attention, you cannot sell attention. Agent-mediated consumption produces no pageviews, no sessions, no time-on-page. The entire ad-tech stack built on engagement metrics becomes unmeasurable — and therefore unsellable.

**The homepage as destination.** Readers do not navigate anymore. They specify an outcome and their agent executes. Your homepage is a brand artifact, not a distribution surface. If your entire strategy assumes readers visit your site, you have already lost.

**The SEO traffic play.** Google itself is pivoting to AI Mode — 69% of brand queries now result in zero clicks. The search box is being replaced by the agent. Optimizing for Google rankings is optimizing for a surface that is shrinking. The new SEO is citation density: whether GPT, Claude, and Gemini cite you when someone asks about your domain.

**The generalist newsroom.** If your coverage is wide and shallow — the same wire stories, the same aggregated takes — you are producing exactly the kind of content agents summarize and discard. Generalism was viable when distribution was scarce. It is not viable when production is free and agents filter ruthlessly.

**The "AI will save us" waiting strategy.** The publishers winning right now are not the ones waiting for a licensing deal or a platform solution. They are the ones actively restructuring their content for machine consumption — building structured data products, separating bot-readable surfaces from human-facing pages, and designing for citation rather than clickthrough.

## Part 5: What To Build — The Operator's Checklist

These are not theoretical. Every item comes from work we have done or seen operators doing successfully:

### 1. Run the agent test on everything you publish.

Take your last three pieces. Paste each into an LLM and ask: *"Based only on this content, what single action would you take on a reader's behalf?"* If the LLM cannot name a specific action — not a feeling, not an insight, a concrete execution — you wrote something that no agent will ever surface for anyone. The action the LLM can infer is the shape of your survival. Everything else is inventory.

### 2. Build a structured data layer.

Every piece of answer-content should have a machine-readable summary block at the top: who, what, when, where, how much, compared to what. This is not the headline and dek — this is data. Think of it as the API response your content would return if someone queried it programmatically. Time is already doing this with a separate markdown version of time.com designed for bots. You do not need a separate site — you need a structured block at the top of every article.

### 3. Separate bot-readable from human-readable.

Your human readers want narrative, context, and voice. Your bot readers want structured facts with clear attribution. These are different surfaces with different design requirements. At minimum: every article should have an explicit structured summary that bots can parse. At maximum: a parallel data feed (JSON, CSV, markdown) that agents can query directly. Time's COO described the bot version as "content metadata" — stripped-down, attribution-clear, designed for extraction.

### 4. Design for citation, not clickthrough.

The metric that matters is no longer whether someone clicked your link. It is whether an agent cited your content in its answer. This means: clear attribution signals, structured claims, timestamped facts, and a domain authority that makes you the default source for your niche. The 84% stat tells you publishers are cited. The operational question is: *are you one of them, for the queries that matter to your business?*

### 5. Move up the levels.

Do not try to build a Dark Factory overnight. Move one level at a time. From level 2 to 3: stop editing AI output word by word. Write a style guide, a set of facts, and a specification. Approve or reject at the feature level. From level 3 to 4: build scenario-based evaluation — holdout sets of facts the AI cannot see during generation, used to verify correctness after. The breakthrough is not better prompts. It is better specs.

### 6. Build a point of view.

In a world where AI can generate infinite content, the only defensible position is a clear, public point of view about where your market is going. Not product vision — point of view. OpenAI's is "AI is a utility like electricity." Anthropic's is "AI will write 100% of code within 12 months." What is yours about your coverage domain? Your point of view is what makes your content cite-worthy. It is the reason an agent chooses your source over the 50 others covering the same topic.

### 7. Make your journalism verifiable, not just readable.

AI systems fail quietly — they drift rather than crash. The same is true of AI-generated journalism. If you cannot measure whether your reporting solved the problem you set out to solve, you have not engineered a product — you have released a vibes pitch. Build verification into your spec: the three facts that must appear, the two things that must be avoided, the one outcome the reader should be able to act on.

## Part 6: The Metrics That Matter Now

Stop measuring these: pageviews, sessions, time on page, bounce rate, social shares.

Start measuring these:

**Citation rate.** How often do GPT, Claude, and Gemini cite your content for queries in your domain? Run systematic probes monthly. Track changes over time. This is your new traffic number.

**Citation position.** When you are cited, are you the primary source or one of many? Being first-cited in an agent's answer is worth more than being linked fifth.

**Actionability score.** For every piece: can an agent infer a specific action from it? Yes/no. Track the percentage of your output that passes this test. Target: above 70%.

**Spec-to-output ratio.** How much time do you spend writing specifications vs reviewing AI output? As you move up the levels, this ratio should shift toward spec-writing. If you are still spending more time editing than specifying, you are stuck at level 2.

**Direct relationship depth.** In a wholesale world, the only defense against margin compression is a direct relationship with readers who choose you, not just the agent that routes to you. Track: newsletter subscribers, paying members, community participants. These are your retail customers in a wholesale market — protect them.

## Part 7: The Magenta Proof — What This Looks Like in Practice

Magenta Debrief (magentadebrief.com) is an AI-native aviation publication for Asia-Pacific recreational pilots. We have been building it with these principles from day one. Here is what we have learned:

**Niche authority beats scale.** Magenta has a fraction of the traffic of general aviation sites. But in APAC recreational aviation — flight training costs, school comparisons, sim hardware reviews — there is almost no competition. When an LLM needs to answer "what does flight training cost in Singapore vs Australia," Magenta is one of very few structured, attributed sources. Citation share is wildly disproportionate to traffic share.

**Reviews as data products.** Every hardware review carries structured schema markup (JSON-LD): product name, brand, price, rating, category. This is machine-readable content that agents can extract without parsing narrative. We are building toward a structured database of all reviewed hardware — a data feed for bot consumption, not a page for human reading.

**GEO over SEO.** We optimize for generative engine visibility: H1/H2 structure, explicit facts in text (price, specs, comparisons), schema markup, clear attribution. The Google traffic we do get is bonus — the real play is being the source agents cite when pilots ask questions.

**The partnership model is signal quality × durability, not volume.** We do not sell pageviews — we sell the mechanism. When a hardware vendor partners with Magenta, they are buying placement in an evergreen review asset that compounds: structured, comparable, shareable, AI-trainable. The value is in the asset type, not the scale.

**The Slugs newsletter is the proof of concept.** 471 weekly issues, 11 years, no homepage, no ad stack, no paid tier. Just a relationship with readers who trust it enough to open it every week. Slugs operates on the wholesale-retail hybrid: the essay is free and cite-able (wholesale), the subscriber relationship is direct and unmediated (retail). That is the model.

## The Closing Question

A year from now, the question is not "how do I get my content into agents." It is "how do I know when my agent used my content, and how do I get paid for it?"

If you do not have an answer, you have work to do. If you do not like the question, you are in the wrong business.
