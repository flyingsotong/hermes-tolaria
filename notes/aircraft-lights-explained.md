These five switches control the exterior lights on a Cessna, each with a specific role for visibility and safety.


**BEACON Light**

The**BEACON** is a flashing red light, usually on the tail. It's turned on _before_ starting the engine to warn ground crew and other pilots that the aircraft is active. It stays on for the entire flight and is the last light turned off after shutting down the engine.


**LAND (Landing) Light**

The**LAND** light is a very bright, forward-facing white beam. Its main job is to illuminate the runway for takeoffs and landings at night. Because it's so bright, pilots also use it during the day (typically below 10,000 feet) to make the aircraft more visible to other planes, helping to prevent collisions. The red ring around the switch in the photo indicates this light is currently on.


**TAXI Light**

The**TAXI** light is a less-intense white light used to illuminate the path ahead while taxiing on the ground at night. It helps the pilot see the taxiway lines and avoid obstacles. It's switched off when the plane enters the runway for takeoff, and the landing light is switched on.


**NAV (Navigation) Lights**

**NAV** lights, also called position lights, show other aircraft your position and direction of travel. They are a set of three steady lights:

- A**red** light on the left (port) wingtip.

- A**green** light on the right (starboard) wingtip.

- A**white** light on the tail.

These are required to be on from sunset to sunrise.


**STROBE Lights**

**STROBE** lights are high-intensity, flashing white lights on the wingtips. They are the most powerful lights and serve as the primary anti-collision system, making the aircraft highly conspicuous. They are turned on when entering the runway for takeoff and switched off after exiting the runway upon landing. Pilots may turn them off in clouds to avoid the disorienting effect of the flashes reflecting back at them.
