These tendencies primarily affect single-engine, propeller-driven airplanes and are less pronounced or non-existent in multi-engine or jet aircraft.

- **Engine power is high:** This increases the torque and slipstream effects.

- **Airspeed is low:** This makes the aircraft more sensitive to the effects of slipstream and P-factor.

- **Angle of attack is high:** This increases the P-factor effect.


1. Torque

- **What it is:** The clockwise rotation of the propeller (as viewed from the cockpit) creates an equal and opposite force that tries to roll the airplane counter-clockwise (to the left).

- **When it's noticeable:** Most pronounced during takeoff and initial climb due to high engine power.

- **How to counteract:** Use right rudder to maintain coordinated flight.

2. Spiraling Slipstream

- **What it is:** The propeller's rotation creates a spiraling airflow that strikes the left side of the vertical tail, causing the nose to yaw left.

- **When it's noticeable:** Primarily during takeoff and climb when engine power is high and airspeed is low.

- **How to counteract:** Right rudder is essential to keep the nose straight.

3. P-Factor (Precession Asymmetrical Loading)

- **What it is:** At high angles of attack (nose up), the descending propeller blade on the right side generates more thrust than the ascending blade on the left, causing a left yaw.

- **When it's noticeable:** During slow flight, climbs, and low-speed maneuvers with high power settings.

- **How to counteract:** Maintain coordinated flight with rudder input.

4. Gyroscopic Precession

- **What it is:** A spinning propeller acts like a gyroscope. Pitching the nose up creates a force that results in a left yawing motion.

- **When it's noticeable:** Most apparent during tailwheel takeoffs and rapid pitch changes.

- **How to counteract:** Anticipate and apply appropriate rudder input.

**Practical Implications for Student Pilots**

- **Rudder Control:** Mastering rudder coordination is crucial to counteract these tendencies.

- **Practice:** Regularly practice maintaining coordinated flight during all phases of flight, especially takeoff, climb, slow flight, and maneuvers.

- **Awareness:** Be mindful of these tendencies and how they affect the aircraft.

- **Progressive Learning:** Your instructor will guide you through understanding and managing these forces.

**When each tendency is most prominent**

- **Torque:** Most noticeable during**high power settings**, especially during the takeoff roll and initial climb.

- **Spiraling Slipstream:** Also most pronounced during**takeoff and climb** when the engine is at high power and the**airspeed is low**.

- **P-Factor:** Primarily affects the aircraft during**high angles of attack**, such as during slow flight, climbs, and when maneuvering at low speeds with high power settings.

- **Gyroscopic Precession:** While it can occur during any pitch change, it's most noticeable during**abrupt maneuvers**, like a tailwheel takeoff or a sudden pitch up.
