# EiR model — case study research

---
## What's actually been done

The EiR model exists in three main forms, none of which is exactly what I'm describing.

**VC firm EiR** — the dominant version. A post-exit entrepreneur parks at a fund for 6–12 months, explores ideas, and either spins out a company or leaves. It's a holding pattern dressed up as a role.

**University EiR** — the Royal Society program is the most documented example. Entrepreneurs spend one day per week embedded in an academic department to help commercialize research.

**Corporate/Foundation EiR** — Dell's Ingrid Vanderveldt bridged Dell's corporate structure and the startup ecosystem, creating a $100M fund. The closest analog to my model is the **Global EiR program** in Michigan — funded by the William Davidson Foundation and the Ralph C. Wilson Jr. Foundation. Foundation pays. Entrepreneur embeds. Company benefits. Three-party structure.

---

## What actually worked

In the VC model, the EiR arrangement works best when the EiR has genuine execution instincts — not just ideas — and when the fund gives them real access to dealflow and decision-making, not just a desk and a stipend. Sachin Rekhi's account of his time at Trinity Ventures is the most honest primary source: value came from sitting in on pitches, understanding how investment decisions get made, and having unstructured time to vet ideas rigorously.

In the foundation-funded model (Global EiR), documented successes were companies that closed real funding rounds — PAXAFE raised close to $1M during their residency. Foundation support created legitimacy that opened doors the entrepreneur couldn't have opened alone.

The Royal Society model showed that EiR impact compounds over time — not in a single engagement, but across cohorts. Individual placements yielded "highly original" work, but most projects needed further development to create real commercial value. One day a week is thin.

---

## What was overpromised

**Role ambiguity kills momentum.** The most common complaint: "we didn't have an exact job description — we'd figure it out together." Without a clear mandate, the embedded operator becomes a well-meaning observer.

**Authority without equity is fragile.** EiRs in corporate settings routinely hit this wall. Expected to drive change but lacking positional authority or formal reporting lines. The companies that worked best gave EiRs a real domain — a specific decision, team, or outcome.

**The VC model declined because it was too advisory.** By the early 2020s, VC firms moved toward "talent buying" — hiring entrepreneurs directly — because the EiR arrangement produced fewer genuine outcomes.

**Short timelines were chronically underestimated.** 25% success rate for EiR-to-founder transitions at VC firms. For my model — driving execution inside an existing business rather than starting one — 4–6 months is tight but more tractable.

**Foundation-funded models can create perverse incentives.** When a third party is paying, the company can treat the EiR as a free resource with low accountability. Contractual accountability on the company side is essential — even if they're not writing the check.

---

## The pattern underneath all of this

Every successful EiR engagement had three things in common: a specific problem the EiR was brought in to solve, genuine access to decision-making, and a clear exit condition. Every failed or mediocre one had ambiguity on at least two of those three.
