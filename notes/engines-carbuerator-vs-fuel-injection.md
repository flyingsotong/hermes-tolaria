Both systems have their place in aviation, with the choice often depending on the aircraft's intended use, cost considerations, and pilot preference.
**Carburetor Systems**
Advantages:
- Simpler design, easier to maintain
- Generally less expensive
- Fewer components to fail
- Easier to troubleshoot
- More familiar to mechanics
Disadvantages:
- Susceptible to carburetor icing
- Less precise fuel/air mixture
- More affected by changes in attitude
- Performance varies more with altitude
- Requires carb heat system
- More prone to fuel starvation in unusual attitudes
**Fuel Injection Systems**
Advantages:
- More precise fuel/air mixture
- Better fuel economy
- No carb ice formation
- More consistent performance at varying altitudes
- Better performance in unusual attitudes
- Smoother operation
- Better throttle response
- Each cylinder gets equal fuel distribution
Disadvantages:
- More complex system
- Higher initial and maintenance costs
- More difficult to start when engine is hot
- Vapor lock can be an issue
- Requires more complex troubleshooting
- May require fuel return line
**Starting Procedures**
Carbureted:
- Prime as needed
- Throttle set
- Mixture rich (When a pilot sets the mixture control to "rich," they're increasing the ratio of fuel to air in the combustion chambers, creating a fuel-rich mixture. This means more fuel is being supplied relative to the amount of air.)
- Start engine
Fuel Injected:
- Requires specific hot start procedure
- More critical fuel pump operation
- May need to clear vapor lock
- More attention to mixture control
