# Coaching - Mohamed El Aassar

**April 2, 2025**

**What Mohamed wanted to achieve:**

- Mohamed reached out because he was questioning whether his next job should be in journalism, his profession for the last 13 years.
- He felt disconcerted as this was the first time since childhood he questioned his purpose and career path.
- He was grappling with the uncertainty of the journalism profession, which he described as "dying," and recent negative experiences in senior roles where relationships broke down.
- He was interested in exploring both advancing his career and potentially entrepreneurship.

**What Mohamed discovered:**

- Accepted that the journalism industry, as he knew it, is dead or dying and that he should likely consider other options.
- Recognized the challenge of being perceived solely as a "journalist" when applying for roles outside the industry and the need to reframe his experience.
- Acknowledged the advice to define his work based on skills rather than his job title to communicate his value to potential employers in other fields.
- Left the conversation feeling "less confused," indicating the direct approach was helpful for him.

**What could have been done better:**

- Exploring feelings: while the assessment of the industry's decline was quickly validated, there might have been an opportunity to explore Mohamed's feelings (eg. grief, loss of identity) about potentially leaving a profession he'd been in for 13 years.
- Deep dive into entrepreneurial idea: Mohamed's idea for a user-owned platform was acknowledged but the conversation quickly pointed out the challenges. A deeper exploration of the idea itself and Mohamed's passion for it could have been valuable before jumping to validation hurdles.
- Balancing directness: Mohamed found the directness useful. However, the strong stance that "the profession is dead" early on could have potentially shut down other avenues of exploration within the changing media landscape.
- Validating past experiences: the challenging experiences with management were acknowledged but quickly moved past. Spending more time acknowledging the difficulty of those situations might have been beneficial before focusing solely on next steps.
