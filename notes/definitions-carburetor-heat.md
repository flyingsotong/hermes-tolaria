# Definitions Carburetor Heat

Carb heat is a system used on reciprocating piston engines (such as those found in airplanes) to manage the temperature of air entering the engine's carburetor. Here’s what it does:

1.**Prevention of Ice Formation**: One of the primary purposes of carb heat is to prevent ice from forming inside the carburetor. When an aircraft flies through cold, moist air at high altitudes, moisture can condense and freeze on the intake system, potentially blocking airflow or causing the engine to quit. Carb heat warms the incoming air, preventing this freezing.

2.**Engine Performance**: By warming the air before it enters the carburetor, carb heat helps maintain optimal performance in cold weather conditions. It ensures that the air-to-fuel mixture is at the correct ratio for efficient combustion, even when temperatures drop.

3.**Precautionary Measure**: Carb heat can also be used as a precautionary measure to avoid issues related to moisture and temperature changes, especially during takeoff and landing phases of flight when the engine is operating under varying conditions.

To use carb heat, pilots adjust the mixture control (which controls the amount of fuel in relation to air) while engaging the carb heat handle or lever. This allows a portion of the exhaust gases to flow through the carburetor, warming it up effectively.
