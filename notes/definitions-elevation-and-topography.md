# Definitions Elevation and topography

**1. MSL (Mean Sea Level)**

The average height of the ocean's surface taken from multiple measurements over time. It serves as the standard reference point for measuring elevations worldwide. When you see elevation numbers on maps or in aviation charts, they're typically given in MSL. For example, if Denver is said to be at 5,280 feet elevation, that's 5,280 feet MSL.

**2. Field Elevation (also called airport elevation)**

The height of the highest point of an airport's usable runways, measured in feet above mean sea level (MSL). This is a fixed reference point that doesn't change and is crucial for airport operations and flight planning.

**3. AGL (Above Ground Level)**

The actual height above the ground directly below an object or aircraft, regardless of the ground's elevation above sea level. For example, if an aircraft is flying at 5,000 feet MSL over terrain that's at 2,000 feet MSL, the aircraft's AGL would be 3,000 feet.
A practical example: If you're flying near a mountain:

- The mountain's height would typically be given in MSL (say 14,000 feet MSL)

- Your aircraft's altimeter shows your altitude in MSL

- But for obstacle clearance, you need to know your AGL height to ensure safe separation from the terrain
