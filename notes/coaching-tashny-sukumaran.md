Based on the transcript of your coaching call with Tashny, here's my analysis:
**What Tashny wanted to achieve**
At the start of the call, Tashny expressed that she was at a crossroads in her career transition. She had already moved from full-time journalism to consulting work in sustainability and human rights, but was struggling with whether to:
1. Fully transition into consultancy work
2. Find a middle ground where she could continue reporting on stories she's interested in occasionally without undermining her consultancy work
As she put it: "I'm sort of at a crossroads on whether I should go fully into the consultancy work I do or is there a middle ground where I can continue reporting on stories that I'm interested in sort of ad hoc piecemeal way and not have that color my consultancy work."
**What Tashny discovered during the coaching call**
Throughout the conversation, Tashny had several key realizations:
1.**Identity issues with journalism**: She recognized she was emotionally attached to her journalist identity and found pride in writing articles that was hard to let go of.
2.**Skill articulation problems**: She struggled to explain her skillset beyond being seen as "just a writer," noting: "I have a difficult time explaining my skill set, even though I've upskilled in many ways, practically and sort of academically."
3.**Branding challenges**: She realized she needed to reframe her experience from a human rights perspective rather than a journalism one. She acknowledged journalists are "very superstitious" and hold onto unnecessary "sacred cows."
4.**Undervaluing significant achievements**: She discovered she had buried important accomplishments (like co-drafting the human rights chapter for Malaysia's foreign policy plan) instead of highlighting them.
5.**Client value proposition**: She came to understand she needed to articulate what value she brings to clients rather than focusing on her past journalism credentials.
6.**Need for complete rebranding**: By the end of the call, she seemed to accept that to be taken seriously as a consultant, she might need to "divorce [herself] fully for at least a couple years" from journalism.
**What you missed as a coach**
While the coaching session was largely effective, there were a few opportunities you might have missed:
1.**Concrete action steps**: The conversation provided excellent insights but didn't culminate in a clear action plan. At the end, you could have asked Tashny what her first 3 steps would be based on your discussion.
2.**Exploring her unique selling proposition**: While you touched on her need to articulate client value, you could have spent more time helping her define her unique selling proposition in the human rights consulting space.
3.**Addressing impostor syndrome**: There were hints that Tashny might be experiencing impostor syndrome ("Am I not starting a little bit late?"). This could have been explored more deeply.
4.**Networking strategies**: Given her career transition, providing guidance on how to build a new professional network in her target industry would have been valuable.
5.**Follow-up structure**: When she mentioned quarterly check-ins, you didn't provide a clear framework for what those might look like or how they could support her transformation.
6.**Setting metrics for success**: The conversation could have benefited from establishing how Tashny would measure success in her rebranding efforts over the next 6-12 months.
Overall, your coaching helped Tashny realize she needs to let go of her journalist identity to succeed as a consultant and highlighted the importance of reframing her experience to emphasize her human rights expertise rather than her journalism background. You effectively challenged her assumptions and helped her see how to better position herself in the market.
