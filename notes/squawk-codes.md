# Squawk codes

**7700** — General emergency 
**7600** — Radio communication failure 
**7500** — Unlawful interference


-**1200:** Civil VFR flights in class E or G airspace in Australia.
-**7700: General Emergency:** This is the most critical and widely known emergency squawk. It signals that the aircraft is facing a serious issue, such as engine failure, a medical emergency on board, or significant technical malfunction. When a pilot enters 7700 into the transponder, it immediately alerts all ATC facilities in the area, ensuring the flight is given priority handling, including a cleared path for landing and emergency services on standby.
***7600: Lost Communications:** This code indicates that an aircraft has lost its ability to communicate with air traffic control. While the crew may still be able to hear transmissions from the ground, they cannot respond. By squawking 7600, the pilots alert ATC to the situation, allowing controllers to clear airspace around the aircraft and use alternative methods to provide landing clearance, such as light gun signals from the control tower.
-**7500: Unlawful Interference (Hijacking):** This is the most serious of the emergency codes, reserved for situations of unlawful interference, including hijacking. It is a discreet way for the flight crew to signal a threat without alerting the perpetrators. When this code is detected, air traffic controllers will notify security and military authorities, and the aircraft will be monitored with extreme caution.
**Visual Flight Rules (VFR)**
For pilots flying under Visual Flight Rules (VFR), where they are primarily navigating by sight rather than under direct ATC control for their entire flight, a couple of standard squawk codes are very common.
-**1200 (United States and Canada):** This is the standard squawk code for VFR flights in North America. It tells air traffic controllers that the aircraft is operating under VFR and is not on a specific, controller-assigned flight plan. While squawking 1200, the aircraft is still visible on radar, allowing for traffic advisories if the pilot is in communication with ATC.
***7000 (Europe):** Similar to 1200 in North America, 7000 is the standard VFR squawk code used in most European countries. It serves the same purpose of identifying the aircraft as operating under VFR.
