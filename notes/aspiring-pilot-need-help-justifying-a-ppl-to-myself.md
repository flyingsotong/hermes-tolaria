# Aspiring pilot... need help justifying a PPL to myself

**December 21, 2024**

<https://www.reddit.com/r/flying/comments/1hiz156/aspiring_pilot_need_help_justifyingppl_to/>

**I wrote on Reddit:**

_I recognize that I'm probably going to get trolled for posting this. But here's the thing, I want to get my PPL -- I need some help justifying it to myself._

_I'm in my 50s. I've had a great career, and now I'm in a position to plan for the next few chapters of my life... and as far as I can tell, my health is ok._

_So that means looking into a PPL, which I've always wanted._

_I understand that this is an expensive hobby (which I can afford). I don't necessarily want to make a new career out of this._

_I have a hard time telling myself that it's ok to do this one expensive thing for myself -- not for my family, not for friends, not for community. Just for me._

_My wife is ok with this. And no one is stopping me from doing this, but myself._

_How do I reconcile this? Have you been down this road before? How did you tell yourself that this is ok?_


**Major Themes from Responses**

**Overwhelming Support**

- Nearly all respondents strongly encouraged you to pursue your PPL
- Many shared similar experiences of starting flying in their 50s or later
- Several emphasized "you only live once" and avoiding future regrets

**Ways to Give Back**

- Many suggested using your pilot's license for charitable work like:
- Angel Flights (medical transport)
- Pilots N Paws (animal transport)
- Young Eagles (youth aviation programs)
- Giving rides to kids and aviation enthusiasts

**Practical Advice**

- Start with a discovery flight to see if you enjoy it
- Get your medical certificate first before starting training
- Consider joining a flying club to reduce costs
- Many recommended getting an instrument rating after PPL
- Expect about 80% of student pilots don't complete their training
- Budget for significant study time beyond just flight hours

**Cost Considerations**

- Most acknowledged it's expensive but suggested viewing it as a hobby
- Several compared it to other expensive hobbies like boats or sports cars
- Many emphasized that if you can afford it and your wife supports it, there's no reason not to proceed

**Personal Growth**

- Many cited the intellectual and personal challenge as rewarding
- Several mentioned it's never too late to learn something new
- Multiple pilots shared how flying enriched their retirement years

The overall consensus was strongly supportive, with many respondents sharing their own experiences of starting flying later in life and having no regrets about their decision. They emphasized that with financial means and family support, the main obstacle appears to be your own hesitation, which they widely encouraged you to overcome.

On Taking the Leap:

> "You get one chance to have the life you want. Do the thing." - ShieldPilot

On Giving Back:

> "Will it help to assuage your guilt if you tell yourself that after you're a pilot, you'll use your power for good? There are lots of charitable things you can do as a pilot -- transport non-emergency sick people to their treatments, transport pets, give rides to kids..." - phliar

On Being "Selfish":

> "I always dreamed of flying, but career, spouse, family, and an unending series of obligations meant you are always in service to others... So, it is time to be selfish. Say it out loud. I am being selfish. Then be selfish... You have less than 1/3 of your life left. Of that, you can only fly for 10-20 years of it (maybe)." - Valuable-Ratio8073

On the Reality of Training:

> "It's expensive. Figure $15k to start and a couple thousand a year to fly a bit and keep current. It's very stressful. If you want a relaxing hobby this ain't it... It's physically uncomfortable. Hot plane. Cold plane. Flying into direct sunlight. Small seat... But if you dig that go for it." - vertexnormal

On the Rewards:

> "I'm just like you, 50 years old. Just got my sport pilot license last year... Bought a new Tecnam P2008 and a hangar in the DFW area. I now bore holes in the sky, fly with my son and occasionally my instructor for fun and food and new challenges. Haven't regretted a single bit of it." - radioref

Practical Perspective:

> "If you had started training a year ago, you'd be a pilot by now. If that were the case, would you currently be happy you had done that? A year from now, what will you be saying about your current self?" - BWEJ

On Future Regrets:

> "You are 80 years old. You are looking back at your life. NO REGRETS! How are you going to feel if you don't?" - leftseat19
