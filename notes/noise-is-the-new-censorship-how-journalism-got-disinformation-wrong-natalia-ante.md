- Highlights

 - In November, as the [Sam Altman](Sam%20Altman.md) drama unraveled in all of its jaw-dropping glory, I watched it — popcorn in hand — from my unlikely new home in Palo Alto, California.

Four months prior, I had arrived in Silicon Valley from Georgia (country, not the state) to join a small but mighty cohort of journalists at Stanford University. Every year, Stanford’s John S. Knight Journalism Fellowship brings journalists from across the world and the U.S. and tasks them with re-imagining the future of our embattled industry.

Freed from the relentless daily pressure of our newsrooms and placed under the equally relentless California sun, we tackle, individually and as a group, some of the biggest challenges facing journalism today. We couldn’t be in a better place. And I am not even talking about the weather.

Silicon Valley, or rather the technology it has birthed, has played a key role in creating the very problems we are trying to solve: the demise of journalism’s old business models, proliferation of viral disinformation and global rise of authoritarian populists, for whom journalists are public enemies number one.

Over the last five years, as democracy nosedived around the world, we journalists have all felt, on a visceral, almost physical level the rapid tightening of a space in which we are allowed to operate.

Weeks before arriving in California, I spent three days with hundreds of participants of Zeg Fest, the annual storytelling festival we host in the Georgian capital Tbilisi. Zeg Fest is all about getting out of silos and bringing storytellers from all industries under the same roof. Journalists curate the conversations, but as they spoke, the profession’s collective grim reality came into a sharp focus.

Zeg Storytelling Festival is an annual live journalism event that brings together storytellers from across different industries. Photo credit: Zeg Fest

Our Ukrainian colleagues described the horrors of the war and the dangers of self-censorship it has brought. Russian, Afghan and Belorusians talked about the nightmare of working from exile. The Hungarians told us about the oligarchs taking over the media. Kenyans and Ugandans told stories of proliferation of disinformation across the African continent. Americans fretted about Trump’s possible return to power and Georgians, the hosts, talked about the rising authoritarianism in their once proudly free society.

The stories all had different roots, languages, flavors but one thing in common: technology created in Silicon Valley.

For me, my newly found proximity to our era’s mightiest power brokers has underlined just how little influence we, journalists, have on them. Silicon Valley — as the openAI saga showed, continues to live by its own, mercurial rules. Most conversations I have here, don’t dwell on the troubles of people whose lives are maimed by algorithms they can’t control. Instead they focus on the exciting frontiers of generative AI. “Collateral damage,” one ex-Googler told me when I challenged him. He meant it well, as in: the long term improvements will make it worth it.

Whenever I cross Stanford’s stunning, sprawling campus, I wonder whether this is what ancient Rome felt like: the place of unspeakable financial and intellectual abundance, infinitely exciting possibilities and… terrifying detachment from the rest of the world.

When I first arrived, I wondered how journalism can change its relationship with the big platforms like Google. Now I am leaning towards thinking that the only way it can survive is by weaning itself off algorithmic dependency on them.

Most people in the industry won’t agree. In part, because of journalism’s dependance on distribution but also because companies like Google are largely seen as friends of the news industry.

To me, the relationship resembles a dysfunctional love affair rather than a friendship. In it, we, journalists, are the chasers. We complain relentlessly and blame tech companies for destroying our business models, yet we want them to stay. We look at them with disdain, yet we can’t hide our adoration. It’s a “we hate them, but can’t stop texting” kind of dynamic.

The platforms’ and journalism’s collective “fight” against disinformation is a great case study of this dysfunction.

Disinformation is the industry’s collective obsession, and I admit, a real problem. I have spent years covering it, first for the BBC and then, as a beat for Coda Story, the newsroom I co-founded in 2017. As the industry, I believe we made a terrible mistake when we framed disinformation as a “fake news“ problem. For a long time, focus on the fake news aspect of the disinformation crisis made us, journalists, reactive, defensive and focused on symptoms, rather than underlying causes of disinformation.

Tech companies encouraged this trend, both narratively and financially. A whack-a-mole game of fact checking and content moderation made social media giants like Meta or YouTube look like they were tackling the issue. Journalism and tech became partners in “combating” disinformation. It became an accepted practice for newsrooms around the world to take money from Facebook to provide fact-checking services.

An alternative scenario would involve doubling down on the actual story: the real-life damage that Facebook’s business model, built on monetizing loudest and most obnoxious voices, was doing to communities around the world.

It took a genocide in Myanmar and a small group of incredibly courageous, dedicated journalists like Maria Ressa in the Phillipines, for the narrative to begin to crack. But ultimately it was Meta that “broke up” with the industry and pulled all of its journalism funding. They decided they no longer cared about the news.

Taking money from tech companies continues to be an accepted industry practice. There is very little research on how it affects coverage of tech, because so many of the academic institutions, such as London School of Economics, Berkman Klein Center or Reuters Institute for the Study of Journalism at Oxford University, that would conduct such research have a long history of receiving major big tech funding, including from Google and Meta.

There are, of course, countless ways digital technology changed our lives for the better. My favorite is that it gave a voice to those who never had it before. I accept that dozens of brilliant mission driven journalism outfits, including my own, wouldn’t exist without audiences we found through search engines and social media networks.

But new voices are a good thing only if they are heard. The problem with tech platforms is that they never brought new voices into a conversation. They brought them into a shouting match, in which the loudest, nastiest, the most outrageous are always poised to win.

This deafening noise of the information ecosystem, I believe, is a huge reason why journalism — and by journalism I mean high quality insightful, inclusive reporting — lost its role as a curator of a public conversation. It’s the element of the disinformation crisis we, journalists, should have been combatting all along. Noise is the new censorship. It is what builds the walls that split millions of communities around the world, polarizing our societies beyond recognition. It is — if allowed to take over — what will eventually render us unable to solve any dispute — be it wars in Ukraine or Gaza, global warming, or organized crime.

Trying to figure out how quality journalism can punch through the noise of the digital ecosystem is, in my view, journalism’s most pressing challenge. It is also the focus of much of my work at Stanford.

If you are smirking and wishing me luck, I’ll take it! The unfolding AI revolution is about to change the information ecosystem beyond recognition. The AI could make the disinformation problem exponentially bigger, the noise may get even louder.

But the sunshine — and the California optimism — are rubbing off. I think the same technology that could render us extinct, could also offer solutions that we have never even thought of before. And maybe, just maybe, AI could be the way for journalism to gain back its ability to curate the public debate with high quality, carefully reported, thoughtful and inclusive information.

To get there, those of us who care about preserving democratic institutions first need to band together against the algorithms that censor reason. Journalism must stop taking money from companies like Google and instead adopt another one of Silicon Valley’s greatest assets: its audacious imagination.
