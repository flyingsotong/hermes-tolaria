# Self-reflection notes by Bhanupriya Rao

**Session 4: Safety**


In the last session we spoke about why swimming with Eric was an enjoyable experience. The main reason was that of all my other coaches at different points of time, Eric treated me like an athlete. What does this mean and why is that important.

What does being an athlete mean to me?

1.**Be**: As a child I was bullied and body shamed at home and school. So felt very under confident with a slouch ( which i carried until recently until swimming set that right). But being under confident and appearing as one in public was also not expected of me. So, essentially even if I felt unsafe and fear of bullying, I had to pretend to not care and soldier on. Who was I? It was very confusing. In school I earned the nickname “Budhiya” ( Old woman) who will not move and found being stationery comforting. Somehow this continued much into adulthood where moving felt like burdensome, body felt tired. So, now if I am able to smash targets upon targets and someone considers me as an athlete, to me, it means someone was ‘seeing’, what I was deep inside and not what I appeared to be. This matters a lot to me.

2.**Powerful**: I have always felt very powerless, fighting against a system stacked against me. I always wondered how people soldier on, rise above their circumstances. For me, sportspersons had the ability to do so and I marveled at that. I have watched hours of Paraolympic programming and wondered why I am not able to rise above mine. Being an athlete finally gave me the power over myself. The fact that someone saw me as an athlete made me feel in control of myself.

3.**What I wanted**: There are many things I did in life but never carried on or gave up midway. Its because I didn't enjoy them or feel they were my own purpose. It was what made others give me the recognition and validity. If I didn't do or achieve those, I wasn’t a person. Being an athlete was not what someone else decided for me. It was what I felt I wanted to do and when Eric articulated that Eric, instead of taking it easy, actually pushed me towards achieving it. On bad days, he didn’t relax rules for me but he wasn’t hard on me as well. I swam my best when I was in pain or exhausted. I hit the 1000m non stop mark last week, 4 days ahead of my target date on a day that I had massive lower back pain. I love pushing myself now because it teaches me to know what I'm capable of and that is whatever I want.

4.**Personhood**: Being an athlete has restored my personhood. And that is a very safe feeling. To know you exist as a real person and not as an imagination of expectation of others.

What does a coaching manual for Bhanu look like.

1.**Respect the person**: Simple as that. A person is a person first. Listen to what they are saying. A person is not a daughter, wife, employee or something or the other. An athlete is themselves and not a being in relation to something. That is empowering.

2.**Give them autonomy**: When I first started training with Eric, it was to just do some form of exercise and finally learn the skill I was dreading. When i first told him it might be good to set goals and said 100 m in total by March, I thought he was joking. Mainly because no one had ever set a non academic goal for me. That trust was important. But it came when I asked for it and it was not rubbished as a fantasy.

3.**Trust them**: As a coach your trust is very important as they come without any trust in themselves. You trust enables them and helps them dive deep within. Swimming (or anything)  is not a physical act but a psychological one of finding oneself.

**What does safety mean?**

1. Respecting the personhood

2. No imposition: Not asking to be someone they would approve of

3. Not saying you are worth only if …………….. This is a slippery slope because if you keep achieving what they want for you to be loved or respected, the goalposts just keep changing. And they know they can keep doing it with you so they can have control over you.

**How does the idea of safety link with business goals as an entrepreneur**

1.**Lack of personhood**was keeping me away from believing the USP/ value we are creating.**Validation wasn’t believed because it was not the ‘ideal’ that I was imagining.**No achievement meant anything because it was not ‘That’ and goalposts keep shifting.

2. Valuing what we have: I have had many challenges with building the team but now I am getting there. It's not perfect but there is a sense of purpose and cohesiveness. Im also not beating myself up for the mistakes in hiring because i'm also learning. Earlier I would look at other orgs and say why can't we have those teams?**Now I value what we have and build on that.**

3. Setting my own goals and targets:**Behanbox will not be unlike anything else because it isnt. This sense of safety is giving em the confidence to say this.**We are charting our own path and our own success metrics.

4.**I feel safe and discovered the person within that I love which translates to love everything that I love to do in a true manner.**Earlier I was as doing things that I love without really finding joy in it. Because I had forgotten how to find joy. Now I love what I do with all its challenges.
