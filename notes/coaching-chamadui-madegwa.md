**January 27, 2025**

The conversation revealed Nelly is a talented journalist who has been diminishing her own achievements due to not following a traditional journalism path. She started writing in 2019 and has already secured significant opportunities, including a Pulitzer fellowship, yet consistently frames her accomplishments with caveats about her non-traditional background.

A transformative insight came when she realized journalism doesn't need to be confined to written articles. This revelation emerged when discussing her community work — she had been seeing her community development activities as competing with journalism, rather than recognizing them as potentially complementary forms of informing and serving her community.

Her approach to learning stood out — she travels eight hours by bus to attend journalism workshops, takes courses, seeks mentorship, and consistently finds ways to develop new skills. However, she wasn't giving herself credit for this dedication to professional development.

The discussion helped her recognize that her data-driven approach and ability to explain complex topics (like COVID-19) in accessible language are valuable strengths, not limitations of her unconventional background.

**Areas that could have been explored more deeply:**

1. Burnout management: Nelly mentioned experiencing episodes of burnout, particularly after facing backlash from misinformation-focused stories. This deserved more exploration about specific triggers and potential prevention strategies.
2. The science-journalism interface: Given her science background helped her effectively cover COVID-19, there could have been more discussion about how to leverage this unique perspective in her future work.
3. Financial sustainability: While she mentioned that "journalism has figured out a way to fund itself," this seems like an area of anxiety that could have been explored more thoroughly, especially regarding long-term career sustainability.
4. Community integration: The fascinating tension between her roles as a journalist and community member could have been examined more deeply, particularly how these dual roles might create a unique form of journalism.
5. Impact measurement: Given her interest in serving her community, there could have been more discussion about how she measures and evaluates the impact of her work.
6. Time management: With multiple projects, community involvement, and skill development activities, exploring how she prioritizes and manages these competing demands would have been valuable.
7. Support systems: The conversation could have explored what support systems she has or needs to maintain this level of commitment and manage the emotional demands of investigative journalism.

The coaching successfully helped Nelly reframe her perspective on her journalism career, but there were opportunities to dig deeper into practical strategies for sustainability and growth. The most powerful moment came when she realized that her community engagement work could be seen as a form of journalism rather than competition to it.
