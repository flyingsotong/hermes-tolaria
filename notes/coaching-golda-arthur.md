**October 29, 2025 (Session 4/4)**

This session was a victory lap. Golda didn't come to the call with questions; she came with **answers, a major ($20,000) win, and a concrete 2026 business plan.** Your work together is complete because she has fully transitioned from a stuck, burnt-out creator into a confident media founder.

**Where she found progress and confidence**

- **She's a business owner, not just a podcaster.** She opened by saying, "everything... has helped me to think much more like a person running a media business." She has moved past creating a podcast to running a brand.
- **She's data-driven and realistic.** She's no longer chasing vanity metrics. She understands the Apple bump was fleeting, but her **1,000 true fans** (her number, not yours) are her real asset. She's learned to "weight" metrics, valuing her 70% newsletter open rate over raw downloads.
- **She has a sustainable 2026 plan.** She has a clear, confident vision: an "always-on" (every other week) podcast, listener-generated content, and a clear "why" for this cadence. Most importantly, she's building in breaks (August and December off) to prevent the burnout that brought her to you.
- **She's executing on new formats.** The workshop is no longer a "what if." It's happening this Thursday. She proved the concept and even navigated the tricky pricing issue with a "pay as you wish" model for her first "ambassadors."
- **She's past her identity crisis.** This is the biggest win. She confirmed: **"I haven't used the word journalism once in this conversation."** She has fully let go of the label that was paralyzing her.

**Her big "aha" moment**

Her biggest "aha" moment was articulating her core creative mission: **"The ugly truth beautifully told."**

This was the "penny dropping" for her. It's the unifying theory for her entire ecosystem. It's why her work resonates, why "wellness" feels like a "veneer" to her, and why her listeners feel so seen. It also powerfully explains why the work is so taxing on her personally — it requires her to engage with "grief," "brokenness," and "ugliness" head-on.

**How to conduct a great final coaching call**

Your final call was an excellent model for how to wrap up a coaching engagement. You didn't just end the contract; you co-created a satisfying conclusion that solidified her progress.

1. Look back: reflect and cement the transformation. Start by reflecting on her journey, which prompts the client to see and state their own progress. Always **mirror the "before" state** to allow the client to articulate the "after" state in their own words.
2. Look here: ground the wins in present reality. Check in on her current capacity. Your prompt — "How are you taking care of yourself as you open yourself up to more of these stories?" — showed you were listening to the person, not just the business plan. A final call must address the **human sustainability** of the new plan, not just the business logistics.
3. Look forward: solidify the future vision. Ask the client to define their own vision of success for the year ahead. This shifts ownership from you back to them.
4. The final summary: name the change. Always end by summarizing the client's single biggest internal shift. Golda's final "aha" — that she hadn't said "journalism" once — was the perfect confirmation that this was the most important work you did together.

**How to structure the fourth and final wrap-up call**

Structure the call in three parts: Look Back, Look Here, Look Forward.

Then finalize the mission: "As of today, what is the official, one-sentence mission that guides the entire Overlooked ecosystem?"

Part 3: Look Forward — ask: "Our sessions are ending. What system will you put in place to keep yourself accountable and get the support you need?" Define success for 2026, then close: "You came into this feeling stuck and are leaving with a multi-platform business plan, a clear mission, and a renewed sense of excitement. You have everything you need to build this."

**May 14, 2024**

Based on the conversation, Golda got several valuable things out of this coaching call:

1. Clarity on her bigger vision and mission beyond just the podcast itself. The coach helped her reframe her work as building a community around overlooked women's health issues, rather than just producing a podcast.
2. Specific ideas for next steps to build that community, like hosting small in-person meetup dinners, collecting email addresses, and potentially an event down the line.
3. Encouragement and validation that what she is doing is important journalistic and educational work, even if it doesn't feel like traditional journalism to her.
4. Practical tips like monetization ideas through branded content partnerships that align with her vision.
5. A boost in confidence from articulating her goals out loud and having the coach see the broader potential in her work.
6. Motivation to take small concrete next steps, like the meetup dinner, to start bringing her vision to life in an intimate, low-risk way.

While the coach provided valuable insights overall, there are a few areas where the coaching could be improved:

- Push for more concrete goal-setting — the coach helped Golda articulate a broader vision, but didn't nail down specific measurable goals for building the community, growth targets for the email list, target numbers for event attendance, etc.
- Dig deeper into challenges and blockers — the coach highlighted Golda's lack of confidence in "selling" as a potential blocker, but didn't probe much deeper into what her specific fears or hurdles were.
- Provide more tactical suggestions — while ideas like meetups and email lists were mentioned, the coach could have provided more specific tactical tips based on their own experience.
- Suggest additional resources — the coach didn't point Golda to any external resources, communities, coaches or consultants that could support her journey.
- Set accountability steps — no specific accountability steps were discussed, like a follow-up call or Golda committing to take certain actions by certain dates.
