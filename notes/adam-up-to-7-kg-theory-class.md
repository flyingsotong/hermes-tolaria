**Aeroplane**

> "Aeroplane" means a power-driven heavier-than-air aircraft, deriving its lift in flight primarily from aerodynamic reactions on surfaces which remain fixed under given conditions of flight;

**Airship**

> "Air-Ship" means a lighter-than-air aircraft capable of vertical take-off, vertical landing, and low-speed flight, which depends principally on engine-driven lift devices or engine thrust for the lift during these flight regimes and on non-rotating aerofoil(s) for lift during horizontal flight.

**PARE — Standard Procedure for Spin Recovery**

-**P**ower idle: Reduce engine power to idle. This reduces the pitching moment and helps to decrease the spin rate.

-**A**ilerons neutral: Center the ailerons. Using ailerons during a spin can worsen the situation or make recovery more difficult.

-**R**udder opposite to rotation: Apply full rudder in the direction opposite to the spin. This is the most crucial step as it counteracts the yawing motion of the spin.

-**E**levator through: Move the elevator control forward (down) to break the stall, then smoothly pull back (up) to recover from the dive. The initial forward movement helps to decrease the angle of attack, unstalling the wings.


**CG vs CP**

The**center of gravity (CG)** is the average location of the weight of an object. It's the single point where the entire weight of an object can be considered to act. For an object in a uniform gravitational field, the center of gravity and the center of mass are the same.

The**center of pressure (CP)** is the point where the total sum of the pressure forces on a body acts. It is the effective point where all the aerodynamic forces (like lift and drag) can be considered to act on an object.

**Origin:**

The Center of Gravity (CG) originates due to the object's mass and the force of gravity acting on it.

The Center of Pressure (CP) originates due to external forces, such as air pressure, acting on the object's surface.

**Nature:**

The Center of Gravity (CG) is generally a fixed point for a rigid body, assuming its mass distribution doesn't change.

The Center of Pressure (CP) is a dynamic point that can shift**depending on various factors like the angle of attack**, airspeed, and the shape of the object.

**Effect:**

The Center of Gravity (CG) determines the object's stability and how it balances under the influence of gravity.

The Center of Pressure (CP) determines the pitching moment and directional stability of an object when it is moving through a fluid (for example, an aircraft or a rocket).

In simplified terms, the CG is about the object's inherent weight distribution, while the CP is about how external forces push on the object. The relationship between these two points is crucial for the stability and control of flying objects like airplanes and rockets.

**SHELL Model**

**SHELL Model**

A framework for analyzing accidents and incidents by looking beyond simple "pilot error" and instead examining the systemic factors that led to the event. It helps investigators identify mismatches at the various interfaces. By addressing these mismatches, aviation professionals can improve safety and reduce the risk of future errors.A framework for analyzing accidents and incidents by looking beyond simple "pilot error" and instead examining the systemic factors that led to the event. It helps investigators identify mismatches at the various interfaces. By addressing these mismatches, aviation professionals can improve safety and reduce the risk of future errors.

-**Accident Investigation:** After an accident, investigators can use the model to structure their analysis. They examine the Liveware-Liveware interface to understand crew communication and teamwork, the Liveware-Hardware interface to check for equipment malfunctions or design flaws, the Liveware-Software interface to evaluate procedures, and the Liveware-Environment interface to consider the impact of external conditions.

-**System Design:** The model is also used proactively during the design of new aircraft, cockpits, and systems. Engineers and human factors specialists consider the interfaces to ensure that the new components are compatible with human capabilities and limitations, ultimately making them safer and easier to use.

-**Training and Procedures:** The insights from the SHELL model are integrated into pilot training programs, such as**Crew Resource Management (CRM)**, to emphasize the importance of communication, teamwork, and decision-making in a complex environment.

**PAVE model**

Risk assessment tool used by pilots, particularly in general aviation, to identify and mitigate risks before and during a flight. It's an acronym that helps pilots remember to consider four key categories of risk. Unlike the SHELL model, which is a broader framework for analyzing human factors, PAVE is a practical, personal checklist that pilots use for decision-making.

Here's a breakdown of each component of the PAVE model:

-**P - Pilot-in-Command (PIC):** This element focuses on the pilot themselves. The pilot must honestly and critically assess their own readiness for the flight. This includes:

 -**Physical and mental condition:** Am I well-rested? Am I sick? Am I under stress? A common sub-checklist used here is the**IMSAFE** checklist (Illness, Medication, Stress, Alcohol, Fatigue, Emotion).

 -**Proficiency and currency:** Am I current and proficient in flying this type of aircraft? Do I have the necessary experience for the planned route and conditions?

 -**Documentation:** Are my pilot's license, medical certificate, and other required documents current and on my person?

-**A - Aircraft:** This component requires the pilot to evaluate the aircraft's airworthiness and suitability for the planned flight. Key questions to ask include:

 -**Airworthiness:** Has the aircraft been properly maintained and inspected?

 -**Equipment:** Is the aircraft properly equipped for the planned flight (e.g., are the instruments and avionics adequate for instrument flight if the weather requires it)?

 -**Performance:** Is the aircraft's performance (e.g., climb rate, payload capacity, fuel consumption) sufficient for the flight, given the environmental conditions and planned route?

 -**Documentation:** Are the aircraft's required documents (e.g., airworthiness certificate, registration) on board?

-**V - enVironment:** This element covers all the external factors that can affect the flight. The pilot must consider:

 -**Weather:** Is the weather at the departure point, en route, and at the destination suitable for the pilot's and aircraft's capabilities? This includes winds, turbulence, icing, and visibility.

 -**Terrain:** Does the flight path include mountainous or challenging terrain?

 -**Airspace:** Are there any specific airspace requirements or restrictions along the route?

 -**Airport conditions:** Are the runways long enough and in good condition? Are there any NOTAMs (Notices to Airmen) that could affect the flight?

-**E - External Pressures:** This is often a subtle but dangerous element of risk. It refers to any influences that can create a sense of pressure to complete a flight, even when it might be unsafe. These pressures can be internal or external, such as:

 -**"Get-there-itis"**: The desire to reach a destination despite deteriorating conditions.

 -**Passenger expectations:** Feeling pressured to please passengers who are eager to get to their destination.

 -**Business or social obligations:** The need to get to an important meeting or event.

 -**Personal pride:** Not wanting to admit a flight is beyond your capabilities.
