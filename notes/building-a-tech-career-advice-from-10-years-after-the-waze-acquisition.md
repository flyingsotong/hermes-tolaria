# Building a Tech Career Advice from 10 Years After the Waze Acquisition

<https://twitter.com/julie_mo/status/1669050123211399177>
**Smart summary:** The thread provides advice for those in the tech industry, emphasizing the importance of being in the right room, seeing every opportunity as an adventure and lesson, committing to roles for the long-term, staying in touch with everyone, and being prepared for the best and worst characters to come back. It also highlights the importance of having a broad range of knowledge that can be applied in surprising ways.
Yesterday, 10(!) years ago, Waze was acquired by Google. So much of startup life is oriented around the 'exit' - but what about the after? I wish someone would have told me what I know now.
1: Probably the best advice I've received: If you're in the right room once, you will be there again.
Waze was right room/right people. I have also been in right room/wrong people (the greatest lesson of my career) and wrong room/wrong people (If you walk into that one, walk out immediately.)
2: Everything is an opportunity. Some Wazers felt like we snuck in the side door, thrilled to be Googlers. Others hated the idea of corporate life & red tape. While working at Google was never my plan, seeing it as a plot twist made me embrace every day as an adventure & lesson
3: I learned policy and polish from a world class organization that I would have never dreamed of having access to. I stayed for 5 years, which leads me to
4: Committing 'years' of your life may not be guaranteed at all, and if it is, vesting schedules fly when you're doing interesting work. Always, always look for a role you can imaging doing for a long time, or one that is early enough to morph and allow you to grow
5: The scrappiness of a tight knit team can outexecute a department of brilliant minds
6: Sort of like the music business- sort of - you will feel pressure to have another big hit to define the first one. This is lame. As you work on things you love, a trend will emerge that helps define your career. 10 years later some of these examples lose their relevancy anyway
I mean, my first exit was Groupon, the fastest growing company of all time, and believe me when I say that reference point did not age well
7: Stay. In. Touch. With. Everyone...
8: ...and be prepared when the best and worst characters come back for another story arc. Life rhymes
9: To that end, everything I know about community I learned from Waze, a non web3 company. Nearly everything I know about marketing is from my PR career in franchise businesses 20 yrs ago. Your knowledge accumulates and will be relevant in surprising ways.
