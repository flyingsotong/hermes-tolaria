- dilution <-> valuation (underpricing)

- meeting expectations on growth and profits -- and exit

- market conditions

- quality of investors (financial vs strategic investors -- eg. telco buying into a telco... often with no exit in mind)

- start with as many shares as possible -- easier as you grow

Defines startups as being pre-revenue

Triple F - founder, friends, family

Sweat capital = opportunity cost

Entrepreneurs tend to succeed at 40, statistically

- money savings

- network and connections

Cashflow is reality Profitability is vanity (in the short term at least! It flips in the long term)

Put yourself in your investor's shoes

- what do you need

- how will you use it

- what are you willing to give up to get this

Post money = pre money + amount invested

The Berkus Method attempts to circumvent the problem of quantifying something, which is not (yet) possible to quantify by using both qualitative and quantitative factors to calculate valuation based on five elements:

- Valuable business model (base value)

- Available prototype (reducing technology risks)

- Abilities of the founding or management team (reducing implementation risks)

- Strategic Relationships (reducing market risks)

- Existing costumers or first sales (reducing production risks)
