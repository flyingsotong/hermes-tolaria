# News is Dead. Long Live Journalism!

- Description: How AI reveals an opportunity to rise above the noise and save journalism.

- [Natalia Antelava](Natalia%20Antelava.md)

- Summary: The author argues that the news industry may face extinction due to generative AI, but journalism as a vital societal institution must be preserved. The challenge is to redefine journalism in an age where AI shapes reality and individuals retreat into ideological bubbles. The author, a co-founder of Coda and a JSK Journalism Fellow at Stanford, believes that journalism should focus on fostering conversations rather than just producing content.**This shift from being gatekeepers to curators of dialogue is crucial for journalism to remain relevant.** The author also critiques the 24-hour news cycle and the role of social media algorithms in diluting quality journalism, suggesting that the current crisis is an opportunity for journalists to rethink their approach and use technology to serve human needs.

Feb 27, 2024

My inbox was full of gloom. “Apocalyptic vibes,” read a message from a colleague referring to yet another round of layoffs rocking the media industry. “We are so fucked!” flashed another, directing me to an insane tale of how an AI-generated story about Vermont was leading the search results over the actual story as reported by local news outlets.

I was scrolling through my messages in the lobby of a Miami hotel, surrounded by hundreds of journalists, media entrepreneurs and funders attending the annual Knight Media Forum. The anxiety of my inbox was vibrating in the room too, but it felt less extreme in the 3D. The camaraderie, the exchange of ideas, and of course, the booze, eased the doom. The momentum behind the star of the show: Press Forward, an ambitious and a much needed initiative to save local journalism in the U.S., also generated a real promise of hope.

I appreciated the hope (and the free booze!) and found many conversations inspiring. But I left Miami with a nagging sense that while individually we are all looking for ways to save journalism, collectively we are in denial. We are still pretending that AI is about to push the journalism industry off a cliff that we’ve been carefully balancing on for years. The truth is, as an industry, we fell off long ago. Or rather, we jumped off the cliff believing we had parachutes that worked. All they did was slow down the fall.

**JOURNALISM VS NEWS**

For the news industry, the rollout of generative AI is the extinction event. But to me this tragic death of an industry I have long been part of, underlines an important distinction between news as an industry and journalism as a core institution of an open, liberal society. We still can, and must, save the latter.

“Our future job will be to authenticate reality,” a senior media executive and a dear friend argued over dinner recently. But what does “authenticate” or “reality” even mean in the world where reality is increasingly determined by how something makes one feel rather than whatever “objective” parameters we as media have long relied on?

If you have played with Apple Vision Pro, you would have felt that cute butterfly land on your finger, or that dinosaur poke its head into your living room. It’s mind blowing and it is exciting because it feels real. And for us, humans, what feels real is real. This sudden availability of this multitude of realities legitimizes and scales the age-old human trait of believing what we want to believe.

But while it feels like a new problem, it isn’t. “A freedom fighter for one is always a terrorist for another” is the old truism of journalism. The news business taught me how to authenticate a reality, but it is journalism that taught me how to navigate that intricate, often uncomfortable space between conflicting realities. As AI takes over more of our lives and more of us retreat into our individual bubbles, that shrinking shared space we call our “public square” is ever more crucial to preserve. Journalism can help us save it, but only if we separate it from the news cycle.

I first started thinking of the distinction between news and journalism when I left the BBC to co-found Coda in 2016. Our mission was to figure out how to stay on the story, how to create context and continuity around coverage of major events. Back then, as we bootstrapped Coda, I found it helpful to think of “News” as a product of a reactive process that is focused on creating incremental pieces of “content.” The “when and what” of the media. Journalism, on the other hand, was the “how and why,” a product of a proactive process that is rooted in context. News can be automated, journalism is all about human connection (even if it is a human’s connection with a robot!). News is food, journalism is nutrition. News is what we need to know to survive as individuals. Journalism is what we need to evolve as a society that values individuals. I can go on!

Coda’s success through the years has often made me feel validated in our proactive, thematically focused approach to journalism. But despite a stellar track record of producing award-winning stories and creating a mixed revenue model, today, Coda, like every other digital newsroom, faces an existential threat: the journalism we work so hard to produce is lost in the flood of “content” in an information ecosystem that puts the equal (or at times higher) value on every reaction, with no credit to the time and thought put into reported pieces.

How can quality journalism punch through the deafening noise is the question that brought me to Stanford as a JSK Journalism Fellow. Six months and hundreds of conversations later, an unexpected answer emerged in the form of an addition to my ever growing news vs journalism list: news is about an article, journalism is about a conversation.

Putting conversation ahead of an individual story may seem subtle, but it is a radical departure for any newsroom. It changes everything: opening up opportunities for new formats, new collaborations and a completely different take on impact and distribution. To me, it also makes a lot of sense because it addresses the simple but often-overlooked fact that journalists are no longer the gatekeepers of information. The platforms are.

To be relevant in our busy, attention-starved, news-avoiding society, journalists must become skilled curators of conversations that connect us. Nuanced, authentic, engaging exchanges that can only happen when we are truly paying attention.

In his award-winning piece for Coda, author Peter Pomeranzev argued that there were once “grand narratives that explained everything from politics to literature” and that the collapse of these “connected storylines” calls for new thinking about what binds us. Conversations bind us. Without them how will we come up with the new narratives and solutions that our societies so desperately need? Conversation is also the opposite of commodifying human attention. Real conversations are the opposite of noise.

Those conversations cannot live in echo-chambers. They thrive and multiply when they occur in that delicate, often intellectually uncomfortable but critically important space between the ideological comfort zones. Over the past years, we as the news industry, contributed to its shrinking. Now is our chance to use journalism to grow it back.

**THE PLUMBING ISSUE**

But how do you have a conversation in the world that is increasingly designed for shouting?

The problem is that our information ecosystem has a major plumbing issue: our sewage and drinking water are going through the same pipes. I love the metaphor, but sadly can’t take credit for it. It belongs to Judy Estrin, a legendary Silicon Valley entrepreneur whose brilliant take on our society’s collective descent into authoritarianism is really worth your time.

While the social media platforms are the ones who laid the pipes and are currently pumping the sewage into them, it was not Mark Zuckerberg who started the trend. It was Ted Turner back in 1980, when he created CNN and with it the 24-hour news cycle.

The news cycle became the new organizing principle of the modern news industry. Even though the idea that there is enough news to fill 24 hours is in itself, well, sewage. News as entertainment worked as a business model, but not as a journalism model.

The news cycle shifted newsrooms’ focus from providing information to providing entertainment by “feeding the beast.” By the early 2000s, the internet allowed the rest of the industry to jump on the trend. Newspaper reporters waved good-bye to filing their stories at the end of the day and began feeding constant updates into the bottomless pit of the internet. The more news we filed, the less time we had for the actual journalism. I remember getting off a plane in Sana’a, Yemen and being told by my (very smart) editor at the BBC to “file immediately.” “But I am still on the tarmac, I haven’t spoken to anyone yet,” I objected. It was no problem. “Talk to people after you file,” he suggested.

In the subsequent years, plenty of newsrooms and initiatives were created to counter the news cycle problem and offer depth, but most of them relied heavily on tech platforms who adopted and perfected the same principles they were trying to get away from. Once it came to distribution their journalism became “content,” flattened along with all the other information into the constantly updating timelines designed to make money for the big tech. Investigations into corruption or war crimes mixed with conspiracy theorists, cute cats, fashion and ISIS beheadings. It was drinking water in the sewage pipe.

And the algorithms directing where each individual piece of content went, or what compilation you got on your timeline, were designed to simultaneously cause outrage and push us into soothing comfort of our individual ideological bubbles — the drinking water, diluted or polluted or unable to get through. This has had a profound effect on the way we consume and process information, on what we as a society care or not care about and how we react to injustice. Would Watergate have the same impact in the age of social media? We know the answer.

And now, with the advances of AI, robots too are joining in. Chatbots are already the new gatekeepers of information: creating content, fact-checking, giving you weather reports and latest football scores and providing superb tools to those who want to flood us with fake news. Soon we’ll be asking the chatbots on the latest from Gaza. The quality of answers will depend on deals that the news industry survivors will do with the tech companies. Personally, I am betting on a lot more sewage.

Meantime this AI revolution is wreaking havoc to my newsroom. Changes to SEO mean that our traffic is down, and tweaks to social media algorithms mean our posts are no longer showing up in people’s feeds. All my industry peers are seeing the same issues, and yet as I steer Coda into a new direction, I also feel optimistic.

The industry may have jumped off the cliff, but AI has also given us a new chance to finally realize that the parachute does not work. Now is the time for us to come together and collectively figure out how we, journalists, want to use technology as a tool to meet human needs and not as a master we serve.
