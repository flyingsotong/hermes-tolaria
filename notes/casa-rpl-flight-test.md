**Aeronautical Knowledge Questions**
1.**Describe the 4 stroke cycle as applied to a light aircraft engine.**
-**Intake:** The intake valve opens, and the piston moves down, drawing a fuel-air mixture into the cylinder.
***Compression:** Both valves close, and the piston moves up, compressing the fuel-air mixture.
-**Power:** The spark plug ignites the compressed mixture, causing it to burn rapidly. This expansion of gases forces the piston down, producing power.
***Exhaust:** The exhaust valve opens, and the piston moves up, expelling the burned gases from the cylinder.
1.**What is the purpose of the carburetor and how does it work?**
- The purpose of the carburetor is to mix fuel with air in the correct proportions for combustion.
* It works based on Bernoulli's principle. Air flowing through a venturi (a constricted passage) in the carburetor creates a low-pressure area. This low pressure draws fuel from the carburetor's float chamber into the airstream, where it atomizes and mixes with the air.
1.**Why do aeroplane engines have dual ignition systems?**
- Dual ignition systems provide redundancy. If one magneto fails, the engine can continue to run on the other.
* It also improves combustion efficiency by providing two spark plugs per cylinder, resulting in more complete and even burning of the fuel-air mixture.
1.**How does a magneto work?**
- A magneto is a self-contained electrical generator that produces high-voltage sparks for the engine's spark plugs.
* It uses permanent magnets and a rotating armature to induce a current in a coil. When the breaker points open, the magnetic field collapses, inducing a high-voltage surge that is sent to the spark plugs.
1.**What is an impulse coupling and what does it do?**
- An impulse coupling is a device used in some magneto systems to provide a strong spark for starting the engine.
* It temporarily retards the ignition timing and spins the magneto rapidly when the engine is being started, producing a hot spark even at low engine cranking speeds.
1.**How does an engine tachometer work?**
- Most tachometers in light aircraft measure engine RPM (revolutions per minute) by sensing the electrical pulses generated by the magneto.
* The pulses are converted to a reading on the instrument. some older systems used a mechanical system driven by the crankshaft.
1.**When a circuit breaker “pops” how many times should you reset it and why?**
- Only once. If it pops again, it indicates a persistent electrical fault that should be investigated by a qualified mechanic. Repeatedly resetting a circuit breaker can lead to overheating and electrical fires.
1.**Describe the aeroplane refueling process from our local fuel bowser.**
- Ground the aircraft and fuel bowser to prevent static electricity.
* Ensure the correct fuel grade is selected.
- Visually inspect the fuel for contaminants.
* Use a fuel strainer during refueling.
- Fill the tanks to the appropriate level, leaving room for fuel expansion.
* Secure the fuel caps.
- Complete the required paperwork.
1.**Describe how you check for water contamination.**
- Visually inspect the fuel for cloudiness or sediment.
* Use a fuel sampler to draw a small amount of fuel from the tank's drain and inspect it for water droplets.
- Fuel testers that change color in the presence of water can also be used.
1.**How do you ‘positively’ check for the absence of water in the aeroplane fuel tanks?**
- Drain fuel from the lowest point of each fuel tank sump and visually inspect the drained fuel in a clear container. Water, being heavier than fuel, will settle at the bottom.
1.**Detonation:**
-**What is it?** Uncontrolled, explosive combustion of the fuel-air mixture in the cylinder.
***How is it caused?** High cylinder temperatures and pressures, lean fuel mixtures, low-octane fuel, or advanced ignition timing.
-**What is its effect?** Can cause engine damage, including piston and cylinder damage.
***How do you fix it?** Enrich the fuel mixture, reduce engine power, use higher-octane fuel, or retard ignition timing.
1.**In normal cruise flight, what will be the indications of a magneto failure?**
- A noticeable drop in RPM.
* Rough engine running.
- Possible engine misfiring.
1.**What are the indications of carburetor icing during the various flight phases?**
-**Cruise:** Gradual loss of RPM, rough engine running, and a decrease in manifold pressure.
***Approach/Landing:** Loss of RPM, engine roughness, and potential engine failure if not corrected.
-**Ground operations:** Possible engine roughness, and inability to achieve full RPM during run up.
1.**When are you most likely to get carburetor icing?**
- High humidity and temperatures between 20F (-7C) and 70F (21C).
* When the humidity is high, even outside of that temperature range, icing is possible.
1.**When operating below 3000′ above mean sea level what are the visual meteorological conditions requirements?**
- 3 statute miles visibility.
* 1,000 feet above, 500 feet below, and 2,000 feet horizontally from clouds.
1.**When should you activate the following aeroplane lighting systems:**
-**Anti-collision lights?** Always on during engine operation.
***Strobe lights?** Always on during flight, unless in clouds where they can cause disorienting reflections.
-**Navigation lights?** Sunset to sunrise, or when visibility is reduced.
1.**Describe how to correctly set the mixture control for the various flight phases.**
-**Ground operations:** Full rich.
***Takeoff:** Full rich.
-**Climb:** Full rich or leaned slightly for best power.
***Cruise:** Leaned for best economy or best power, depending on the situation.
-**Descent:** Full rich.
***Landing:** Full rich.
1.**What are thrust and power? How are they developed in one of the training aeroplanes?**
-**Thrust:** The forward force produced by the propeller.
***Power:** The rate at which work is done.
- In a training aircraft, thrust is developed by the propeller accelerating a mass of air rearward. Power is developed by the engine rotating the propeller.
1.**How would you configure a piston-engine aircraft with a fixed pitch propeller for:**
-**Maximum range in no wind conditions and why?** Fly at a higher altitude and a lower RPM. This minimizes drag and fuel consumption per mile.
***Maximum endurance and why?** Fly at a lower altitude and a lower airspeed. This minimizes fuel consumption per hour.
1.**What are the maximum range and maximum endurance configurations for our training aircraft?**
- This will vary based on the specific aircraft. Consult the Pilot Operating Handbook (POH) for your aircraft.
1.**How are these configurations in Q 20 derived?**
- Flight testing and performance calculations by the aircraft manufacturer.
1.**While maintaining a turn with a constant angle of bank, what happens to the radius of turn if you change the airspeed, and why?**
- Increasing airspeed increases the radius of turn.
* Decreasing airspeed decreases the radius of turn.
- \==This is because the faster the aircraft is moving, the more horizontal component of lift is required to maintain the same rate of turn.==
1.**State the lift formula and define each component.**
- Lift = CL** (ρ** V2 / 2)** S** CL = Coefficient of lift
 - ρ (rho) = Air density
 * V = Velocity (airspeed)
 - S = Wing area
1.**Demonstrate a practical application of the lift formula.**
- If you increase your airspeed, the lift will increase by the square of that airspeed increase. To maintain level flight, you would need to decrease the angle of attack, which decreases the coefficient of lift.
1.**Describe Bernoulli’s theorem.**
- As the velocity of a fluid (air) increases, its pressure decreases.
1.**Describe how you would achieve a minimum radius level turn in our training aircraft.**
- Use a steep bank angle and a low airspeed, while maintaining sufficient power to prevent a stall.
1.**Describe how the stability of our training aircraft is affected as the loaded center of gravity is moved progressively to the aft limit.**
- The aircraft becomes less stable, with a tendency to pitch up. It also becomes more difficult to recover from a stall.
1.**What is the difference between equal time point and point of no return? Describe how you calculate these figures.**
-**Equal Time Point (ETP):** The point along a track where the time to return to the departure aerodrome is equal to the time to continue to the destination aerodrome.
***Point of No Return (PNR):** The point along a track where the fuel remaining is just sufficient to return to the departure aerodrome, considering wind and fuel consumption.
-**ETP Calculation:**
 - \ETP = (Distance / 2) + (Wind Effect / 2)\
 * \Wind Effect = (Groundspeed Out - Groundspeed In) / (Groundspeed Out + Groundspeed In) \* Distance\
***PNR Calculation:**
 - \PNR = (Endurance \* Groundspeed Out) / (Groundspeed Out + Groundspeed In)\
 * These calculations require accurate groundspeed calculations, and fuel consumption rates.
1.**What is our flying school fuel policy and how do we calculate the following?:**
- This will vary by flying school. You must get the exact numbers from your school's operating procedures.
***Taxi Fuel:** Fuel used for engine start, taxiing, and run-up.
-**Circuit manoeuvring fuel:** Fuel used for flying the traffic pattern.
***Trip fuel:** Fuel required to fly from departure to destination.
-**Variable reserve:** Fuel to account for unexpected delays or diversions (often based on a percentage of trip fuel).
***Fixed reserve:** Fuel required to fly for a set time (e.g., 30 minutes) at a specific power setting.
-**Margin fuel:** Extra fuel added for safety.
1.**When calculating a lowest safe altitude on a night VFR light, what splay do we use from a Navaid?**
- This varies from country to country, and also can vary by the type of Navaid. Consult the local regulations for the specific splay.
1.**What is the difference between pilot activated lighting and aerodrome frequency response unit aerodrome lighting?**
-**Pilot Activated Lighting (PAL):** Lighting systems activated by the pilot using radio transmissions on a specific frequency.
***Aerodrome Frequency Response Unit (AFRU):** A system that automatically activates aerodrome lighting based on radio transmissions from aircraft.
1.**Describe how to calculate pressure altitude and density altitude.**
-**Pressure Altitude:** Altitude indicated when the altimeter subscale is set to 29.92 inches Hg (1013.2 hPa).
 - \Pressure Altitude = (29.92 - Current Altimeter Setting) \* 1000 + Field Elevation\
-**Density Altitude:** Pressure altitude corrected for non-standard temperature.
 - Use a flight computer or E6B to calculate.
1.**What is the automatic enroute information service and how do you access it?**
-**Automatic Enroute Information Service (AEIS):** A continuous broadcast of aviation weather and NOTAM information.
* Accessed by tuning to the appropriate frequency.
1.**What is an Isoganal?**
- A line on a chart connecting points of equal magnetic variation.
1.**What is magnetic dip and when is it most noticeable?**
-**Magnetic Dip:** The downward component of the Earth's magnetic field, causing a compass needle to point downward as well as northward.
* Most noticeable at high latitudes, near the magnetic poles.
1.**In relation to the principles of operation of a gyro, describe the following characteristics:**
-**Precession:** The characteristic of a rotating gyro to react to an applied force 90 degrees in the direction of rotation.
***Rigidity:** The characteristic of a rotating gyro to maintain its orientation in space.
1.**What is the purpose of a counterweight in a constant speed unit mechanism?**
- To balance the propeller blades and reduce vibration.
1.**What is washout and what is its purpose?**
-**Washout:** A design feature where the wing's angle of incidence decreases from the wing root to the wingtip.
* Purpose: To improve stall characteristics by causing the wing root to stall before the wingtips, maintaining aileron effectiveness.
1.**Why do we check the magnetos prior to shut down?**
- To ensure that the magnetos are properly grounded and that the engine will not continue to run after the ignition switch is turned off.
1.**What is a fohn wind?**
- A warm, dry wind that occurs on the leeward side of a mountain range.
1.**What is virga?**
- Precipitation that evaporates before reaching the ground.
1.**Describe the principles for calculating specific ground range.**
- Specific Ground Range = Groundspeed / Fuel Flow. It tells you how many nautical miles you can fly per gallon of fuel.
1.**What is Coriolis force?**
- An apparent force caused by the Earth's rotation, which deflects moving objects to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.
1.**What are Hadley cells?**
- Large-scale atmospheric circulation patterns in the tropics, characterized by rising air at the equator and sinking air at about 30 degrees latitude.

1.**What is a katabatic wind?**
- A wind that flows down a slope due to gravity, often at night, as the air cools and becomes denser.
1.**What frequency band does the Very High Frequency Omni Range work on?**

- Check the bearing accuracy against published tolerances.
1.**How does a GPS unit calculate its position?**
- By receiving signals from multiple GPS satellites and calculating its position using trilateration.
1.**How do you know if the GPS unit is serviceable and generating accurate data?**

1.**Explain what is likely to happen if the aeroplane vacuum gauge is consistently indicating well below the green arc for the flight.**
- The vacuum-driven gyroscopic instruments (attitude indicator and heading indicator) will become unreliable, possibly leading to inaccurate indications.
1.**How do you check the magnetos at the conclusion of a flight and why?**
- Briefly switch the magnetos off one at a time to ensure the engine RPM drops, confirming that each magneto is operating and properly grounded. If the engine continues to run after the switch is set to off, there is a grounding issue.

- This helps identify potential problems before the next flight.

1.**What is the purpose of a mass balance fitted to a primary aerodynamic control surface?**
- To prevent flutter by balancing the control surface around its hinge line.
1.**What is a frise aileron and what is its purpose?**
- An aileron that protrudes its leading edge into the airflow on the rising wing, creating drag to counteract adverse yaw.
1.**Why is the tail plane angle of incidence usually smaller than the wing angle of incidence?**
- To provide longitudinal stability and prevent the tail from stalling before the wing.
1.**What is asymmetric propeller blade effect?**
- Also known as P-factor, it's the uneven thrust produced by a propeller when the aircraft is at a high angle of attack, causing a yawing moment.
1.**How does a carburetor accelerator pump work?**
- It injects a small amount of extra fuel into the venturi when the throttle is rapidly opened, providing a richer mixture for acceleration.
1.**Explain ‘full throttle height’.**
- The maximum altitude at which an engine can produce its rated horsepower at full throttle.
1.**Why is the vertical fin offset in some aeroplanes?**
- To counteract the yawing moment caused by P-factor.
1.**Why is pre-stall buffet felt through the controls in some aeroplanes?**
- It's caused by turbulent airflow over the wing as the stall approaches, providing a warning to the pilot.
1.**What is the reason for removing stone chips from a metal propeller blade and who is authorised to do it?**
- To prevent cracks and stress concentrations that can lead to propeller failure.
* A certified aircraft mechanic is authorized to perform this repair.
1.**Name three possible applications of a sideslip. What is the limiting factor in a sideslip?**
- Crosswind landings, forward slips for altitude loss, and correcting for a skid.
* The limiting factor is the amount of rudder available and the risk of a stall.
1.**Explain the possible effects of running an aeroplane piston engine with either too rich or too lean fuel mixture settings.**
-**Too Rich:** Fouled spark plugs, reduced power, increased fuel consumption, and carbon deposits.
***Too Lean:** High cylinder head temperatures, detonation, and engine damage.
1.**You are flying a single engine light aeroplane fitted with a constant speed propeller. In the event of a total engine failure what propeller pitch setting do you select and why?**
- Select a fine pitch (low pitch angle). This reduces drag and maintains propeller rotation, assisting in autorotation and providing some control.
1.**You are flying a single engine light aeroplane fitted with a constant speed propeller. In the event of a total loss of engine oil, what will happen to the propeller and why?**
- The propeller will likely go to a fine pitch (low pitch angle). This is due to the loss of oil pressure which is used to move the propeller to a coarse pitch.
1.**Explain a dynamic stall.**
- A stall that occurs at a lower angle of attack than a static stall, often during rapid changes in pitch or airspeed.
1.**During your pre-flight inspection of a single engine light aeroplane fitted with a constant speed propeller, you note the manifold pressure gauge reading. What should it read and why?**
- With the engine off, the manifold pressure should read ambient atmospheric pressure, which is roughly equal to the altimeter setting.
1.**What is an inset hinge balance?**
- A type of aerodynamic balance where the hinge line of a control surface is set back from its leading edge, reducing hinge moments.
1.**What is the purpose of a ‘shower of sparks’ device fitted to some aeroplanes?**
- To provide a strong spark for starting the engine in cold weather.
1.**Why do most gliders have high aspect ratio wings? What are the advantages and disadvantages and why?**
-**High Aspect Ratio:** Long, narrow wings.
***Advantages:** Reduced induced drag, improved glide performance.
-**Disadvantages:** Reduced maneuverability, increased wingtip vortices.
1.**What are the performance implications of flying an aeroplane out of balance and why?**
- Increased drag, reduced performance, and potential control difficulties due to asymmetric lift distribution.
