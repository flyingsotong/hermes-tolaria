**Course, Heading, and Why You're Not Flying Straight South**

This is happening because of**wind**. *Your aircraft is a boat on a river of air.*

Think of it this way: You want to go directly south (your**Course** of 180∘). But if there's a wind blowing from the west, it will push you to the east. To counteract this, the autopilot has to point the nose of the plane slightly into the wind (to the west).

-**CRS (Course):** The path you _want_ to follow over the ground. You set this to 180∘.

-**HDG (Heading):** The direction the aircraft's nose is _actually pointing_. The autopilot adjusts this to fight the wind.

-**TRK (Track):** The aircraft's _actual path_ over the ground. The autopilot's goal is to make your TRK match your desired CRS or DTK.

-**DTK (Desired Track):** In GPS mode, this is the track the G1000 calculates as the most direct path to your next waypoint. It's essentially the same as your desired course.

-**BRG (Bearing):** The current direction _from_ your aircraft _to_ the selected waypoint. It constantly changes as you fly.

So, the map shows you flying a bit sideways (crabbing) because your**Heading** is different from your**Track** to compensate for the crosswind. The small differences you see between DTK and TRK are just the autopilot making tiny, constant corrections.

**The Three CDI Modes (Purple vs. Green)**

The CDI (Course Deviation Indicator) shows how far you are from your selected path. The color tells you what source you're navigating with.

-**GPS (Purple):** This is the most common mode. The CDI needle shows your deviation from the flight plan you've programmed into the G1000's GPS.

-**NAV1 (Green):** This means you're navigating using the frequency tuned into your**NAV1 radio**. This is for "old-school" ground-based navigation aids, typically a**VOR** (VHF Omnidirectional Range) or a**LOC** (Localizer) for an instrument landing.

-**NAV2 (Green):** Same as NAV1, but it uses the frequency tuned in your**NAV2 radio**. Having two allows you to have one ready for the next leg of your flight or monitor a second station.

In short:**Purple = GPS**,**Green = Radio Beacons**. You switch between them using the \CDI\ softkey on the PFD (Primary Flight Display).


**Cowl Flaps**

These are vents that control airflow over the engine to manage its temperature.

-**OPEN:** Maximizes cooling. Use during high-power, low-airspeed operations like taxiing, takeoff, and climbing.

-**CLOSED:** Reduces drag. Use during cruise and descent when the engine is producing less heat and you have high airspeed providing sufficient cooling.

>**Simple rule:** Open on the ground and for the climb. Close them when you level off for cruise. Open them again in the descent before landing.

**Pitot Heat & Prop De-ice**

These are anti-icing/de-icing systems.

-**Pitot Heat:** Heats the pitot tube to prevent ice from blocking it. A blocked pitot tube will cause the airspeed indicator to fail, which is extremely dangerous.**Use it whenever you are in visible moisture (clouds, fog, rain) and the Outside Air Temperature (OAT) is near or below freezing (+5∘C is a good buffer).**

-**Prop De-ice:** Heats the propeller blades to shed ice. Ice on the prop reduces thrust and can cause destructive vibrations. Use it in the same conditions as Pitot Heat.

**Cabin Levers**

These are mostly for pilot/passenger comfort and emergencies.

-**Cabin Heat / Defrost:** These pull hot air from a shroud around the engine exhaust into the cabin. Use them to warm the cabin or defrost the windshield in cold conditions.

-**Engine Fire Pull:** This is an emergency handle. In the real plane, it shuts off fuel and oil flow to the engine and may discharge a fire extinguisher. You should only ever use this if the engine is actually on fire.

**More Cool G1000 Features**

The G1000 is incredibly powerful. Here are a few things to try next:

-**Direct-To Navigation:** The \D->\ button is your best friend. Press it, enter an airport code or waypoint, and hit Enter twice. The plane will now fly a direct course to that point. Perfect for diversions or just flying VFR.

-**Procedures (PROC):** Use the \PROC\ softkey. Here you can load and activate instrument approaches (like an ILS or RNAV approach) to your destination runway. This will give you both lateral and vertical guidance for a perfect landing. It's a game-changer for IFR flight.

-**MFD Pages:** Use the large FMS knob to cycle through pages on the right-hand screen (MFD). You can see your full flight plan list (\FPL\), nearest airports (\NRST\), and auxiliary info (\AUX\) where you can set things like your arrival time alerts.

-**Onboard Checklists:** Use the \CHKLST\ softkey to bring up checklists for every phase of flight, from pre-flight to shutdown. It’s a great way to learn the proper flow and procedures for the aircraft.
