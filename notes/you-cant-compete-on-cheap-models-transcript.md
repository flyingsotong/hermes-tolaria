# You Can't Compete on Cheap Models Anymore (Nate B. Jones)

## The strange convergence

Something odd is happening to everyone using AI at the same time. The tools keep getting better. The prices keep dropping. You can produce more than ever — and yet everything's starting to feel the same. Your output, your competitors' output, half of what's in your feed all looks similar.

Better tools, samey results. That's not a coincidence, and it's not a tooling problem.

Something specific happened. AI made doing things cheap — and whenever execution gets cheap, the value doesn't disappear. It just moves.

## The Hashimoto experiment

Mitchell Hashimoto — co-founder of HashiCorp, creator of the Ghostty terminal, one of the most respected engineers working today — ran a revealing test when the current frontier model launched.

He spent days testing it against cheaper models on ordinary work: implement this feature, build that thing. The stuff on everybody's list. All three models produced equally acceptable output. The budget model cost under a buck and finished in minutes. The mid-tier model about a buck fifty. The frontier model cost 40 minutes and $9. Same work, same quality, 9x more expensive.

If you stop there, the frontier model looks like a rip-off. A lot of people are stopping there — that result launched a thousand hot takes about routing everything to cheap models. And honestly, you should route a lot of execution to cheap models. That makes sense.

But when everyone in AI is saying the same thing at the same time — and right now everyone is saying route to cheaper models — that's exactly when a different question becomes interesting: where does value move once we've all figured this out?

Routing is real. It's also about to be table stakes. Everyone's going to have it. Execution is commoditizing fast. So the question that matters isn't the one the whole industry is answering right now. It's the one hiding in the second half of Hashimoto's experiment.

## The $40 question

Hashimoto ran one more test. He handed the frontier model a problem the cheap ones couldn't touch at all — optimizing a gnarly piece of systems code he'd written himself. It took 2 hours, cost $40, and reached a level of performance that Hashimoto, one of the best engineers in the world at exactly this, says he couldn't have hit on his own.

Now here's the question worth wrestling with: who assigned that task?

It wasn't on a backlog. It wasn't in a sprint. No PM prioritized it. It didn't exist as a task until one person — an expert — suspected something new had become possible and spent money trying to find out. No individual process generates this task. No best practices guide contains it. It comes from somewhere else.

AI can only do work that someone has imagined. These cheap coding tools execute, but they don't decide what's worth executing. Which means the ceiling on what AI is worth to you was never the model or the price or the prompt pack. It's the size of your list of things you know how to ask for. It's your imagination.

## Execution isn't optional — it's the multiplier

This isn't an argument that execution doesn't matter. Execution matters enormously. It is the thing that gets multiplied by imagination. Hashimoto's $40 job still needed 2 hours of world-class model execution to become real. The point is about what sets that multiplier.

Driving down execution costs is a smart move, and you should keep making that move aggressively. It's a layer where you can control costs, and that control is yours. That's why Hashimoto ran the sub-dollar model in his initial test.

The mistake would be treating that layer as the whole strategy. The leverage comes from what you put on top of a strong execution strategy — a targeted, surgical application of frontier models to the questions that change what the execution layer is even building.

Cheap open execution is a great engine. Frontier imagination is where you steer the plane. You need both. They're not in competition. The cheaper your execution layer gets, the more commoditized it gets, and therefore the more valuable every frontier-prompted question becomes.

## Why everything looks the same

Look at the first half of Hashimoto's experiments again. Cheap model ties expensive model. That's not a fact about the models — it's a fact about the task.

"Implement this feature" is work everyone already knows how to ask for. And the work everyone knows how to ask for is exactly where the models have converged. That's why a $1 model ties a $9 model.

Now zoom out from models to people. The prompts we use are often shared. The playbooks are public. Many of us follow the same productivity channels. When you and a million other people run the same tasks across the same tools, of course the results converge. That's fine if you want similar quality at a cheap execution price. But AI isn't responsible for making our work outputs generic — it just reveals that differentiation is hard and that it's a human task.

## BlackBerry and Apple

We've watched this exact dynamic decide a market before.

BlackBerry and Apple both made smartphones. BlackBerry executed brilliantly — the best keyboard in the world, the best email, the best security, and the market leadership to prove it. What killed BlackBerry wasn't an execution failure. It was executing superbly inside a category everyone had already imagined. While Apple imagined a different answer to what a phone even was, and then executed on that.

Same industry, comparable execution muscle, and imagination set the multiplier. One company's execution became worth a hundred times the other's. That divergence starts exactly where the known list of execution tasks ends. That's where the models still separate — and much more importantly, that's where people will separate. The $40 job had no competition. Nobody else was doing what Hashimoto did, because nobody else had thought of it.

## The test

Here's a test that works for a person as well as a company. Has your task list changed in the last 12 months? In the last six? In the last three? Has what you've asked AI to do shifted? Or are you doing your old list faster and cheaper and calling that AI transformation?

If it's the old list, nothing is wrong with your tools. You have an imagination shortage, and you're going to be spending a lot on optimizing execution in a commoditized market.

## Imagination is a practice, not a gift

The good news is that imagination isn't what you think it is. The word gets overused. It sounds like a gift, something artists have and analysts don't. That's not what was operating in the $40 story, and it's not what's scarce.

Hashimoto could pose that question because he has hundreds or thousands of hours inside these models. He knows where the capability line has moved — not from a benchmark chart, but from instinct, from touch. You can't imagine with capabilities you haven't touched. Nobody imagines a use for a tool they've read a summary of.

And this is where most of us quietly sabotage ourselves. We interact with AI with cost savings in the back of our heads, or a defined task list we expect AI to do. We wonder if AI can help us go faster or cheaper. It's like pointing the telescope at the ground — at work you already have.

The questions that find new territory are different. You're asking: what can this do that I've never been able to even ask before?

## The porch-marketing example

Here's a real example. Someone got a frontier model to use Google Maps to map all the porches in a geographic area that are unshaded — that get all-day daylight in an area where average summer temperatures cross a certain threshold. Then find those properties, get a 3D model of the structure from Google Maps, and mail each homeowner a custom card offering a covered porch with specific data on their situation, including a visual of how their porch would look when built.

All of that powered by a frontier model. Can you get a cheaper model to execute parts of it once the idea is prototyped? Absolutely. But combining all those tasks together — running them through Blender, through other tools, managing the spatial and logical reasoning, and translating it all into a business flow with an address table — that's something earlier model versions just can't get you.

Once you imagine it and get the first few houses done, you can start moving parts of the pipeline back to simpler models. But the idea of putting it all together, analyzing where the sun is and where shade is — that's frontier model stuff. That takes imagination. Not just imagination to prompt the model. Imagination to say: a new kind of marketing is possible. I can be hyper-targeted, hyper-specific, and hyper-relevant to my customers because this model can do analysis that was never possible before.

This is not an artist-type imagination task. It's a technical, business imagination task — looking at a particular problem in a new way because you have fingertip awareness of what new models are capable of.

## The factory electrification parallel

When factories electrified, the technology worked on day one. The productivity payoff took decades — because factories kept their steam-era layout. Every machine crowded around one central drive shaft, and they just bolted an electric motor where the steam engine used to be. Same building, new power source, barely any gain.

The payoff arrived when a new generation of managers exercised technical imagination and redesigned the factory around what cheap distributed motors make possible. The unit of change wasn't the motor. It was the building.

AI is the same kind of tech, and companies are making the same move. They're bolting it onto the old layout. They're running the existing task list through cheaper models and reporting on the savings. The savings are real — and they're available to every competitor with the same insights. They're table stakes.

## Stripe's building redesign

Here's what redesigning the building looks like when it works. Stripe ran a migration across 50 million lines of code in one day — work estimated at two-plus months for a team. The impressive number isn't "a day." The important number is the years Stripe spent building task coverage that could verify that many changes, review systems that could move at that speed, and people who knew how to drive the model through a complicated task like that.

The model deleted two months of typed code — two man-months of typing. The building to accommodate that change — the team structures, the review cycles to verify quality — had been redesigned in advance. Point the same model at a company that hasn't done that work in their codebase and you wouldn't get a one-day migration. You'd get 50 million lines of changes that nobody could approve. Stripe built the infrastructure first, then harvested the value with frontier models and technical imagination.

## You can't hire your way out of this

A warning for leaders, because the shortcut you're thinking about won't work. You can't hire your way out of this with one imaginative person.

That $40 job needed imagination, deep context, and permission to ask — all in one head, as Hashimoto said. Your new AI visionary has all the imagination, sure, but none of your context. So ask yourself: do the people with the context in your company — the people who could exercise technical imagination — have the permission to do that? Do they have the tools?

Imagination only fires when it sits next to context, and your context is spread across everyone who actually does the work. The job isn't hiring imagination. It's manufacturing it — putting the people who have context in your systems in contact with capable models and giving them permission to make bets.

Who on your team is allowed to pose a $400 question to a model today without asking anyone? If the answer is nobody, or just a tiny number of people, that's an imagination constraint. It was never about the price of the model.

## What the blackout proved

One last piece of evidence that imagination is the asset. A few weeks ago, a frontier model shipped on a Tuesday and was gone by Friday. Everyone carefully optimized their model setups since. But look at what the blackout couldn't take away.

The people who had spent those first 72 hours dreaming of what was possible — the questions they posed were kept, the workflows they'd redesigned were kept. The model came back into a price war. But the people who had imagination to use the models went right back to imagining with it.

## Where your 10x comes from

This is the answer to the question of sameness. It was never the tools. The tools did their job — they made execution cheap, and you need to optimize for execution. Nothing here says don't do that.

You also have to have an answer for where your 10x multiplier comes from. Where your iPhone moment comes from. Where Hashimoto's $40 question comes from. Spend the frontier where it multiplies — where you have real context, real bets on the line, and real questions that exercise technical imagination around what's possible with a larger model.

There is no substitute for that.
