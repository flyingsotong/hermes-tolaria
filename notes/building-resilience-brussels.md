Singapore as an example

The Foreign Interference (Countermeasures) Act (FICA) was passed in Parliament on 4 October 2021.

Measures to prevent, detect and disrupt foreign interference in our domestic politics conducted through (i) hostile information campaigns (HICs) and (ii) the use of local proxies

HICs:

1. Inauthentic accounts

2. Bot activity

3. Ads for boosting

4. Creating artificial sense of public support or opposition to a point of view

5. Inciting trolls

6. Creating mass and fake accounts around benign topics like fashion and using those accounts for influence

**Escalation** Stop Communication (End-User) Direction  Disabling Direction  Must-Carry Direction - mandatory message from the Government in a conspicuous and timely manner Account Restriction Directions - block accounts from being seen in Singapore Access Blocking Direction - outright blocking of sites App Removal Direction

Penalties for breaking the law include fines running up to about $741,000 and 14 years in jail.

Problematic:

- vague provisions also allow for unfettered executive discretion in interpretation and implementation of the law

- Unlimited executive discretion – together with severe penalties under the law – can result in executive overreach into what it deems permissible as civic discussion and public debate

- provides no mechanism for independent judicial oversight or provision of remedy

- The government can also designate individuals as “politically significant persons” after which they can be required to follow strict limits on sources of funding and disclose all links with foreigners or foreign entities

- individuals can face criminal sanctions under the law for “clandestine foreign interference by electronic communications activity” and non-compliance with directions, which may result in steep fines and imprisonment terms. These criminal offences are arrestable and non-bailable
