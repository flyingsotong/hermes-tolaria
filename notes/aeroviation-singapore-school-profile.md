## The school in one paragraph

Aeroviation is a [simulator-based flight school](https://aeroviation.com/) at 30A Kallang Place in Singapore, founded by [Faris Iskandar](https://aeroviation.com/founder/) — an aerospace engineering graduate who moved from the private jet sector into aviation education. The school runs three programs: [Junior Aviator](https://aeroviation.com/junior-aviator/) (ages 7-12, $265-$325/month), [Young Aviator](https://aeroviation.com/young-aviator/) (ages 13-16, $300-$360/month), and a full [RAAus RPC pathway](https://aeroviation.com/recreational-pilot-certificate/) for students 15 and up. The RPC is a two-phase program: Phase 1 in Singapore (40 hours ground theory, 20 hours simulator on a TRC472FG Cessna 172 replica with Garmin G1000), then Phase 2 at a partner school in Australia (25 hours flight in a Jabiru, including 5 solo, plus the flight test). The school prices the entire RPC package at [SGD $18,911](https://aeroviation.com/recreational-pilot-certificate/) — the most transparent all-in cost we have seen from any school in the region, covering not just training but return airfares, accommodation, transfers, and insurance. Aeroviation also runs a [Seletar Discovery Flight](https://aeroviation.com/flight-experience/seletar-discovery-flight/) (SGD $2,000 for 5 hours ground, 3 hours sim, and 1 hour actual flight in a Bristell aircraft at Seletar Airport) and a schools/corporate program called [Air-Venture](https://aeroviation.com/flight-training/schools-corporate/).

## How we assess schools — adapted for this profile

Magenta Standard profiles evaluate flight schools across five pillars. Every pillar maps to a decision a prospective student is actually making.

We built this framework around traditional flight schools — the kind where you show up at an airfield and fly. Aeroviation is different. It is a simulator-grounded school that handles ground theory and procedural training in Singapore, then sends students to Australia for the flight component. The five pillars still apply, but the lens shifts:

- **Pathway clarity** — unchanged. Does the school map the full route from enrollment to license?
- **Cost integrity** — applies directly. Is the pricing complete and upfront?
- **Training environment** — reframed. The training environment here is the simulator facility first, the Australian partner airfield second. We assess both.
- **Instructor quality** — unchanged. Who teaches and what are their qualifications?
- **Overseas readiness** — inverted. For a Singapore school, this means how the Australia phase is handled: visa support, accommodation, logistics, and cap discipline.

## Pillar 1 — Pathway clarity

**Verdict: Strong.**

Aeroviation publishes [three distinct programs](https://aeroviation.com/flight-training/) with clear age ranges, pricing, and outcomes.

The [Junior Aviator](https://aeroviation.com/junior-aviator/) and [Young Aviator](https://aeroviation.com/young-aviator/) programs are 3-year progressive curricula (Beginner → Intermediate → Advanced, one year per level), each with 36 sessions per year. Pricing is published per level. These are educational pathways — no license outcome — and the site is upfront about that.

The [RPC program](https://aeroviation.com/recreational-pilot-certificate/) is documented in unusual detail. The page breaks down exactly what is included across two phases:

- **Phase 1 (Singapore):** 40 hours ground theory, 20 hours simulator training, uniform set, learning materials
- **Phase 2 (Australia):** 15 hours dual flight, 5 hours solo, 5 additional flight hours, RAAus membership, theory exams, flight test with CFI, license lodgement
- **Travel & accommodation:** Return flights (national carrier), 4-star hotel, transfers, travel insurance, tourist visa for Singaporeans

What is not published: a lesson-by-lesson syllabus or a weekly schedule for Phase 1. The 40-hour ground theory and 20-hour sim blocks are stated as totals but not mapped across weeks. The Australian phase is also described in total hour blocks rather than a session-by-session plan. For a school targeting young students and their parents, a more granular schedule would help families understand the time commitment.

## Pillar 2 — Cost integrity

**Verdict: Strong.** This is Aeroviation's best pillar.

The [RPC is priced at SGD $18,911](https://aeroviation.com/recreational-pilot-certificate/) including GST — one all-in number that covers ground theory, simulator, return airfares, accommodation, transfers, insurance, RAAus membership, exams, and the flight test. This is the most transparent single price we have seen across every school we have profiled. A student or parent can read that number and understand the total financial commitment before making a phone call.

The [Junior Aviator](https://aeroviation.com/junior-aviator/) and [Young Aviator](https://aeroviation.com/young-aviator/) programs are priced per month by level ($265-$325/mo and $300-$360/mo respectively), also clearly stated.

The [Seletar Discovery Flight](https://aeroviation.com/flight-experience/seletar-discovery-flight/) is SGD $2,000 exclusive of GST, with three components listed: 5 hours ground, 3 hours simulator, 1 hour actual flight.

What is not published: the cost breakdown between Aeroviation's Singapore phase and the partner Australian school. A prospective student cannot tell how much of the $18,911 stays with Aeroviation versus goes to the Australian operator. The school/corporate Air-Venture program also does not list pricing on the website.

## Pillar 3 — Training environment (adapted)

**Verdict: Strong for the simulator phase; qualified for the Australia phase.**

Aeroviation's [Singapore facility](https://aeroviation.com/discover-flight/training-facility/) is a 5,000 sq ft space at Kallang Place with 4 flight simulators, 4 classrooms, and dedicated zones including a conference room, lounge, and an area called the Aeroverse. The two primary simulators are:

- **[Cessna 172 Skyhawk (TRC472FG)](https://aeroviation.com/discover-flight/cessna-172-flight-simulator/):** An exact replica cockpit with Garmin G1000 glass cockpit and 180-degree HD visual system. Supports takeoffs/landings, emergency procedures, and scenario-based training.
- **[Diamond 20 (DV20/DA20 Katana)](https://aeroviation.com/discover-flight/diamond-20-flight-simulator/):** A Belgian-made simulator — the newest addition — with a 240-degree horizontal by 40-degree vertical HD visual system and control force loading on all three axes (pitch, roll, yaw). This is a serious piece of training equipment that exceeds what most Australian RAAus schools have on site.

The facility is purpose-built for procedural training. A student can learn flows, checklists, radio calls, and emergency responses on a high-fidelity sim before ever sitting in an aircraft. That is the value proposition.

The Australia phase is less transparent. The partner school is not named on Aeroviation's website. "Jabiru aircraft" is mentioned but not which operator, at which airfield, or under what supervision structure. The RAAus registration process and flight test are described generically. An overseas student researching the program cannot independently verify the quality of the flight training operator they will be sent to.

Weather at the Australian location is not discussed — a notable gap given that weather was a material constraint in every Australian school we have profiled, and the flight phase involves a fixed timeline with pre-booked return flights.

## Pillar 4 — Instructor quality

**Verdict: Moderate.** The team is named but not described.

Aeroviation lists [6 instructors on its website](https://aeroviation.com/future-of-aviation/the-team/):

- James Hubbard — Chief Flight Instructor
- Mirza Ariffin — Senior Flight Instructor
- Matt Coleman — Senior Flight Instructor
- Irfan Khan — Junior Flight Instructor
- Foo Yang — Junior Flight Instructor
- Rezeki Hamdan — Junior Flight Instructor

The [founder, Faris Iskandar](https://aeroviation.com/founder/), has an aerospace diploma and a mechanical engineering degree with experience in the private jet sector.

What is not published: instructor credentials, flight hours, instructional experience, or supervision structure. Names and titles alone do not help a prospective student judge who they will be learning from. The Australian partner school's instructor team is also not named or described.

The author's personal experience training at Aeroviation gave him direct exposure to the instruction quality. All 15 hours of simulator training were conducted by the same instructor, in the same simulator, which provided consistency — no rotating pool of random freelancers, no hour-building instructors cycling through. That consistency is a structural advantage of the simulator-grounded model: instruction is not weather-dependent, not aircraft-availability-dependent, and can be delivered on a predictable schedule.

## Pillar 5 — Overseas readiness (inverted)

**Verdict: Strong for Singapore students; not assessed for others.**

This pillar is inverted because Aeroviation is a Singapore school serving Singapore students. The "overseas" question is about the Australia leg of the program, not about whether a foreign student can train in Singapore.

Aeroviation publishes the Australia travel logistics in detail: return flights on national carriers, 4-star hotel accommodation, return airport transfers, daily transportation, travel and medical insurance, and tourist visa application for Singaporeans. This is a level of logistical transparency that most traditional flight schools do not match.

The partner school in Australia is not named, and the supervision structure there is not described. A student or parent cannot independently research where their child will be flying.

For students from outside Singapore (Malaysia, Hong Kong, Thailand, etc.), the website says nothing. Visa arrangements, accommodation, and logistics for non-Singaporean international students are not addressed.

## The Magenta take

Aeroviation is doing something the Australian schools we have profiled do not: publishing an all-in price for a complete RPC pathway, documenting the two-phase structure clearly, and building a dedicated simulator facility that exceeds what most RAAus schools have on the flight line.

The strength of the model is the Singapore phase. A young student can learn ground theory, practice procedures, build scan skills, and make mistakes on a high-fidelity simulator before stepping onto an aircraft in Australia. That is safer, cheaper, and more time-efficient than learning those same lessons at $360/hr in the air.

The gap is the Australian partner. Aeroviation publishes nothing about who operates the flight component, where it happens, who the instructors are, or what the aircraft maintenance record looks like. A student buying the [SGD $18,911 package](https://aeroviation.com/recreational-pilot-certificate/) is trusting that Aeroviation has vetted the partner thoroughly — but the student cannot verify that trust from the website alone.

If Aeroviation named the Australian operator, published a syllabus map for both phases, and added instructor credentials to the team page, this would be one of the most transparent flight training pathways in the region. As it stands, the Singapore side is best-in-class for a simulator school. The Australia side is a black box.

## Who should train here

- Singapore-based students who want to learn procedures and build scan skills on a high-fidelity simulator before committing to flight hours in Australia
- Parents looking for a structured, progressive aviation program for children aged 7-16 with clear pricing and a documented pathway to a license
- Students who value cost transparency and want a single all-in number for their RPC rather than estimating from an hourly rate
- Young students targeting the RAAus RPC as a first step, with the option to continue to RPL/PPL/CPL through an Australian school afterward

## Who should look elsewhere

- Students who want to see a named flight school, named instructors, and aircraft maintenance records for the Australia phase before paying
- International students from outside Singapore who need visa support, accommodation, or published policies for non-Singaporean applicants
- Students who prefer a single-location training experience without a cross-border travel component
- Students who want a GA pathway (RPL/PPL/CPL) from the same operator — Aeroviation's RPC is RAAus only, and onward GA training requires finding a separate CASA school
