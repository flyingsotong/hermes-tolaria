# Bernoulli’s Principle

**As the speed of a fluid (like air) increases, its pressure decreases.**

Faster air = lower pressure

Air flowing over the top surface of a wing travels a longer distance and thus faster than the air flowing under the bottom surface.

This difference in speed creates a pressure difference:

**Lower pressure on top, higher pressure on the bottom – and that higher pressure pushes the wing upwards.**

_Think of it like two lanes of traffic. The top of the wing is like a longer, winding lane, while the bottom is a shorter, straighter lane. Cars in the longer lane have to go faster to cover the same distance in the same amount of time. The faster-moving air on top of the wing has lower pressure, just like the faster-moving cars in the winding lane._


**How This Applies to a Stall**

**Angle of Attack:** The angle of attack is the angle between the wing and the oncoming airflow. As the angle of attack increases, the air flowing over the wing has to travel a longer distance and faster compared to the air flowing underneath.

**Lift Generation:** This difference in speed creates a pressure difference, with lower pressure above the wing and higher pressure below. This pressure difference generates lift, which is what keeps the aircraft in the air.

**Critical Angle:** However, there's a limit to how much the angle of attack can be increased. At a certain point, called the critical angle of attack, the airflow over the wing becomes turbulent and separates from the wing's surface.

**Stall:** When this happens, the pressure difference is lost, and lift dramatically decreases, causing the aircraft to stall.

T_hink of a hand out of a car window. If you tilt your hand at a slight angle, the air flows smoothly over it, and you feel lift. But if you tilt it too much, the air starts to "burble" and your hand suddenly drops. That's similar to what happens during a stall._
