**1. Professional identity transformation**

This emerges when journalists realize their traditional definition of journalism may be limiting them, as seen with Raastineeda Shaikh (realizing broadcast skills could translate to other forms of storytelling), Julianna Barcela (struggling to see value beyond traditional newsroom roles), Catherine Wang (grappling with evolving media landscape), and Aniket Narawad (fixating on traditional writing when his data skills are valuable).

Deep coaching questions:
- "How might your definition of journalism be limiting your view of possible futures?"
- "What would your work look like if you focused on impact rather than format?"
- "How are you defining 'real journalism' and who taught you that definition?"
- "What skills and values would remain if we removed the 'journalist' label?"
- "How might your varied experiences actually be preparing you for journalism's future?"
- "What assumptions about journalism are you making that may no longer be true?"
- "How could your unique background help reinvent what journalism means?"
- "What parts of your work bring you alive, regardless of whether they fit traditional journalism?"

---

**2. Confidence and self-worth in transition**

This appears when journalists question their value outside traditional roles, seen with Kate Delos Reyes (perfectionism blocking entrepreneurial moves), Thea Alberto-Masakayan (questioning her value in a new role), Kelly Le (doubting her non-traditional path), and Aidila Razak (feeling guilty about potentially leaving journalism).

Deep coaching questions:
- "What validation are you seeking from traditional journalism that you could give yourself?"
- "How might your diverse experiences be an advantage rather than a liability?"
- "What would it look like to define success on your own terms, not journalism's?"
- "Whose voice of criticism do you hear when you consider leaving traditional media?"
- "What strengths have your varied experiences given you that 'pure' journalists might lack?"
- "How might your ability to see beyond traditional journalism actually be a superpower?"
- "What permission do you need to give yourself to explore new definitions of your work?"
- "What would you attempt if you weren't bound by traditional journalism expectations?"

---

**3. Entrepreneurial identity and sustainability**

This surfaces when journalists try to create new models of media work, seen with Ankur Paliwal (building Queerbeat), Rachael Akidi Okwir (transitioning from BBC to consulting), Mike Tatarski (evolving his newsletter beyond traditional journalism), and Emmanuel Samarathisa (building a sustainable newsletter business).

Deep coaching questions:
- "How might your journalism skills translate into creating new kinds of value?"
- "What unique perspective could you bring to reinventing media business models?"
- "What assumptions about journalism business models need challenging?"
- "How could your understanding of storytelling create new forms of value?"
- "What would success look like if you weren't bound by traditional media metrics?"
- "How might your journalism background actually be your entrepreneurial advantage?"
- "What experiments could you run to test new forms of media value creation?"
- "What fears about leaving traditional journalism are holding back your innovation?"

---

**Common threads across categories**

- Need to challenge inherited definitions of journalism
- Struggle to see skills as transferable beyond traditional newsrooms
- Fear of betraying journalism ideals
- Difficulty valuing non-traditional media work
- Resistance to evolving definitions of media impact

When coaching, listen for references to "real journalism" or "true journalism," assumptions about what "counts" as legitimate media work, guilt about exploring non-traditional paths, fixation on traditional newsroom validation, and difficulty seeing skills as valuable in new contexts.
