# RFTW coaching Vignesh Vellore

**August 27, 2025**
This was a highly effective coaching session. You successfully reframed Vignesh's tactical challenges into a more fundamental strategic question about his company's core identity and value proposition.
**What Vignesh Initially Wanted**
Vignesh came into the call with several specific, tactical problems he wanted help with, all stemming from the challenge of growing reader revenue for his and other small, editorially-driven media outlets in India.
His primary requests were:
1.**Data Strategy for Conversion:** His most urgent need was to figure out how to use his existing audience data—specifically a list of 100,000 email addresses—to**convert more users into paying members**. He was focused on the mechanics of email marketing, A/B testing discounts, and analyzing the user journey to reduce drop-offs.
2.**Community Building:** He wanted to know how to build a more engaged and loyal community that would support The News Minute for the long term, expressing a desire to grow his monthly editorial meetings from 50 attendees to 200.
3.**Content and Video Feedback:** He sought an external, expert evaluation of his successful YouTube shows. He wanted to know if the format, content, and anchors were effective enough to be placed behind a paywall.
Essentially, he was looking for a better**playbook** to optimize his existing reader revenue model.
**What He Was Surprised to Get**
While Vignesh came seeking tactical advice, you provided a crucial strategic reframing that he was surprised and receptive to.
The key surprise for him was the challenge to his core assumption about his own business. Instead of just offering tips on optimizing his "article-first" subscription model, you pointed out a truth he hadn't fully embraced:
>**You might be a "YouTube-first" media company that excels at generating one-time payments.**
This insight shifted the entire conversation from "How do we fix our current model?" to "What business are we _really_ in?" You helped him see that his audience's behavior was already telling him what was working.
He was surprised to be pushed to**deconstruct his entire narrative** about selling journalism and instead focus on understanding the**audience's intent**. The big takeaway for him wasn't a new email tactic, but the fundamental question: "**Why are people paying us?**" This is evident in his closing remarks, where he acknowledges being "stuck in a rut" thinking journalism was the only answer and realizing it "could be something else."
**What You Could Have Done Better**
The call was very strong, but there are a couple of areas where you could refine your approach for even greater impact.
1.**Provide a "Quick Win" Alongside the Big Picture:** You correctly pivoted to the high-level strategic issue. However, since Vignesh came in with a pressing tactical problem (the 1,000 users who dropped off at the payment page), you could have offered one small, actionable suggestion for that specific issue _before_ zooming out. For example, suggesting a simple 2-step automated email sequence for that segment. This would have satisfied his initial request and built immediate trust, making him even more receptive to the larger strategic reframing.
2.**Assign More Concrete "Homework":** The call ended with a general encouragement to "have a think about that" and "go back to the drawing board." To make the insights more actionable, you could have assigned specific, tangible tasks. For example:
- "For our next call, I want you to personally email 15 of your most loyal supporters and ask them that one question: 'What do we give you that no one else does?' Bring their verbatim answers."
- "Identify the top 3 hypotheses for why your YouTube audience pays and your website audience doesn't. We'll design experiments to test them next time."
This creates clearer accountability and a focused agenda for the follow-up.
**Plan for the Next Call**
The next session should build directly on the "deconstruction" theme from the first call. The goal is to move from insight to action.
Here is a potential agenda:
1.**Debrief on Audience Intent:** Start by asking what Vignesh learned.
- Did he reach out to any users? What did he discover about _why_ they pay?
- What were the key themes from his team's discussions about "truths we aren't willing to accept"?
1.**Define the Two Audiences:** Solidify the hypothesis that he has (at least) two distinct audiences:
-**The YouTube Fan:** Who are they? What is their motivation (entertainment, unique perspective, connection with the anchor)? What job are they "hiring" the shows to do for them?
-**The Article Loyalist:** Who are they? What drives them (supporting a cause, deep-dive investigations, regional focus)?
1.**Brainstorm Strategies for Each Segment:** Based on the defined personas, brainstorm separate value propositions and engagement strategies.
-**For the YouTube Fan:** Instead of a paywall, how can they double down on what works? Ideas could include member-only Q\&As with the show's host, a call for user-submitted topics, or exclusive behind-the-scenes content funded by one-time payments.
-**For the Article Loyalist:** How can the value proposition be framed around**impact and belonging** rather than just content access? Discuss strategies for making their support feel more tangible (e.g., "Your subscription funded this specific investigation").
1.**Connect Strategy Back to the Merger:** Revisit his long-term vision of consolidating smaller newsrooms. How does this new, nuanced understanding of audience and value affect which media outlets he should acquire and how he should integrate them?
