# Slugs: The untrainable is the moat

Hi Friend,

Every media operator I talk to is asking some version of the same question: if production is free and distribution is algorithmic, what vessel am I actually building?

Sarah Guo of Greylock published an essay this week that gives you a way to answer it. She calls the current moment "mid-2026 AI psychosis" — the VC consensus swinging from "everything is investable" to "nothing is investable" in eighteen months. The model layer is commoditizing so fast that "what's left for us?" has become a shared reflex across every industry that touches information.

THE UNTRAINABLE CORNER

The "thin wrapper" panic is the media version of this psychosis. You build on top of someone else's model, someone else's distribution, someone else's platform — and if those layers are all being commoditized, what exactly are you owning? Guo's framework gives you a way to think past the panic.

Value doesn't disappear. It migrates toward work that can't be scored on a public leaderboard. "A benchmark is a thing you can measure," she writes, "and a thing you can measure is a thing you can train against." Anything you can measure, a well-trained model can probably do. The work that survives is the work whose correctness is private, per-firm, and illegible to external benchmarks.

This is the core insight: the untrainable corner is about work a model can't be evaluated on — because the evaluation itself is private. What this firm, on this kind of matter, will accept as good work is a judgment that lives inside your operation, your community, your subscriber base. No public benchmark can score it.

The absorption frontier keeps rising — we keep learning to measure more of the work, and the measurable keeps getting eaten. Today's untrainable work is tomorrow's benchmark. But that doesn't mean the category disappears. The gap just keeps moving. The race is to stay illegible: to keep arranging your private reality — your sources, your relationships, your institutional knowledge — in ways that a general-purpose model can't be judged on.

"Intelligence keeps getting cheaper," Guo writes, "and value keeps moving toward the untrainable, illegible, and firm-specific."

So here's the question I have for you, FNAME. The untrainable work in your newsroom — is it the journalism, or is it the admin?
