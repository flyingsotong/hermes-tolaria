Here are the right-of-way rules specifically under Australian air law, primarily governed by the Civil Aviation Safety Authority (CASA) through the Civil Aviation Safety Regulations (CASR) 1998, particularly Part 91 (General operating and flight rules), and supplemented by guidance in the Aeronautical Information Publication (AIP) Australia.
**General Principles (Australia)**
- **Pilot Responsibility:** Regardless of right-of-way rules, the pilot in command (PIC) operating in Visual Meteorological Conditions (VMC) is responsible for maintaining vigilance to see and avoid other aircraft (CASR 91.340).
- **Giving Way:** An aircraft required to give way must take early and adequate action to keep well clear and avoid passing over, under, or ahead of the other aircraft unless passing well clear (CASR 91.345, AIP ENR 1.1).
- **Maintaining Course:** An aircraft that has the right-of-way must generally maintain its heading and speed. However, the PIC is not relieved of the responsibility to take action to avoid a collision (CASR 91.345).
- **ATC Instructions:** Instructions issued by Air Traffic Control (ATC) always take precedence over these general right-of-way rules, particularly in controlled airspace or at controlled aerodromes.
**In-Flight Right-of-Way Rules (CASR 91.345)**
1.**Distress:** An aircraft in distress has the right-of-way over all other air traffic. Pilots aware of an aircraft compelled to land (e.g., emergency) must give way.
2.**Converging Aircraft:**
- **Different Categories:** When aircraft of different categories converge at roughly the same altitude (except head-on), the right-of-way hierarchy is (each has right-of-way over those listed below it):
 - Balloons
 * Gliders
 - Airships
 * Aircraft towing other aircraft or objects
 - Power-driven heavier-than-air aircraft (aeroplanes, helicopters, gyroplanes) _(Note: This means powered aircraft must give way to balloons, gliders, airships, and aircraft towing something)._
- **Same Category (or converging power-driven aircraft):** When aircraft of the same category (or power-driven aircraft) converge at approximately the same altitude, the aircraft that has the other on its _right_ must give way. The aircraft giving way should generally alter heading to the right to pass behind.
1.**Approaching Head-On:** When two aircraft are approaching head-on, or nearly so, and there is a risk of collision, _both_ pilots must alter their heading to the _right_.
2.**Overtaking:** An aircraft being overtaken has the right-of-way. The overtaking aircraft (defined as approaching from the rear within 70 degrees of the other aircraft's plane of symmetry) must keep out of the way by altering its heading to the _right_, regardless of whether climbing, descending, or in level flight. The overtaking aircraft remains responsible for keeping clear until well past the overtaken aircraft.
3.**Landing:**
- **Aircraft landing or on final approach to land have the right-of-way over other aircraft in flight or operating on the surface (manoeuvring area).**
* When two or more heavier-than-air aircraft are approaching an aerodrome to land, the aircraft at the _lower_ altitude has the right-of-way. However, the lower aircraft must _not_ take advantage of this rule to cut in front of another1 aircraft on final approach or to overtake that aircraft.
**On-the-Ground Right-of-Way Rules (Taxiing - Primarily AIP & CASR Part 91 Subpart 91.D.5)**
Rules on the ground are heavily influenced by ATC instructions at controlled aerodromes and established local procedures at non-controlled aerodromes.
1.**ATC Control:** At controlled aerodromes, pilots must follow ATC instructions for all ground movements. A taxi clearance is required, and specific clearance must be obtained before entering or crossing _any_ runway. Holding points must not be crossed without clearance.
2.**Runway Operations:**
- Aircraft must not take off unless there is no apparent risk of collision.
* Aircraft exiting a runway after landing should be given priority over aircraft waiting to enter the runway or taxiing nearby. Expedite clearing the runway.
- Pilots must read back any ATC clearance or instruction to hold short of, enter, cross, line up on, take off from, land on, or backtrack on any runway.
1.**General Taxiing & Aprons:**
- While CASR doesn't explicitly detail a simple "converging give way to the right" rule for taxiing as comprehensively as for flight, pilots must exercise extreme vigilance.
* When taxiing, give way to aircraft already established on a taxiway you are entering, and give way when leaving a holding bay to join the main taxiway.
- Collision avoidance on aprons and taxiways is a shared responsibility between pilots, vehicle drivers, and ground crew. ATC clearances regulate flow but do not guarantee separation from all obstacles or vehicles, especially on aprons.
1.**Vehicles:** Ground vehicles must give way to taxiing aircraft unless specifically directed otherwise by ATC or when performing essential safety duties (e.g., emergency response, aircraft escort). Vehicles require ATC clearance to enter the manoeuvring area (runways and taxiways).
In summary, Australian rules align closely with international ICAO standards, emphasizing pilot responsibility, a clear hierarchy for converging aircraft in flight, specific actions for head-on and overtaking scenarios, priority for landing aircraft, and strict adherence to ATC instructions, especially for ground movements and runway operations.
