**Fixed-Pitch Propellers**
(Common on many Cessna 152s, 172s)
Many basic training Cessnas like the 152 and some 172s have fixed-pitch propellers. This means the angle (or "pitch") of the propeller blades is set by the manufacturer and cannot be changed by the pilot during flight.
**How to think about it:**
-**Like a single gear on a bicycle or car:** You have one "gear" for your propeller. It's a compromise.
-**RPM is directly tied to airspeed and throttle:**
 -**More throttle** means**more RPM**.
 -**Higher airspeed** means**more RPM** (because the propeller is "biting" more air).
 -**Climbing** means**lower RPM** (due to slower airspeed and increased load).
 -**Descending** means**higher RPM** (due to higher airspeed and decreased load).
-**Limitations:** Since you can't adjust the pitch, a fixed-pitch propeller is optimized for a certain phase of flight (e.g., a balance between climb and cruise). This means it won't be as efficient in all flight regimes as a constant-speed prop.
**Pilot Control:** You only have a**throttle** control (black knob) to manage engine power. The propeller RPM will vary with throttle and airspeed.
**Constant-Speed Propellers**
(Common on many Cessna 182s, 206s, and other higher-performance Cessnas)
More advanced Cessnas feature constant-speed propellers. These propellers have blades whose pitch can be changed in flight by the pilot, typically via a**propeller control lever** (blue knob, usually between the throttle and mixture controls).
**How it works:**
-**Propeller Governor:** A constant-speed propeller system uses a "governor" to automatically adjust the blade pitch. The pilot sets a desired engine RPM using the blue propeller control lever. The governor then adds or releases engine oil pressure to change the blade angle and maintain that selected RPM, regardless of changes in airspeed or throttle.
-**Blade Pitch and RPM:**
 -**Fine Pitch (Low Pitch / High RPM):** The blades are at a smaller angle to the airflow. This is like a "low gear" on a bicycle.
 -**Provides maximum thrust for takeoff and climb:** The engine spins faster, generating more power.
 -**Used for landing/go-around:** Provides immediate power if needed.
 -**Coarse Pitch (High Pitch / Low RPM):** The blades are at a larger angle to the airflow. This is like a "high gear" on a bicycle.
 -**Provides efficiency for cruise:** The engine spins slower, reducing wear and fuel consumption while still maintaining speed.
 -**Less drag:** Useful for dives to prevent overspeeding.
**Pilot Control (Throttle, Propeller, Mixture):**
You have three primary controls:
1.**Throttle (Black Knob):** Controls the amount of air/fuel mixture going into the engine, thus regulating**manifold pressure (MP)** – essentially the engine's power output.
2.**Propeller (Blue Knob):** Selects the desired**engine RPM**. The governor then adjusts the blade pitch to maintain this RPM.
3.**Mixture (Red Knob):** Adjusts the fuel-to-air ratio for optimal combustion at different altitudes.
**How to think about it:**
-**Like a multi-gear bicycle or car with an automatic transmission:** You select your desired engine RPM (gear), and the propeller system automatically adjusts the blade angle to maintain that RPM, optimizing for the desired performance.
-**Matching Power and RPM:** The key to using a constant-speed prop effectively is understanding the relationship between manifold pressure (throttle) and RPM (propeller control).
 -**For Takeoff/Climb:** Propeller is usually**full forward (fine pitch / max RPM)** for maximum power. Throttle is also full forward.
 -**For Cruise:** You typically reduce both RPM and manifold pressure to achieve a desired power setting (e.g., 65-75% power for efficiency). You'll typically**"square"** the settings, meaning you might set 2300 RPM and 23 inches of manifold pressure, for example, based on your aircraft's performance charts (POH). Lower RPMs in cruise generally mean lower noise and better fuel economy.
 -**For Landing/Go-Around:** Propeller is brought back to**full forward (fine pitch / max RPM)** as part of the pre-landing checklist. This ensures maximum power is immediately available for a go-around if needed.
**Important Order of Operations (Power Changes):**
-**Increasing Power:** Increase**RPM first**, then increase**manifold pressure (throttle)**. (**Prop before Power**)
-**Decreasing Power:** Decrease**manifold pressure (throttle) first**, then decrease**RPM**. (**Power before Prop**)
This order prevents the engine from "lugging" (operating at too low an RPM for the power output, which can be damaging) or overspeeding. Always refer to your specific aircraft's Pilot's Operating Handbook (POH) for exact settings and procedures.
