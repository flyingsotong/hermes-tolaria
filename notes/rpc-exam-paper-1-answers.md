# RPC Exam Paper 1 Answers

Okay, here are the answers to your RPC Exam questions, along with explanations based on standard Australian aviation regulations and practices (primarily CASA regulations and AIP, which RAAus operations generally align with).


**1) To maintain VFR when flying in Class G airspace below 10,000ft, the minimum visibility is:**

- **Answer:** 5,000m

- **Why:** In Australia, Visual Flight Rules (VFR) in Class G airspace below 10,000 ft AMSL require a flight visibility of at least 5 kilometres (5000 metres) and specific distances from cloud (1,500m horizontally, 1000ft vertically when above 3000ft AMSL/1000ft AGL). [Reference: AIP ENR 1.2]

**2) What is the minimum height at which you may fly above a closely settled area, provided that you are able to glide clear of buildings, dwellings, and persons in the event of an engine failure?**

- **Answer:** 1,000 FT AGL

- **Why:** Regulations (CAR 157) state that aircraft must not fly over a city, town, or populous area at a height lower than 1,000 ft above the highest obstacle within a 600m radius. The ability to glide clear is an additional consideration for safety/emergency planning but doesn't change the base minimum altitude requirement over populous areas.

**3) Is hypoxia a condition relative to the amount of oxygen available to the cells, and what is insufficient for normal functioning?**

- **Answer:** Yes, and this can be a problem even below 5,000ft for smokers and people with respiratory illness

- **Why:** Hypoxia is indeed insufficient oxygen for the body's cells. While altitude is the primary factor, factors like smoking (carbon monoxide reduces oxygen transport), poor health, certain medications, and fatigue can lower an individual's tolerance, making them susceptible to hypoxic effects even at relatively low altitudes (below 5000ft).

**4) Shaik had planned to fly his Jabiru... What could he have done differently?**

- **Answer:** All of the above

- **Why:** This scenario highlights multiple failures in airmanship and planning:

 - **Delayed the flight:** Addresses the issue of departing late and potentially flying into deteriorating weather or darkness.

 - **Obtained weather/checked daylight:** Crucial pre-flight actions that would have revealed the poor conditions and approaching darkness.

 - **Ensure clear features:** While important for navigation, it wouldn't solve the weather/light issues but is part of good VFR planning. Addressing all these points represents sound Aeronautical Decision Making (ADM).

**5) How does haze affect the ability to see traffic during daylight?**

- **Answer:** All traffic appears further away than actual distance

- **Why:** Haze scatters light and reduces contrast. This makes objects appear less distinct and can create an illusion that they are farther away than they actually are, potentially delaying collision avoidance actions.

**6) Sustained exposure to high noise levels can cause temporary or permanent hearing damage. For this to occur, the noise level would need to be in excess of**

- **Answer:** 80DB

- **Why:** Occupational health standards generally recognise that sustained exposure to noise levels above 80-85 decibels (dB) can lead to hearing damage. Light aircraft cockpits often exceed this level, making hearing protection advisable.

**7) When approaching an aerodrome, when should an inbound radio call be made?**

- **Answer:** 10nm, or at an appropriate time and distance for the performance of your aircraft

- **Why:** Standard procedure at non-towered aerodromes (CTAF) in Australia is to make an inbound call by 10 NM out (AIP ENR 1.1). The addition of "appropriate time and distance" allows for variations based on aircraft speed or specific local procedures, but 10 NM is the baseline requirement.

**8) Which of the following calls is correct when describing Jabiru (Aircraft type) registration 24-1234?**

- **Answer:** Jabiru, one, two, three, four

- **Why:** Standard radiotelephony requires numbers in a registration to be spoken individually. While the full callsign is technically "Jabiru Two Four One Two Three Four," abbreviated callsigns often use the type and the last three or four digits _after_ initial contact. "One Two Three Four" represents the individual pronunciation of the numerical part (1234). Grouping ("twelve, thirty four") is incorrect. Assuming the question focuses on pronouncing the number part individually (the most standard method for numbers), 'a' is the best fit among the flawed options. [Reference: AIP GEN 3.4]

**9) Whilst conducting circuits at a Certified aerodrome in Australia, you suspect the radio has failed. The correct procedure is to?**

- **Answer:** Keep broadcasting pre-fixed with 'transmitting blind', land and discontinue circuits

- **Why:** If radio failure is suspected in the circuit (especially at a certified aerodrome where radio is usually required), the procedure involves prefixing calls with "TRANSMITTING BLIND" to alert others, continuing the current circuit pattern very cautiously while looking for light signals from the tower (if applicable) or other traffic, landing as soon as practicable, and clearing the runway. Continuing circuits indefinitely is unsafe.

**10) If flying through heavy rain, other objects may appear?**

- **Answer:** Further away than they really are

- **Why:** Similar to haze, heavy rain reduces visibility and distorts light transmission, making objects appear less distinct and farther away than their actual distance.

**11) The maximum distance a pilot could expect to see another aircraft is?**

- **Answer:** Both A and C

- **Why:** Visual detection range is highly variable (A: depends on pilot health, light, contrast, visibility). Polarizing sunglasses can sometimes reduce the visibility of glint from other aircraft canopies or surfaces, potentially hindering detection (C). Therefore, both statements are relevant.

**12) Take-off and landing distance required is:**

- **Answer:** Increased by a tailwind and decreased by a headwind

- **Why:** A headwind reduces the groundspeed required to achieve lift-off speed and reduces groundspeed on landing, thus shortening both distances. A tailwind increases the groundspeed required, lengthening both distances.

**13) Which part of the ear will be most affected by pressure changes if the Eustachian tube is blocked?**

- **Answer:** The middle ear

- **Why:** The Eustachian tube connects the middle ear to the back of the throat to equalize pressure between the middle ear and the outside atmosphere. If it's blocked, pressure differences build up across the eardrum, causing pain and pressure sensations within the middle ear cavity.

**14) If the engine fails at 150ft in a climb after take-off the pilots' first action should be to:**

- **Answer:** Pitch the nose down and attain best glide speed

- **Why:** The absolute first priority after an engine failure at low altitude is to maintain control by preventing a stall. This requires immediately lowering the nose to maintain or establish the aircraft's best glide airspeed (Vg​). Only then can the pilot focus on selecting a suitable landing area (usually straight ahead or slightly offset).

**15) If thick frost is not cleared from the wings and control surfaces of a recreational aircraft?**

- **Answer:** Take-off distance and stalling speed will increase

- **Why:** Frost disrupts the smooth airflow over the wing surface. This decreases lift, increases drag, significantly increases the stalling speed (the aircraft stalls at a higher airspeed), and requires a longer take-off run.

**16) Aeronautical Decision Making is best described by which of the following statements?**

- **Answer:** The process of detecting, evaluating, responding and re-assessing any flight situation

- **Why:** ADM is a systematic, continuous process. It involves recognizing changes or hazards (detecting), assessing the risks involved (evaluating), deciding on a course of action (responding), and continuously monitoring the outcome and situation (re-assessing). Models like DECIDE (Detect, Estimate, Choose, Identify, Do, Evaluate) embody this.

**17) Aircraft A is on final approach and aircraft B is taxying and has not yet entered the runway to line up. Which of the following statements is true?**

- **Answer:** Aircraft A has the right of way

- **Why:** Aircraft landing or on final approach have priority over other aircraft in the air or operating on the ground (CAR 162). Aircraft B must hold short and give way to Aircraft A.

**18) Pilots use standardized radio calls because of the limitations of:**

- **Answer:** The short term memory

- **Why:** Standard phraseology reduces ambiguity and the mental workload (cognitive load) needed to process and formulate radio calls. This helps compensate for the known limitations of human short-term memory, especially under pressure or in busy airspace, ensuring clearer communication.

**19) If you finish your last alcoholic drink at 10 pm and you intend to fly the next day, when can you legally fly?**

- **Answer:** 6 am

- **Why:** Regulations (CASR 91.745) stipulate a minimum of 8 hours must pass between consuming alcohol and acting as flight crew ('bottle to throttle'). 10 pm plus 8 hours is 6 am. Pilots must also ensure they have no residual effects and a BAC below 0.02%.

**20) You must not commence your take-off roll when departing behind another aircraft (for less than 2000kg MTOW) unless the preceding aircraft is airborne and at least how far ahead?**

- **Answer:** 600m

- **Why:** To ensure separation from wake turbulence, when departing behind another light aircraft (<7000kg MTOW) that is airborne, the following aircraft must wait until it is at least 600 metres ahead before starting its take-off roll. [Reference: AIP ENR 1.1]

**21) If there is a danger of 2 aircraft colliding head-on:**

- **Answer:** Each shall alter their headings to the right

- **Why:** The standard right-of-way rule for aircraft approaching head-on (or nearly so) is for both aircraft to alter their course to the right, ensuring they pass each other's left (port) side. [Reference: CAR 162]

**22) What is the minimum separation from the cloud when flying above 3000ft AMSL for RPC?**

- **Answer:** 1000ft above or below the cloud and 1500m horizontally from it

- **Why:** These are the standard VFR cloud clearance requirements in Class G airspace above 3000ft AMSL (or 1000ft AGL, whichever is higher) and below 10,000ft AMSL in Australia. [Reference: AIP ENR 1.2]

**23) Prior to refuelling an aircraft, what precautions should be taken?**

- **Answer:** Ensure appropriate earth bonding of aircraft and the refuelling source

- **Why:** Flowing fuel creates static electricity. Properly grounding (earthing) the aircraft and the fuel source prevents static sparks that could ignite flammable fuel vapours, which is a critical fire prevention measure.

**24) On a visual terminal chart, the notation "C LL 2500" means:**

- **Answer:** Controlled airspace starts at 2500ft above sea level

- **Why:** On VTCs, 'C' denotes Class C airspace, 'LL' means Lower Limit, and the altitude (unless specified as AGL) is Above Mean Sea Level (AMSL). So, this indicates the floor of Class C airspace is at 2500 ft AMSL in that depicted area.

**25) Maximum take-off weight refers to the maximum weight of the aircraft, which includes which of the following?**

- **Answer:** The aircraft, fuel, pilot, passenger and any baggage or equipment

- **Why:** MTOW is the maximum certified weight limit for the aircraft at the start of the take-off run. It includes the aircraft's basic empty weight plus the weight of everything loaded onto/into it: crew, passengers, fuel, oil, baggage, cargo, and any installed equipment.

**26) While maintaining a stable final approach, how is a sudden decrease in the headwind component detected?**

- **Answer:** A decrease in indicated airspeed and increase in rate of descent

- **Why:** A sudden loss of headwind means the airflow over the wings decreases, causing a drop in Indicated Airspeed (IAS). Reduced IAS leads to reduced lift, causing the aircraft to sink more rapidly, thus increasing the rate of descent.

**27) When broadcasting altitude, how is 2500ft pronounced?**

- **Answer:** Two thousand Five Hundred

- **Why:** Standard aviation phraseology dictates pronouncing altitudes by stating the thousands figure followed by "THOUSAND" and the hundreds figure followed by "HUNDRED". So, 2500 is "TWO THOUSAND FIVE HUNDRED". Adding "FEET" is usually required as well. [Reference: AIP GEN 3.4]

**28) A distress message is prefaced with the expression:**

- **Answer:** MAYDAY, MAYDAY, MAYDAY

- **Why:** "MAYDAY" repeated three times is the international standard radiotelephony signal indicating grave and imminent danger requiring immediate assistance.

**29) You hear a call from another aircraft and only catch your aircraft call sign but nothing of the rest of the transmissions, you should?**

- **Answer:** Respond with your call sign and request aircraft calling to "say again"

- **Why:** If you hear your callsign but miss the message, the correct procedure is to acknowledge you were called and ask for the message to be repeated using the standard phrase "SAY AGAIN". Example: "[Your Callsign], say again."

**30) An effective practice to ensure you are not tempted to turn back early if you experience an engine failure on take-off below 500ft is?**

- **Answer:** Ensure you complete a pre-takeoff safety brief appropriate to the aircraft and environment, before every flight

- **Why:** Mentally rehearsing the procedure for an engine failure after take-off (EFATO) during the pre-takeoff brief is crucial. This includes identifying suitable landing areas ahead/aside and consciously committing to _not_ attempting the hazardous low-level turn-back (the "impossible turn"). This preparation helps override the instinct to turn back.

**31) With regard to hypoxia, smoking:**

- **Answer:** Both b and c

- **Why:** Smoking introduces carbon monoxide, which binds to haemoglobin, reducing the blood's oxygen-carrying capacity. This makes smokers effectively operate at a higher physiological altitude and thus experience hypoxia onset at a lower actual altitude (b). Symptoms of hypoxia include light-headedness and tingling fingers/toes (c).

**32) The onset of fatigue is a safety threat because:**

- **Answer:** The effect can be insidious and go unnoticed

- **Why:** Fatigue impairs judgment, reaction time, and vigilance. A major danger is that its onset can be gradual, and pilots may not recognise the extent of their impairment until performance is already significantly degraded, leading to errors.

**33) If you have completed multiple dives in a day that do not total more than 4 hours or require decompression, what is the minimum time interval suggested between surfacing from the last dive and flying?**

- **Answer:** 12 hours

- **Why:** Flying after scuba diving can cause decompression sickness due to reduced atmospheric pressure. Standard guidelines (like those from Divers Alert Network - DAN, often referenced by CASA) recommend a minimum surface interval before flying. For multiple no-decompression dives in a day, the _minimum_ recommendation is often 12 hours before flying at typical cabin altitudes (up to 8,000 ft). (Note: More conservative advice/longer waits - 18 or 24 hrs - are often recommended for multiple dives).

**34) Landing towards the setting sun:**

- **Answer:** Can result in sudden total loss of vision

- **Why:** Landing directly into a low sun causes intense glare, which can completely obscure the pilot's vision of the runway, obstacles, and surroundings, creating a very hazardous situation.

**35) Whilst taxiing you observe another aircraft taxiing directly towards you:**

- **Answer:** Both aircraft shall alter their heading to the right ... (best reflects the required action)

- **Why:** Similar to the rule in the air, when two aircraft are approaching head-on while taxiing, both should alter course to their right to pass each other on their respective left (port) sides. Option D correctly states the action "alter their heading to the right," even if the second clause describing the result is slightly awkward. [Reference: CAR 162 applied to manoeuvring area]

**36) What is the reply to a perfectly readable radio check?**

- **Answer:** Reading you five

- **Why:** Radio checks use a 1-to-5 scale for readability: 1 = Unreadable, 5 = Perfectly readable.


**37) What is the correct word used is in a transmission to indicate the letter A?**

- **Answer:** Alpha

- **Why:** "Alpha" (or sometimes spelled Alfa) is the internationally recognized ICAO phonetic alphabet word for the letter 'A'.


**38) When would you use the phrase "Break, break" during a radio call?**

- **Answer:** You intend to transmit to two separate aircraft or ground stations during the same transmission

- **Why:** The word "BREAK" is used to separate messages addressed to different recipients within a single transmission. "BREAK BREAK" adds emphasis to this separation, often used in busy environments when communicating with multiple stations sequentially.


**39) The expression "Entering and backtracking runway [number] is used when:**

- **Answer:** You intend to use that runway for the purpose of taxiing

- **Why:** "Backtracking" means taxiing _on_ the active runway, usually in the direction opposite to landing/take-off, to position for departure or to reach a taxiway exit.


**40) What is the most effective way to scan for other aircraft during level flight?**

- **Answer:** Only shift your focus point approximately 10-20 degrees at a time, pause to permit the peripheral vision to detect movement and repeat

- **Why:** Effective visual scanning involves systematically dividing the viewing area into segments. Focus on each segment for a few seconds (allowing peripheral vision to detect movement) before moving to the next. A continuous sweep or staring straight ahead is ineffective.


**41) What is the angle between the chord line of the aerofoil and the relative airflow called?**

- **Answer:** Angle of attack

- **Why:** This is the definition of Angle of Attack (AoA), which is the primary determinant of the amount of lift and drag produced by the wing at a given airspeed.


**42) One of the causes of detonation in an aircraft engine is:**

- **Answer:** Using a fuel of a lower grade than recommended

- **Why:** Detonation is the uncontrolled explosion of the fuel-air mixture. Lower octane (lower grade) fuel is less resistant to detonation under the high temperatures and pressures found in engine cylinders, especially under high power settings.


**43) When is the earliest time that you can fly in the morning?**

- **Answer:** First light

- **Why:** VFR flight is restricted to daylight hours. Daylight begins at morning civil twilight, which is commonly referred to operationally as "first light." Flying before this time requires night VFR qualifications and equipment. [Reference: CAR 2 Definition of Day]


**44) When overflying an aerodrome with the intention of landing, you notice a single white cross next to the windsock. What does this mean?**

- **Answer:** The aerodrome is closed for all operations

- **Why:** A large white cross displayed horizontally in the signal area (or sometimes near the windsock) is the standard visual signal indicating that the aerodrome, or at least the runway system, is unserviceable and closed to aircraft operations. [Reference: AIP AD 1.1]


**45) Which of the following instrument/s will read incorrectly in a descent if the pitot tube becomes blocked?**

- **Answer:** The airspeed indicator only

- **Why:** The pitot tube supplies ram air pressure only to the Airspeed Indicator (ASI). If it becomes blocked (but the drain hole remains open), the ASI essentially traps pressure and will read inaccurately (acting like an altimeter - showing increasing speed in descent). The altimeter and VSI rely on the static system and are unaffected by a pitot blockage alone.


**46) The person ultimately responsible in ensuring that the recreational aircraft is safe to fly is:**

- **Answer:** The pilot in command

- **Why:** Aviation regulations (e.g., CASR 91.095) universally designate the Pilot in Command (PIC) as having the final authority and responsibility for the safe operation of the aircraft, including the decision on its airworthiness for the intended flight.


**47) If the static system has a blockage, the instruments which will be affected are?**

- **Answer:** Airspeed indicator, altimeter, and vertical speed indicator

- **Why:** The static system provides ambient air pressure reference to the ASI (for comparison with pitot pressure), the Altimeter (which measures static pressure directly), and the VSI (which measures the rate of change of static pressure). A blockage will cause all three to give erroneous readings.


**48) How far from any aircraft from the ground can you smoke a cigarette?**

- **Answer:** 50ft (15m)

- **Why:** Fire safety regulations prohibit smoking near aircraft due to the risk of igniting fuel or vapours. A common minimum distance specified is 15 metres (approximately 50 feet), particularly during refuelling or maintenance.


**49) Is a fight with your partner or boss a potential stress point affecting decision making?**

- **Answer:** Yes

- **Why:** Emotional stress from personal conflicts, work pressure, or other life events significantly impacts cognitive function, judgment, and decision-making abilities. It is a critical factor in the "IMSAFE" pre-flight checklist (S = Stress) and can render a pilot unfit to fly.


**50) What is the angle between the chord line and the aircraft longitudinal axis called?**

- **Answer:** Angle of incidence

- **Why:** The Angle of Incidence is a fixed, designed-in angle between the wing's chord line and the reference line running lengthwise through the fuselage (longitudinal axis). It determines how the wing is mounted on the aircraft.
