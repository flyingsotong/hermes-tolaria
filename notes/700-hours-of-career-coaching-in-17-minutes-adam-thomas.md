I can hardly believe it, but I've spent over 700 hours coaching people in the last three and a half years. I've coached entrepreneurs and helped them found new businesses, philanthropists giving away millions of dollars, and nonprofit leaders solving some of the world's biggest problems.
Many of them are multi-passionate like I am—driven by curiosity but sometimes finding it hard to focus, pick one path, know when to give something up, or start something new. This often leads to burnout and scattered focus, and traditional advice like “grind through it” or “pick a niche” doesn’t help, often making things worse. So, I’m condensing my 700 hours of coaching into five key areas to help you manage your focus, career, life, and energy as a multi-passionate person. This applies to others too, not just those who identify as multi-passionate.
**First, it always comes down to identity—making peace with the kind of person you actually are. Forget about careers, jobs, and professional development for a moment.** The hardest part isn’t the work, it’s feeling misunderstood and having trouble justifying why you do so many things. To help with identity and storytelling, lean into your multi-passionate identity; it’s a superpower. In a world that’s chaotic and changing, those who spot patterns, connect dots, and move across disciplines are the ones who will succeed and solve big problems. Don’t compare your timeline to specialists—they’re on a different trajectory, with linear progression and clear awards or job titles as milestones. Instead, frame your story in terms of interests and impact, not roles or awards. Find a group of multi-passionate peers for support and understanding, rather than only those on linear paths. And keep a “brag file”—a space to collect positive feedback and encouragement for times when you need a boost.
**The second area is designing your career. You want a career that allows you to experiment and stay curious, but also provides stability and the ability to earn.** Use skill stacking as your advantage—don’t worry about being deep in one area, but use your unique intersection of interests, skills, talents, and passions to carve out a niche that’s uniquely yours. The qualities that make you a “curious generalist” become your specialty, and nobody else can occupy that space. Focus on future-proofing meta skills like emotional intelligence, systems thinking, and storytelling, as these transfer across domains. When designing your career portfolio, balance stability and moonshot experiments, just like an investor (some safe bets plus some big swings). Use tools like Ikigai to map out what you’re good at, love, what the world needs, and what you can be paid for—think holistically. If faced with options, pick the one that offers the most optionality, keeps doors open longest, and provides more paths forward.
**Multi-passionate people often struggle to choose the right job because no single role feels like a perfect fit. Not everyone can be an entrepreneur; sometimes you need a job, and that’s fine.** There are archetypes of multi-passionate careers like the group hug, the slash, the Einstein, and the Phoenix models (from Emily Wapnick’s book, "How to Be Everything"), which help you balance satisfaction and stability. Look for bridge roles—jobs where you connect people, information, or money, such as project manager, product manager, design, or even managing a bar. Optimize for the right environment rather than the ideal role: workplaces that celebrate achievements outside work and allow you to show up as a fully rounded person are best. Finally, negotiate for flexibility rather than pay; if you can choose between jobs, prioritize flexibility (like a four-day week or generous parental leave) over cash, as it sustains curiosity and growth long-term.
**The fourth area is managing energy and focus.** With so many interests, multi-passionate people can run out of bandwidth and sometimes feel down on themselves. Set boundaries so that one project doesn’t swallow everything; time-block recovery just like you would meetings, and schedule time to do nothing, rest, or pursue unrelated interests. Think in seasons rather than trying to balance everything every day; focus on one major area for a time, keep others running in the background, then switch as needed. Looking back, you’ll see your career in chunks—seasons of focused work, followed by shifts to new chapters. That’s normal and healthy.
**Finally, curiosity and growth mindset. Curiosity sustains you, but it can also be a trap.** The challenge is knowing when to keep exploring, when to double down, and when to walk away or quit. Curiosity should be your compass; when something excites you, follow it—but time box the effort (e.g., 30 days) before deciding if it’s worth continuing. Notice persistent themes and patterns—they point you toward deeper pursuits. Sometimes discomfort signals the direction you need to go next, even if you resist it. Just because you can do something doesn’t mean you have to; being talented and adaptable can lead to overcommitting and perfectionism. You need to give yourself permission to pursue hobbies purely for enjoyment, without turning everything into a career or side hustle (resist “Etsification” of your life). If it makes you happy and you’re curious, that’s enough.
That’s 700 hours of multi-passionate coaching condensed into 17 minutes—thank you for being here, and more on this topic soon.
