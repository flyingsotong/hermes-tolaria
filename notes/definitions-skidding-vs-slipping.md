# Definitions Skidding vs Slipping

In aviation, skidding is generally considered more dangerous than slipping.
When an aircraft skids, it's moving sideways toward the outside of a turn, with the tail swinging outward. This creates several hazards:

1. It increases the risk of a spin entry, particularly a cross-controlled spin which can be difficult to recover from

2. Skidding turns at low altitudes can lead to a stall/spin accident with insufficient altitude for recovery

3. The pilot has reduced control effectiveness during a skid

A slip, by contrast, is when the aircraft moves sideways toward the inside of a turn. Slips are actually used intentionally as a flight maneuver to:

- Increase descent rate without increasing airspeed

- Counteract crosswinds during landing

- Improve forward visibility during landing approaches

While slips can be dangerous if executed improperly (especially close to the ground), they're generally considered a controlled maneuver when performed correctly. Pilots are trained to use slips safely.

The classic aviation adage "skids kill, slips don't" emphasizes this distinction. Skids tend to develop unexpectedly and can rapidly deteriorate into dangerous flight conditions, while slips are typically a deliberate control input with more predictable aircraft behavior.
