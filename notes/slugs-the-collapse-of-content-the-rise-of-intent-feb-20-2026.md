# Slugs The collapse of content; the rise of intent (Feb 20, 2026)

Last week, I shared how the ground shifted for AI reasoning. But if you want to see the future of journalism, look at what’s happening in software engineering.

In the coding world, a massive shift is underway. The cost of production — the act of writing code — is collapsing toward zero. But as the cost of "doing" falls, the cost of "specifying" is skyrocketing. We are seeing a new class of high-value workers who don't merely “use" AI; they architect and contextualize intent.

For journalism, the lesson is clear:**Content is about to cost nothing, and knowing what to report on — and why — is about to cost you everything.**

**The logic of the "Wrong Thing"**
In a recent analysis of thousands of AI-generated code requests, researchers found that AI produces 1.7 times more logic issues than humans. The code "compiles"—it looks right and runs fast—but it does the *wrong thing correctly.*

We are seeing this in newsrooms already. We can now generate a thousand "correct" articles in an afternoon. They are grammatically perfect, SEO-optimized, and factually plausible. But if the mission is wrong — if the report doesn't serve a specific user need or satisfy a civic responsibility — you haven't saved time. You’ve just accelerated the production of noise.

In software, companies like AWS are now forcing developers to write a "testable specification" *before* any code is generated. They are slowing down to get the intent right. In journalism, we must do the same. The "factory" model of journalism is dead because the machine is a better factory than you are.

**Human judgment as the bottleneck**
If production is free, what is scarce?**Judgment.** We are moving into a bifurcated world of information work:

1.**Low-leverage reporters:** Those using AI as a better autocomplete or a grammar checker. They are doing the same work as before, just faster. They are being commoditized because they are still stuck in the production phase.
2.**High-value mission drivers:** These are the architects. They specify precisely *what* needs to be investigated and *why* it matters. They don't just "write stories.” Instead, they manage agent fleets to analyze data, find patterns, and verify claims at a scale that was previously impossible.

The person who can take a vague community need and translate it into a rigorous reporting "specification" is the new center of gravity in the newsroom.

**From content to strategy**
For years, we’ve been insulated by the friction of production. It was hard to print a paper or build a website, so we felt valuable. We told ourselves we were serving a need. We sold our work by page views. That insulation is gone.

Strategy in media is effectively becoming a**specification**. Mission is no longer a "vibe" or a slogan on a wall. It’s not about stating why we matter (“now more than ever!”). It’s a set of testable claims about how your information changes a user's life. Your mission doesn’t matter if you can’t validate it with a user.

If you can’t specify what success looks like — in terms of impact, not just clicks — you cannot direct the machines. You will simply be buried by them.

Look, the answer isn't to "learn to code." It’s to learn the engineering discipline of**precision.**

***Hit the right level of abstraction:** Don't tell an AI to "write a report on the budget." Tell it to "audit these 500 PDFs for discrepancies in school funding vs. enrollment growth over five years, but connect via MCP to Flutter for visualization, and via API to the goverment’s demographics data.”
***Make your output verifiable:** If you can’t measure whether your reporting solved the problem you set out to solve, you haven't engineered a product; you’ve just released a "vibes pitch."
