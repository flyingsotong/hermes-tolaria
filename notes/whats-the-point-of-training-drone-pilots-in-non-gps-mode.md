Flying in GPS mode is often compared to "pointing a camera in the sky." The pilot tells the drone where to go, and the flight controller handles how to get there, correcting for wind and drift.

Flying in Attitude mode forces the pilot to learn the "feel" of the aircraft. They must manually compensate for wind, manage momentum, and make constant, small corrections to hold a position, just like a traditional helicopter pilot.

**Better Flight Performance (Smoother Video)**

Ironically, this "old-school" skill is highly valued by professional cinematographers.

A drone in GPS mode can sometimes make small, jerky corrections as it fights to hold its exact coordinates. A skilled pilot in Attitude mode can fly much more smoothly and create more fluid, cinematic shots by "feathering" the controls and letting the drone drift naturally.

**Required for Specific Industries**

Many high-value industrial inspections (e.g., inside boilers, tanks, mines, or under bridges) take place in GPS-denied environments.

While specialized indoor drones exist, the ability to handle a drone manually without GPS is a non-negotiable skill for these advanced, high-paying jobs.

 Arguments AGAINST Requiring Non-GPS Mode

The core argument here is that technology has made the skill obsolete, and requiring it creates an unnecessary barrier that increases cost and risk.

**1. Technology Has Made It Obsolete**

Modern flight controllers are incredibly reliable. Failsafes like Return-to-Home (RTH) are so advanced that in the event of signal loss (a more common issue than GPS failure), the drone will fly itself back.

Vision Positioning Systems (VPS) on modern drones are now so effective that even if the GPS fails, the downward-facing cameras can lock the drone in place, effectively mimicking a GPS-hold.

**2. Modern Drones Actively Hide the Mode**

This is a major practical argument. Manufacturers like DJI—which dominate the market—have removed the ability to manually switch into Attitude mode on most of their newer consumer and "prosumer" drones (e.g., Mini, Air, and Mavic series).

Because the mode only activates automatically during a failure, it's very difficult (and dangerous) for pilots to practice. This creates a catch-22: the law requires a skill that the hardware is designed to prevent you from using.

**3. Increases Training Cost and Creates a Barrier to Entry**

The US (FAA Part 107) model is cheap and accessible: pay \~$175, study online, and pass a knowledge test.

Mandating a practical test that includes a difficult skill like non-GPS flight requires pilots to pay for expensive hands-on training ($1,000+), buy a more advanced drone capable of the mode, and spend many hours practicing. This can be seen as an unnecessary financial barrier for people starting a simple aerial photography business.

**4. It's Not Relevant for Most Commercial Work**

The argument is that 99% of commercial drone work (e.g., real estate photography, basic mapping, roof inspections in an open field) is done in GPS mode.

Proponents of this view argue that it's better to focus training on the actual job: airspace rules, mission planning, and understanding automated flight modes, rather than on a niche manual skill that most pilots will never use.
