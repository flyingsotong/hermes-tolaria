**Monday, April 14, 2025**

**What led Yvette to quit**

Yvette's decision to quit stemmed from a confluence of extremely challenging and negative experiences over roughly the last 3–17 months at her previous job:

1. Intense workload and pressure — she worked 10–15 hours daily for 17 months, leading a highly complex project to overhaul the company's entire digital infrastructure with minimal internal support and managing multiple vendors.
2. Lack of recognition and unfair blame — despite successfully completing this "mission impossible" project, her success was ignored. Instead, she was publicly blamed in board meetings for issues like vendor-caused transaction failures and high project costs she wasn't responsible for planning.
3. Humiliation and disrespect — she felt repeatedly humiliated, particularly in board meetings where she was interrupted and dismissed rudely by the Chairman, and told she had "no sense of tech."
4. Toxic leadership and lack of support — difficult interactions with leadership and a lack of support from senior colleagues who knew the truth but remained silent when she was unfairly criticized. This toxic environment also led other key team members to resign.
5. Unfair treatment and deception — she discovered she was paid significantly less than peers despite the company claiming financial difficulties. She also learned the CEO (Jean), who was credited with some of her achievements upon departure, was actually fired for poor performance.
6. Burnout and trauma — the cumulative effect of the stress, long hours, unfair treatment, and humiliation left her feeling burnt out and deeply traumatized, describing the final months as "horrible."

**Awareness Yvette gained from the call**

The primary awareness Yvette seemed to gain from the call was the **validation and confirmation of the severity and unacceptability of her experience**. By articulating the sequence of events and the intense negative emotions — and having you listen actively and acknowledge the difficulty — she likely reinforced her own understanding that:

- Her feelings were justified
- The situation was objectively toxic and unsustainable
- Leaving was a necessary act of self-preservation
- She needed time to recover and focus on herself

The call provided a safe space for her to process the traumatic events and feel heard, solidifying her decision to quit as the right one for her well-being.

**Potential missed coaching opportunities**

While you provided crucial support and validation (aligning well with Competency 4: Cultivates Trust and Safety and Competency 5: Maintains Presence), there were areas where ICF competencies might suggest different approaches:

1. Competency 3: Establishes and Maintains Agreements — while Yvette clearly needed to share her story, was a specific coaching outcome for this session agreed upon beyond venting? Partnering with her briefly at the start to ask what she hoped to gain from sharing could have focused the session further.
2. Competency 6: Listens Actively — to deepen the listening, you could have occasionally summarized longer stretches or reflected back the underlying meaning or patterns you heard. For example: "It sounds like a pattern of being overloaded, delivering, and then being unfairly blamed repeated itself?"
3. Competency 7: Evokes Awareness — the focus was heavily on validating her experience. Opportunities might have existed to ask more powerful questions. Examples: "Having been through this, what have you learned about what's most important to you in a work environment?" "What strengths did you discover in yourself during this incredibly difficult time?"
4. Competency 8: Facilitates Client Growth — you rightly acknowledged her need for rest and self-care. To facilitate growth, you could have partnered slightly more actively on what that means for her. Example: "You mentioned needing support over the next few months. What might that support look like ideally?"
