An apolitical beagle will lead the revolt of creators against mainstream media

Every time a journalist goes independent and thrives, they create a blueprint for others to follow. CEE publishers and media funders can't ignore these structural shifts anymore.

Peter Erdelyi

Oct 24, 2024

We need to talk about Snoopy.

He may seem affectionate and quirky on the surface, but did you know he recently endorsed Donald Trump for President? Okay, it wasn’t Snoopy himself, but rather whoever runs the SnoopyWeekly fan account on Twitter. Cute pictures of the Peanuts gang for years, and then bang, Donald Trump should run the most powerful country in the world. Who could have known?

I found out because I follow Taylor Lorenz on Substack. Until recently, Lorenz was the social media and digital culture reporter for The Washington Post. She left her prestigious job to launch her own publication on the platform and the Snoopy affiliated culture war was her very first story. The person known for her exceptional understanding of creators is now leaving a legacy publication to become a creator herself.

I promise to explore Charlie Brown’s views on the EU’s new competitiveness initiative, reproductive rights and the complex geopolitical realities of the Middle-East in a later edition, but today I want to focus on influencers/creators/new media entrepreneurs, their relationship with mainstream news publishers, and what this means for the future journalism.

**The two-wave digital disruption**

I started my career in what feels like 1472 (the early 2000s) at a prestigious print daily in Hungary. I still remember my first major investigation, when a source justified giving me a scoop by dismissing another reporter who was also pursuing the story, saying, “But that person writes on the internet.” That was probably the last time a source ever said such a thing.

As the first wave of digital disruption hit the demand side hardest the prestige of print diluted quickly. The way people consumed news shifted dramatically, affecting the supply side too, but large institutions still dominated the market. It became easier for individuals to reach vast audiences, but monetizing that attention through ads or reader revenue remained difficult.

Youtube’s partner program launched in 2007, but it didn’t take off immediately. In 2009 Entertainment Weekly put the platform on its Best of the decade list, explaining YouTube is “providing a safe home for piano-playing cats, celeb goof-ups, and overzealous lip-synchers”. I think the real inflection point was probably in the mid 2010s when multiple platforms (Patreon, Twitch, etc.) made creator monetization mainstream.

But up until \~5 years ago, this revolution was still largely fuelled by advertising and because the unit of revenue per user is very small, creators needed massive audiences to achieve financial sustainability. This made entertainment the dominant category, and if you created content in niche CEE languages (Jó napot kívánok!), your chances of breaking through were slim. Many who left mainstream journalism for new platforms did so seeking independence—not necessarily because they could earn a better living.

But recent technological advances are shifting the status quo yet again. Payment processing and gated content are now practically effortless. Stripe has become a trusted, global name, and anyone can use it with no technical expertise. People are also more comfortable paying directly for all kinds of digital services, which means creators no longer have to rely solely on advertising and have a massive reach. Instead, producing relevant content for a smaller, more affluent audience can also be financially viable.

And here’s the real threat to traditional news publishers: their top reporters—those who’ve built personal brands and followings—now have a reasonable alternative.

**The crisis of institutions**

Before diving into intra-newsroom dynamics (my favorite kind), we need to consider a larger structural change. In 2014, former CIA media analyst Martin Gurri published The Revolt of the Public and the Crisis of Authority in the New Millennium, a book that accurately predicted many of the shifts that followed in the U.S. and beyond. Republished in 2018 by Stripe (somewhat symbolically), the book explains the erosion of institutional authority in the digital age.

Gurri’s theory revolves around the idea that traditional institutions and elites once derived their legitimacy from controlling information. In the pre-digital era, a small group of gatekeepers—media organizations, governments, and academic institutions—held a near-monopoly on information dissemination. This control allowed them to shape public narratives and maintain authority over the discourse.

The digital revolution, however, upended this information hierarchy. The rise of the internet and social media broke down barriers to publishing and distributing information. Gurri argues that this explosion of information has led to widespread skepticism toward traditional institutions. The public, now equipped with alternative viewpoints and easier access to information, has increasingly questioned elite narratives. This democratization of information has eroded the authority once held by traditional gatekeepers, leading to what Gurri calls a “crisis of authority.”

This technological shift did more than introduce new tools—it reshaped the entire information ecosystem, creating an environment ripe for a new type of trusted figure: the creator or influencer. As trust in mainstream institutions declines, many people now turn to individuals who are seen as more transparent, authentic, and relatable. Influencers and creators offer a direct connection to their audiences, contrasting with the sometimes opaque and impersonal nature of large institutions. This personal link fosters trust, even when these creators lack formal credentials or journalistic training.

In essence, influencers and creators are filling the vacuum left by the decline in institutional authority. They’re not seizing power maliciously but are capitalizing on the breakdown of information monopolies. By offering content that feels tailored and responsive to their audience’s concerns, they’ve redefined how trust and legitimacy are constructed in the digital age. Trust, once tied to institutional prestige, is now often rooted in the perceived authenticity and relatability of an individual.

Ioana’s August interview with Joy Mayer also touches on some of these topics

“People trust people, so the instinct that journalists have not to share their own personality or humanity or to hide behind their byline actually gets in their way, because humanity and authenticity make your message more credible.”

This shift in trust is evident even within long-established institutions. Take The Economist, a 180-year-old bastion of mainstream journalism. For most of its history, the magazine published articles without individual bylines, aiming to build trust in the institution rather than in individual writers. But as the outlet successfully expanded into podcasts, generating substantial audio revenues, it began leveraging the personal brands of its hosts.

Even the largest and most established news organizations are increasingly relying on the individual appeal of their star reporters and hosts—think Ezra Klein, Sarah Koenig, Ben Smith, and Marina Hyde. The emphasis on individual credibility, even within traditional institutions, underscores a significant shift in how audiences engage with information and where they place their trust.

**The independence paradox**

Being a solo(ish) creator can seem incredibly liberating—and often, it is. But this freedom comes with trade-offs that aren’t talked about enough.

Every time I write this newsletter, I rely heavily on the work of institutions. My insights are built on research and original reporting from organizations like Nieman Lab, Press Gazette, AdWeek, RISJ, INMA, and countless others with the resources to “create” new information. I can analyze, synthesize, and perhaps offer a fresh perspective, but ultimately, I’m a curator and interpreter of information that larger institutions have generated.

Much of the creator economy still depends on such institutions. Figures like Matthew Yglesias, Nate Silver, Noah Smith, Adam Tooze, and even Taylor Lorenz owe part of their success to building analysis atop information produced elsewhere. Their rise isn’t undeserved, but it’s crucial to recognize that successful creators don’t operate in a vacuum; they’re usually part of an ecosystem.

This ecosystem is evolving though. The technical and financial barriers that once made independence impractical are falling away, and many journalists are finding they can strike out on their own. The creator economy offers both new monetization options and the promise of something perhaps even more valuable: true independence.

There's something incredibly appealing about being your own boss, setting your own agenda, and not having to convince three layers of editors that your story is worth pursuing or the particular take/perspective you are putting forward is the right one.

But I think this independence is a bit of a double edged sword.

Yes, you’re free from institutional constraints, internal politics, or pressure from external stakeholders. However, you also lose the safety net that good institutions provide.

Some of my best work came from editors pushing back on my ideas, poking holes in my arguments, and forcing me to think deeper. When I'm wrong (and if you follow this newsletter, you’ll know it happens often enough), I’d need someone to catch it before it goes public. Institutions, when they work well, aren't just bureaucratic obstacles – they're quality control systems built from decades of experience.

And they can protect you from more than just your own mistakes. When investigating powerful people or organizations, having an institution’s legal team and financial resources behind you isn’t just comforting—it’s essential. Much of the hardest-hitting investigative journalism still comes from institutions, which have the resources to support these stories and provide safety nets for reporters undertaking difficult, sometimes risky work. Good watchdog journalism is inherently collaborative.

There’s also a certain naivety among some who leave established institutions. Yes, arguing with editors over seemingly trivial things can be disempowering. But have you ever tried arguing with a tech company that’s demonetized or deplatformed you without explanation or decided to change their algorithm in favor of some other topic or format?

In the end, you may simply be trading one type of dependency for another. No one in the information space is ever completely independent.

Even if you trust tech companies to have your best interests at heart (in which case, call our helpline immediately), being without an institution means becoming more dependent on your audience. And while putting the audience first is generally the right approach for most reporters and editors, it can create some problematic dynamics.

If you build a large enough audience, cultivate direct relationships, and rely on their support, you might find it increasingly difficult to produce content that challenges them or presents perspectives they disagree with. Not everyone does this, but a lot of people who pay for journalism do so, at least partly, to have their views affirmed. A large institution can afford to occasionally upset its audience (though it doesn’t happen as often as it should). Independent creators, on the other hand, usually find their success hinges on understanding and catering to their audience’s preferences. This dynamic can subtly pressure creators to prioritize validation over challenging discourse. (On this topic, read the excellent paper by my dear colleague Guillermo Draper: Snow White Mirror Syndrome: protecting editorial values in a reader revenue model )

**Aging into media**

While there’s a long line of people eager to dance on the grave of legacy media, they probably shouldn’t hold their breath just yet. Many of them forget that how we consume and process information fundamentally shifts as we move through life.

I teach at a large liberal arts university in Budapest, and each year, when I mention newsletters to my students, I’m met with the “Mmmokay Boomer” look that makes teaching so very rewarding. But when I turn to media professionals in their mid 30s and older, newsletters are suddenly all the rage. Our survey data from CEE countries repeatedly shows this pattern: people quite literally age into certain media formats.

Media consumption isn’t just driven by technology or preferences; it’s also shaped by life stages. At 20, in-depth analysis of mortgage rates or pension reform might not seem relevant. Fast forward a decade, add a mortgage, a couple of kids, and that dry economic policy piece suddenly feels crucial. As people begin to put down roots—securing their first serious job, taking on a mortgage, or starting to care for others—their information needs shift dramatically. Tax policy becomes essential, healthcare becomes fascinating (in a terrifying way), and local government decisions matter in ways they never did before.

This doesn’t mean traditional media can be complacent. It still needs to evolve and find ways to stay relevant. But it’s essential to avoid drawing sweeping conclusions based solely on the media habits of younger audiences. Information needs evolve with life stages, and media organizations should be mindful of this reality when planning for the future.

**The talent exodus is coming already here**

For a while, media executives in Central and Eastern Europe held onto a somewhat hubristic belief: sure, journalists in the U.S. might be leaving legacy publications for creator platforms, but that won't happen here. Our markets are different. Our journalists are different. Our audience is different.

But we all know how this story ends. Look around, and you'll see journalists quietly building their own platforms and cultivating personal audiences. Dneven Red in Bulgaria, Jo Ilie, Bianca Oanea, Cristi Lupșa and a long list of other people in Romania, Szabolcs Dull, Alinda Veiszer, Szabolcs Tohotom Toth and others in Hungary, literally everyone in podcasting in Poland.

These aren’t just any journalists—they’re the ones publishers can’t afford to lose. They’ve built expertise, credibility, and, most importantly, audience trust. They’ve managed to develop personal brands while operating within institutional constraints, making them precisely the kind of talent most likely to succeed independently.

CEE publishers should tread carefully, because the window for action may be closing faster than expected. As creator platforms gain traction and we hear more success stories (and we will), the trickle of departing talent could quickly become a flood. Every time a journalist goes independent and thrives, they create a blueprint for others to follow.

The challenge isn't just about retention either – it's about transformation. Publishers need to ask themselves some hard questions:

How can we create environments where talented journalists can build their brands without feeling constrained?

What value can we offer that platforms can't?

How do we balance institutional authority with individual authenticity?

What new business models might support this transformation?

And perhaps most importantly: how do we do all this while maintaining the collaborative, resource-intensive journalism that we discussed earlier?

I wish I had definitive answers for you, but I don't. The best I can suggest is that you start thinking about this, and maybe study Vox Media in the US, who are evolving into something that looks like a mix between a talent agency and a traditional newsroom. They're helping their journalists develop distinct intellectual properties, supporting personal brand building, and creating revenue-sharing models that make staying more attractive than leaving.

**How do you fund an influencer?**

The audience of this newsletter is fairly evenly split between people working at news publishers and those in media funding and development organizations. Over the past few months, I’ve had several conversations with some of you on the funding side, all circling around the same challenge: how do you support quality, public-interest journalism in a world where trust increasingly flows to individuals rather than institutions?

The first problem is practical: how do you invest in individuals? Traditional grant-making frameworks aren’t really designed for someone building their journalism career on platforms like Substack or YouTube. There’s more risk involved, less institutional accountability, and an informality that can make traditional funders uneasy. (Of course, it’s that very informality that often makes these creators effective and trustworthy in the eyes of their audiences.) Even if you navigate these technical hurdles, a deeper challenge remains: how do you promote journalistic standards and values in a space that tend to take pride in breaking away from traditional journalism?

The stakes here are actually pretty high. As we discussed earlier, some people trust these individual voices, more than they trust traditional institutions. If creators optimize purely for engagement and monetization (and let's be real, the platforms certainly encourage this), we risk losing something important about what makes public interest journalism valuable to society.

Here’s the thing: traditional media institutions might sometimes be slow, bureaucratic, and occasionally exasperating, but they’ve also built robust systems to ensure accuracy, fairness, and public service. The challenge isn’t to force creators into these old models—that ship has sailed—but to find ways to foster these values within the frameworks of the creator economy.

Some experiments are underway, but there are no clear solutions yet and development organizations will need to grapple with this issue in the coming years as the media landscape continues to evolve.

A healthy information ecosystem is adaptable

In a world where previously apolitical beagle can suddenly become a commentator on immigration policy, the lines between personal expression, public discourse, and institutional credibility are increasingly fluid.

What we’re witnessing isn’t the death of institutional journalism or the complete triumph of the creator economy. Instead, it’s a painful, awkward, but necessary evolution in how we produce, distribute, and consume information.

For those of us in Central and Eastern Europe, this evolution comes with its own unique challenges and opportunities. Our markets are smaller, our languages more limited in reach, but the fundamental shifts in trust, authority, and audience relationships are just as relevant here as anywhere else—perhaps even more so, given our region’s, let’s say, complex relationship with institutional authority.

Good institutions are not defined by rigid adherence to tradition but by their ability to adapt while maintaining core values. The real challenge for everyone in the information space—publishers, creators, funders, and yes, newsletter writers—is to figure out how to protect what makes public-interest journalism valuable while embracing structural change. The goal is not to resist this evolution but to help guide it in ways that preserve accuracy, fairness, and public trust.
