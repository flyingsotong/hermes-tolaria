**Coaching Journal: Reflections on the Bilal Lakhani engagement**

This is a profound moment for your own practice. When a coach feels "defeated," it usually means they have transitioned from observing the client's journey to *experiencing* it. That is a vulnerable place to be, but it is where the most significant professional growth happens.

Here is a synthesis of the lessons you have learned through your work with Bilal, structured for your personal journal.

**1. The "expert" trap with intellectual clients**

Intellectual clients like Bilal are highly skilled at inviting the coach to be an "expert." When Bilal asked for industry spells and transformation maps, he was unconsciously creating a smokescreen. By providing brilliant strategy, I inadvertently helped him move the spotlight away from his internal pain.

- **The lesson:** A client's request for "data" or "strategy" is often a defense mechanism. In the future, I must ask: *"Is this question serving your business, or is it helping us avoid a harder conversation about how you feel?"*

**2. The mirror effect (parallel process)**

My feeling of "defeat" and "failure" after Session 3 was not an objective measure of my coaching; it was a reflection of Bilal's internal state. He felt defeated by his father, the military, and the market. He felt like a failure because his "Mars" mission didn't launch.

- **The lesson:** If I suddenly feel "unsuccessful" or "uninteresting" as a coach, it is likely that I am carrying the client's baggage. I need to recognize the **Parallel Process**: my feelings are data about the client's world, not an indictment of my skills.

**3. Mourning as a necessary strategic phase**

I initially viewed Bilal's "retreat" and loss of interest as a coaching failure. I now realize it was a *mourning process*. Bilal had to let his "Journalist-in-Chief" fantasy die before he could become a realistic steward.

- **The lesson:** Transformation isn't always a "climb" toward a new goal; sometimes it is a "descent" into reality. A client's loss of interest in an old goal isn't a lack of progress — it is often the most honest progress they've made. My job is to sit with them in the quiet of that loss without trying to "re-spark" them prematurely.

**4. Stewardship: The "third way"**

This engagement taught me that not every leader is meant to be a "Disruptor" or a "Visionary." For some, the most courageous act is **Stewardship**. Bilal's path isn't to beat his father or "save" journalism; it's to protect the brand, his team, and his family's legacy in a restricted environment.

- **The lesson:** When the "Hero" narrative fails, help the client find the **Steward** narrative. It is quieter, less "exciting," but often far more sustainable and noble.

**5. Validating the "between the lines"**

The most successful interventions in this engagement happened when I stopped talking about media models and started talking about "oxygen," "the romance of journalism," and "stewardship."

- **The lesson:** Intellectual clients have a high "Head-to-Heart" ratio. My value isn't in competing with their "Head" (their business knowledge), but in connecting with their "Heart" (the feelings they try to intellectualize away).

**Final reflection: The value of "failed" sessions**

I learned that a session that feels "bad" is often the one where the most ego-dissolution is happening. Bilal's "retreat" felt like a defeat to me because I was still tied to his initial goal of "transformation." Once I let go of that goal, I could see that he was finding a path to peace.

**The ultimate lesson:** My success as a coach is not measured by the client reaching the goal they *started* with, but by the client reaching the clarity they actually *need*.
