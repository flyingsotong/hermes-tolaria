**Alternative media careers**

Ignore the skeptics. Here's the good news: you're witnessing the golden age of media.

- Increased opportunity to reach new audiences and target them with specific content

- Everything you need to get started is on your phone

- Barrier to entry is zero

The bad news is that journalism isn't a part of it.

There are two kinds of people in this audience — those who got into journalism as a destination in itself, and those of you who believe that journalism is just a method or process that you apply to any work you do.

For the first segment. If you believe you're in journalism so you can be a journalist, these are some key trends in the past five years alone — stuff that's bigger than you.

- Newsrooms aren't hiring as much as they used to — that means fewer jobs for journalists

- Large newsrooms still struggle with revenue — that means you don't get as many opportunities to learn new skills and grow

- Newsrooms are getting flatter and leaner — that means your chances of promotion are slimmer

- Newsrooms are leaning more on freelancers and contractors to reduce costs

- AI won't steal your jobs — but someone who knows how to use AI will

- Defining journalism is getting harder — that means your employability outside of journalism is tough

What an alternative looks like:

- No one owes you a career. Own the responsibility of learning and growing — seek out opportunities to develop new skills on your own. There's a wealth of content on YouTube on every important skill you need to thrive in the next 10 years. Seek out coaches and mentors.

- Obsess over one thing they don't teach you in journalism: Serving a need. Solve someone's problem. Be useful.

- You're now able to serve an audience of one at scale thanks to AI.

- The barrier to entry to start something today is zero.


**"Let's Get To Work" — closing keynote**

This was supposed to be an uplifting closing keynote. It won't feel uplifting at first, but I promise you there's a point to my provocation.

The biggest mistake that many of you will make is in believing that you're in the content business.

I'm a journalist.

So I write stories.

My stories matter.

So you should be buying them.

This is a receipe for failure.

The biggest mistake you can make is in believing that people are going to buy your content.

I'm here to help you find a different way.

**You see, people don't buy products. They don't buy news. They don't buy journalism.**

They bought the newspaper for the classifieds. Or the movie times. Or last night's stock market numbers.

Or they bought CNN cable bundle because it came with sports.

They buy:

Outcomes Painkillers Status Transformations Change Hope Money

They are buying solutions to get from point A to point B.

Sell the problems you solve, not the products you have.

We have a sales problem.

Your value is directly related to the problems you solve

Not measured in page views, uniques.

So what is it you're selling? What is it that you do for your users?

You're in the get-shit-done-for-people business. But to win, we need to be in the hospitality business.

Why is it that this industry doesn't have a customer success team? Whose job is it to help your reader succeed?

What does a JD for a customer success manager look like?

Here's where the optimism comes in. We're all living in a moment where we're able to serve people — an audience of one, and at scale.

The Gutenberg Age is over. The biggest mistake that media companies make is to assume that the internet audience is a mass one. The internet is about niche audiences.

The future of media is demand, personalized, and interest driven. To be valuable, you service needs to address all three of those.

Everything you do needs to stand out -- it needs to compete for the currency of the internet: attention.

We are at peak content. The world doesn't need yet another piece of content. It does however need content that addresses daily jobs-to-be-done. That content needs to be specific and targeted.

Your competitor isn't the other media company -- it's every app on your phone: shopping, crosswords, sports, chats, banking -- services that help you get things done.

Like any viable business, you need to solve a specific problem for someone. If you're not useful, you're not creating value.

To be useful, you need to understand what people want. Ask them. Listen to them. Act on the feedback. Repeat. This is the mindset that drives a successful media business.

If you're not creating value for your user, you're not going to be financially viable. You're not in the media business. You’re in the business of helping people solve problems and make informed decisions every day.

Pursue problems that people want solved. Not trends.
